## Why Arms-Up Actually Makes Sense

The conventional swaddling wisdom — arms pinned at sides, tight wrap — is built around one goal: suppressing the Moro (startle) reflex that jolts babies awake. That's valid. But it assumes babies are neutral about arm position, which many are not.

Watch an ultrasound or a sleeping newborn in an NICU: a significant portion of babies naturally keep their arms raised, hands near their face. That's how they spent the last trimester in the womb. For these babies, forcing arms down doesn't feel calming — it feels like a constraint they need to escape. The Swaddle UP Original works with that instinct rather than against it. The snug, stretchy cotton jersey wings hold the arms in a raised position (hands near face level) while still providing gentle compression around the body. The Moro reflex is still somewhat dampened by the containment of the suit itself, but the arms can flex naturally within the material.

This is the core insight of the Love To Dream Swaddle UP, and it's genuinely different from everything else on the market. The HALO SleepSack Swaddle, for comparison, offers a three-way adjustment (arms in, arms out, one arm free) but defaults to arms-down and can't accommodate the arms-up position at all. If your baby fights every arms-down configuration, the HALO won't solve the problem.

## The Cotton Jersey Fabric

The Swaddle UP Original is made from cotton jersey — not bamboo, not muslin, not plush. Cotton jersey is the same stretchy knit used in most quality T-shirts. It has meaningful give in four directions, which is exactly what this product needs: the arms-up wings have to stretch around moving limbs without creating pressure points or bunching near the face.

The fabric quality is solid for a $35 product. It doesn't pill significantly after machine washing (we've put ours through 50+ wash cycles), and it stays soft without fabric softener. It's not as luxuriously soft as the bamboo blends you'll find in the Kyte Baby Sleep Bag, and it doesn't have the same silky hand-feel. But it holds up better over time because bamboo-rayon blends can weaken with repeated washing.

One thing to understand about the cotton jersey construction: it's thicker and less breathable than a woven cotton or muslin. That matters for TOG selection, which we'll cover below.

## TOG Ratings Explained — and Why the Choice Matters

The Love To Dream Swaddle UP Original comes in three TOG ratings: 0.2, 1.0, and 2.5. TOG (Thermal Overall Grade) is the textile industry's standard measurement of thermal resistance. A higher number means more insulation.

This matters for safe sleep. The AAP's safe sleep guidelines emphasize that babies should sleep on their back, on a firm flat mattress with a fitted sheet, and with no loose bedding — no blankets, no pillows, no positioners. A wearable blanket like the Swaddle UP becomes the only thermal layer besides the baby's clothing underneath. Getting the TOG wrong means your baby is either too cold (and wakes frequently because of it) or overheated (which is independently associated with increased SIDS risk).

Here's how to match TOG to your room:

- **0.2 TOG**: Rooms consistently above 75°F. Suitable over a short-sleeve onesie. This is the right choice if you're in a warm climate without strong air conditioning, or in summer months.
- **1.0 TOG**: The sweet spot for most U.S. households in the 68–72°F range year-round. Pair with a long-sleeve onesie or a short-sleeve onesie depending on the exact room temperature.
- **2.5 TOG**: Cool rooms below 65°F. Northern winters, drafty homes, or if you keep the thermostat low. Layer with footed pajamas underneath.

The most common mistake we see: buying the 1.0 TOG for a climate-controlled room that runs warm, then wondering why the baby seems uncomfortable or sweaty. If your nursery stays above 72°F, go with the 0.2 even if it looks thin. The fabric itself plus a layer of clothing is often enough.

## Sizing: The Tricky Part

The Swaddle UP Original runs in XS through L, with weight ranges on the packaging. The sizing works correctly when you follow weight, not age. The trap is that two babies at the same weight can have very different body proportions — a long, lean baby may need a larger size for length even if they haven't hit the weight cutoff.

The fit across the shoulders matters most. The wings need to sit snugly enough that there's no loose fabric near the face, but not so tight that the arms can't raise naturally. If the fit looks baggy across the chest and shoulder area, size down. If the arms seem constricted and the baby is working hard to raise them, size up.

One sizing challenge specific to this product: the swaddle is designed to grow a little (the jersey stretches), but it can't grow much. If your baby is right at the upper weight limit of a size, buy the next size up. The overlap region is not forgiving — you'll have maybe two weeks of usable time before it feels too small, and returning or exchanging is a hassle.

## The Zip-Off Sleeve Transition

This is arguably the best feature of the Swaddle UP line, and something neither the HALO SleepSack Swaddle nor the Nested Bean Zen Sack offers. When your baby starts showing rolling signs — typically around 3 to 4 months — it's time to stop swaddling. The AAP is explicit: once a baby can roll, swaddling becomes a safety risk because they may roll onto their stomach and lack the arm freedom to push up.

Cold-turkey transitions from swaddle to sleep sack typically cause 3–7 nights of disrupted sleep. The baby has become conditioned to the containment, and the sudden absence is jarring. The Swaddle UP Stage 2 (a separate product) has zip-off sleeves that let you remove one arm for a week, then both arms. This staged approach gives the baby time to adapt while still feeling some familiar containment.

The practical process: Week 1, zip off the left sleeve only. Week 2, zip off the right sleeve as well. By the time both are off, the baby is essentially in a standard sleep sack and has been acclimating for two weeks. Most parents who use this method report dramatically smoother transitions than they experienced going cold-turkey with other swaddles.

Note: the zip-off sleeve feature is on the Swaddle UP Stage 2 Transition Suit, which costs slightly more than the base $35 Original. If you're buying for a newborn knowing you'll need to transition at 3–4 months, buying the Stage 2 from the start makes more financial sense than buying the Original and then replacing it.

## Safe Sleep Compliance

The Love To Dream Swaddle UP is designed to meet AAP safe sleep standards. The snug fit is intentional: unlike a traditional sleep sack that has extra fabric around the body, the Swaddle UP fits like a second skin. This eliminates the risk of loose fabric migrating toward the face during sleep, which is a concern with oversized sleep sacks.

Key safe sleep reminders when using this product:
- Always place baby on their back for every sleep, every time.
- Use a firm, flat mattress with a fitted sheet — no pillow-top inserts, no mattress pads with extra cushioning.
- No loose bedding, bumpers, positioners, or stuffed animals in the crib.
- Stop using the swaddle at the first signs of rolling — usually around 3–4 months, but some babies roll earlier. This is non-negotiable.
- The hip-healthy design of the Swaddle UP allows the legs to move freely within the suit, which is important for healthy hip development. The International Hip Dysplasia Institute recommends swaddles that leave room for leg movement, and the Swaddle UP's open lower section accommodates this.

## What It Doesn't Do Well

The cons listed in the frontmatter are real, and worth explaining in practical terms.

**Not suitable for arms-down babies.** Some babies — particularly those who seemed comfortable with the fetal position more toward arms-down — actively dislike having their arms raised and held in place. If you try the Swaddle UP and your baby seems more agitated rather than less, they may simply prefer the arms-down configuration of something like the HALO. There's no way to know which camp your baby falls into without trying. Buy from a retailer with a good return policy on the first attempt.

**More expensive than basic swaddles.** At $35, the Swaddle UP Original costs more than a Velcro swaddle blanket or a basic muslin wrap. That's a meaningful price difference when you factor in that you need multiple sizes as the baby grows, and possibly different TOG ratings for different seasons. A full swaddle setup with the Swaddle UP could cost $80–100+ over the first four months.

**Sizing between weights.** The weight ranges on each size overlap slightly, and the jersey stretch means the same suit can fit very differently on babies of the same listed weight but different builds. If you're between sizes, you'll need to make a judgment call — and the wrong call means the product doesn't work properly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What makes the arms-up design better?**
Many babies naturally raise their arms while sleeping — it mimics their position in the womb. Arms-down swaddles fight this instinct; the Swaddle UP works with it. The result is a baby who doesn't spend energy fighting the swaddle, which translates to faster settling and longer sleep stretches.

**Which TOG rating should I choose?**
0.2 TOG for warm rooms (75°F+), 1.0 TOG for standard rooms (68–72°F), 2.5 TOG for cool rooms below 65°F. When in doubt, err on the cooler TOG — a baby who is slightly cool will signal it by waking; a baby who is too warm is less able to regulate.

**How does the transition to a sleep sack work?**
With the Swaddle UP Stage 2: zip off one sleeve for a week, then both. The baby adjusts gradually rather than going cold-turkey, which dramatically reduces sleep disruption and the length of the regression period.
