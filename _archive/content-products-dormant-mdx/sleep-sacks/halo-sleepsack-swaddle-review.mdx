## What the HALO SleepSack Swaddle Actually Does

The fundamental job of a newborn swaddle is to suppress the Moro reflex — the involuntary startle response that causes babies to fling their arms outward and wake themselves from sleep. Newborns experience this reflex constantly, especially in light sleep phases. A properly fitted swaddle contains the arms enough that the movement doesn't complete fully, the baby doesn't fully startle awake, and sleep continues.

The HALO does this through a two-panel velcro wing system. Two fabric wings wrap around the baby — one over the arms from the left, one from the right — and secure with velcro across the chest. The tightness is adjustable by how far you overlap the wings before securing. That adjustability is one of the product's main strengths.

What makes the HALO different from a standard swaddle blanket is that the wings attach to a fixed wearable sack, not a loose wrap. Loose wrapping can unravel during sleep, creating a fabric hazard. The HALO's wings can only come loose at the velcro point, and the sack underneath stays on regardless.

## The 3-Way Swaddle System

The three swaddle configurations are a genuine differentiator. Most swaddle products — including the basic Aden + Anais muslin wraps and the Burt's Bees Beekeeper — don't offer any arm configuration options. The HALO lets you choose:

**Arms-in (standard)**: Both wings wrap over the arms, containing them at the sides. This is the most effective Moro reflex suppression and the right choice for newborns.

**One arm out**: One wing wraps the arm, the other leaves one arm free. This is useful during the transition period — typically 2–3 months — when you're starting to wean off the swaddle.

**Arms-out (sack only)**: Both wings wrap below the arms, creating a standard sleep sack without swaddle function. This lets you continue using the HALO after the swaddle phase is finished, extending the product's life.

The one-arm-out configuration is the key transition tool. The Love To Dream Swaddle UP Stage 2's zip-off sleeves accomplish something similar, but the HALO builds the transition capability into the base product without requiring a separate purchase or a different product line. That's a meaningful value advantage at the $26 price point.

## The Inverted Zipper: A Detail That Actually Matters at 3 AM

The HALO SleepSack Swaddle zips from the bottom up, not the top down. This is intentional, and it's more useful than it sounds. When a baby needs a diaper change at 3 AM, a top-down zipper requires unzipping the entire front, disturbing the swaddle wings and potentially waking the baby fully in the process. With the HALO's inverted zipper, you can open just the bottom portion, access the diaper, and close it again without touching the swaddle wings at all.

If you've ever changed a diaper on an already-swaddled baby at 2 AM, fumbling with a blanket wrap in the dark while the baby squirms, you understand why this design detail matters. It's one of those things that sounds minor until you're using it five times a night.

The zipper itself is a medium-weight coil zipper with a fabric flap that covers the pull end. This prevents the zipper pull from scratching the baby's skin. Over multiple wash cycles, the zipper stays smooth — we haven't seen significant degradation in zipper function even after months of regular use.

## Cotton Fabric and the 0.5 TOG Rating

The HALO SleepSack Swaddle is made from 100% cotton. It carries a 0.5 TOG rating, which is notably lower than the 1.0 TOG mid-range commonly found in other sleep sacks. This matters for safe sleep.

TOG (Thermal Overall Grade) measures thermal insulation. A 0.5 TOG rating is appropriate for room temperatures between 68–72°F when paired with a single layer of clothing underneath — a onesie or a footed sleeper. It's intentionally on the lighter side because newborns are particularly vulnerable to overheating, and overheating is an established risk factor associated with SIDS.

The AAP's safe sleep guidelines emphasize that babies should sleep in an environment where they are comfortable but not too warm. A 0.5 TOG product gives you room to add a clothing layer if needed, rather than starting warm and having nowhere to go. For rooms that run cooler — below 65°F — you'd layer with a long-sleeve onesie and footed pajamas underneath.

The cotton fabric breathes well. It doesn't trap heat the way polyester blends can, and it absorbs moisture without feeling clammy. After washing, it gets softer rather than stiffer. It's not as immediately soft as bamboo-rayon blends or as stretchy as the cotton jersey used in the Love To Dream Swaddle UP, but for a product designed to be used for only the first 2–4 months of life, the durability is more than adequate.

## Hip-Healthy Design and IHDI Certification

The HALO SleepSack Swaddle carries certification from the International Hip Dysplasia Institute (IHDI), which is worth understanding. Hip dysplasia — where the hip joint doesn't form correctly — can be caused or worsened by swaddling that holds the legs straight and together. Traditional tight-wrapping techniques that extend the legs are a known risk factor.

The HALO addresses this by ensuring the lower section of the sack is loose and unrestricted. The swaddle wings contain only the arms and torso; the legs can move freely, flex at the hip, and naturally adopt the frog-leg position that supports healthy acetabular development. If you're choosing between swaddle products and one has IHDI certification and one doesn't, the certified option is the safer choice — particularly if there's any family history of hip dysplasia.

The only swaddle product we commonly recommend alongside the HALO that also maintains a hip-healthy design is the Love To Dream Swaddle UP, which achieves the same hip freedom through its open lower section.

## Sizing Reality: Size Up If You're Close

The HALO SleepSack Swaddle runs small. This is a known and consistent issue across the product line. The Newborn size officially fits babies from 5 to 10 lbs. In practice, a baby who is born at 8 lbs will outgrow the Newborn size within 2–3 weeks, sometimes less.

Practical sizing guidance:
- Baby under 7 lbs at birth: Newborn size is appropriate for the first month or so.
- Baby 7–9 lbs at birth: Buy one Newborn for the first 2–3 weeks, then transition directly to Small (10–18 lbs).
- Baby over 9 lbs at birth: Skip Newborn entirely and start in Small.

The Small size will initially look very large on a newborn, but the wings adjust to fit, and you'll get significantly more use from it before sizing up again.

The second sizing issue: wide or chunky babies. The swaddle wings have a fixed length and need to wrap all the way across the torso. On a baby with a larger circumference for their weight, the wings may not overlap enough to secure properly. If you find the wings just barely reaching each other, size up even if the baby hasn't hit the listed weight limit.

## The One Real Flaw: Wings Loosening on Active Babies

The velcro wings are the HALO's most useful feature and its most common point of failure. Very active babies — the ones who seem to fight every sleep attempt — can work the wings loose through repetitive arm movement. When the wings loosen, the swaddle containment decreases, the Moro reflex can complete, and the baby startles awake.

This is worse during REM sleep, when babies move more. A firmly active baby may work a wing loose within an hour, requiring a re-swaddle mid-sleep cycle — which defeats the purpose.

The fix is tighter initial application: when you wrap the wings, pull them snugger than you think necessary before pressing the velcro. The wings have enough length to allow a good grip. Re-pressing each wing firmly — ensuring the velcro has full contact, not just a partial grab — significantly reduces the rate of loosening. But for genuinely very active babies, this is still an intermittent issue that no technique fully eliminates.

The Love To Dream Swaddle UP doesn't have this problem — its sleeves are sewn construction, not velcro, so there's nothing to come undone. If wing loosening becomes a recurring issue with the HALO, switching to a sewn-construction swaddle is worth considering.

## Safe Sleep Standards and the Role of Wearable Blankets

The AAP's safe sleep guidelines recommend that babies sleep on their backs, on a firm flat surface, with no loose bedding. A loose blanket in the crib is a suffocation hazard, and the guidelines explicitly recommend against it. The HALO SleepSack Swaddle replaces that blanket with a product that stays on the baby's body and can't migrate over the face.

This is the core safety argument for any wearable blanket, including the HALO, and it's well-supported by the research. The HALO SleepSack was specifically developed in partnership with First Candle (formerly the National SIDS Foundation), making it one of the few swaddle products with direct roots in SIDS prevention advocacy.

Safe sleep reminder: stop swaddling at the first sign of rolling. Rolling typically begins around 2–3 months, though some babies start earlier. Once a baby can roll, a swaddle that contains the arms becomes a safety risk — if the baby rolls to their stomach, they can't push up or reposition. Transition to the arms-out configuration of the HALO SleepSack, or to a standard sleep sack, at this point.

## How It Compares to Alternatives

**vs. Love To Dream Swaddle UP ($35)**: The HALO is cheaper, hospital-backed, and more versatile for babies who are neutral on arm position. The Love To Dream is the better choice specifically for babies who resist arms-down configurations. If you don't know which camp your baby will fall into, the HALO is the lower-risk starting point — and if it doesn't work due to arm position preference, you'll know quickly enough to exchange.

**vs. Nested Bean Zen Sack**: The Nested Bean's lightly weighted front panel mimics the feel of a parent's hand on the chest. It's a different mechanism — it's not about arm containment, it's about tactile reassurance. The Zen Sack is better for babies who have already moved past the Moro reflex phase and just need help settling. The HALO is the right tool for the first 2–3 months when the startle reflex is strongest.

**vs. Burt's Bees Baby Beekeeper Wearable Blanket ($22)**: The Beekeeper is a post-swaddle sleep sack, not a swaddle. It has no wing system and no arm containment. It's an excellent product for babies who have finished swaddling, but it does nothing to suppress the Moro reflex. These two products serve different developmental phases and aren't really competitors.

**vs. Nanit integration**: The Nanit Pro camera's breathing wear and sleep analytics pair well with any wearable blanket that allows natural chest movement. The HALO's arms-in configuration provides the right fit for consistent breathing band readings.

## Who Should Buy the HALO SleepSack Swaddle

The HALO SleepSack Swaddle is the right default choice for most newborns. It works for the majority of babies, it's pediatrician-backed, IHDI certified, and at $26 it's affordable enough to buy multiple units for laundry rotation.

Buy two of the size you expect to use longest — either Newborn plus Small, or just two Smalls if your baby is likely to be large. You'll go through sleep sacks faster than you expect in the early weeks between spit-up and diaper leaks.

The only reason to skip it: if you already know your baby tends toward arms-up sleeping from ultrasound observations, or if you've tried it and confirmed your baby fights the arms-down configuration. In that case, the Love To Dream Swaddle UP is the logical next step.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When should I stop swaddling?**
Stop swaddling when your baby shows signs of rolling — typically around 2–3 months, though some babies begin earlier. At that point, transition to the arms-out (sleep sack only) configuration of the HALO, or to a dedicated sleep sack without swaddle wings.

**Is the HALO SleepSack safe for overnight sleep?**
Yes. It replaces loose blankets with a wearable blanket that stays on the baby's body and can't migrate over the face. It meets all current AAP safe sleep guidelines and was developed with the input of safe-sleep researchers and SIDS prevention organizations.

**What size should I buy for a newborn?**
Newborn fits 5–10 lbs, but the HALO runs small. If your baby is likely to be born above 8 lbs, buy Small (10–18 lbs) directly. You can always add a Newborn for the first two weeks if needed, but most large babies will be in Small within a month of birth.
