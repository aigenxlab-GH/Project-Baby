## What the Infantino Squeeze Station Actually Is

The Infantino Squeeze Station is not a baby food maker. It does not steam, blend, or cook anything. It is a filling station — a device that takes homemade puree you have already made and loads it into reusable squeeze pouches, the same format as Gerber or Happy Baby store-bought pouches.

That distinction matters. If you do not already have a way to make purees — a blender, food processor, BEABA, Baby Brezza, NutriBullet Baby, or even a fork — the Squeeze Station adds nothing. It is specifically for parents who are already making food and want a better delivery mechanism than a spoon and bowl.

For parents in that position, it is genuinely clever.

## The Problem It Solves

Store-bought pouches are convenient but expensive and wasteful. A single organic pouch costs $1.50–2.50. A baby going through two pouches a day spends $3–5 daily, roughly $90–150 per month on pouches alone. Over a six-month pouch-feeding period, that is $540–900.

The Squeeze Station at $22 with 10 reusable pouches changes the math dramatically. A batch of homemade sweet potato puree (one medium sweet potato, $0.75 organic) fills 4–5 pouches. Per-pouch cost drops to $0.15–0.20. Over the same six-month period, making half your baby's pouches at home saves $400–600.

Each pouch is rated for 12–15 fills. The 10 included pouches collectively handle 120–150 fill cycles before replacement. Replacement pouches cost about $8 for 10. Annual replacement cost is minimal.

Beyond cost: reusable pouches eliminate approximately 150–300 single-use plastic pouches per baby per six months. That is a real environmental difference, not just marketing language.

## Why Babies Like Pouches

There is a practical developmental reason babies accept pouches so readily. The sucking motion required to consume a pouch mirrors the nursing or bottle-feeding motion they already know. It is familiar, self-directed, and does not require a spoon or a patient adult moving food toward a moving face.

For babies in the 8–12 month range who are developing hand function and independence, pouches allow self-feeding before the fine motor coordination for spoon self-feeding arrives. This matters for daycare drop-off, car rides, and situations where spoon-and-bowl feeding is inconvenient.

The Squeeze Station's homemade pouch produces the same experience as a store pouch. Babies who have been eating store pouches will transition to homemade pouches without hesitation — the format is identical.

## How Filling Works

The process is faster than it sounds:

1. Pour smooth puree into the station's central cup
2. Place a clean pouch (open end down) into the station aperture
3. Press down on the station's lever — the pressure forces puree up into the pouch through the bottom
4. Seal the bottom clip on the pouch
5. Done. 30 seconds.

Ten pouches from a ready batch of puree takes about 5–7 minutes including setup and cleanup.

The key technical limitation: texture. Chunky stage-3 textures with visible vegetable or fruit pieces will clog the filling port. The Squeeze Station works best with smooth stage-1 and stage-2 consistency purees. If you want to fill pouches with chunky mashes, you need to either use a different filling method (a spoon and a wide-mouth pouch) or blend more finely than you would for spoon feeding.

For stage-1 smooth purees, it works flawlessly.

## What to Store In the Pouches

The pouches hold 4 ounces each, which is a typical portion for a 6–10 month old. Single-ingredient purees work perfectly. Multi-ingredient combinations also work well once you have identified any food sensitivities — introduce one new food every 3–5 days before combining.

Foods that fill and pour easily:
- Sweet potato, butternut squash, carrot, pea
- Apple, pear, mango, peach
- Avocado, banana (use quickly — these brown fast)
- Combinations: apple-carrot, pea-spinach-pear, sweet potato-apple

Foods that require smooth blending before filling:
- Leafy greens (kale, spinach) — blend thoroughly
- Any mixture with skins or seeds — strain if needed

Foods that do not work well:
- Chunky stage-3 mashes with visible pieces
- Yogurt (too thin — use directly from container)
- Anything with long fibers that tangle in the port

## Refrigeration and Freezing

Filled pouches refrigerate for 48 hours. Freeze for up to 3 months.

For freezing: lay pouches flat in the freezer until solid, then stand them upright in a freezer bag or container. Defrost in the refrigerator overnight or run under warm water for 5–10 minutes.

Label every pouch with contents and date before freezing. A batch from Sunday should be dated and used by the end of the following week if refrigerated, or by three months if frozen.

Do not microwave the filled pouches to defrost — uneven heating creates hot spots and the plastic is not rated for microwave temperatures.

## Cleaning: The Main Hassle

The pouches require thorough washing to prevent mold and food buildup. This is the most consistent complaint from parents who use the system.

The filling port is small and narrow. A dedicated pouch-cleaning brush (Infantino sells one; OXO also makes one that fits better) is necessary — standard bottle brushes are too large for the port.

Recommended cleaning routine:
1. Rinse pouch immediately after emptying — do not let puree dry inside
2. Fill with warm soapy water, shake, squeeze out
3. Use a pouch brush to scrub the interior
4. Rinse thoroughly
5. Stand upright open in a drying rack — let dry completely before closing

Dishwasher top rack is acceptable but does not clean the interior as thoroughly as hand washing. Parents who put pouches through the dishwasher without pre-rinsing find food residue remains in the port area.

Inspect pouches monthly for discoloration, mold, or damage to the seams. Replace immediately if any cracking or dark spots appear.

The filling station itself is easy — rinse the cup and port after each batch, wipe down the lever arm, and let dry.

## Comparing to Store Pouches

**Gerber or Happy Baby store pouches:** convenient, no prep needed, single-use, $1.50–2.50 each. The right choice when you genuinely do not have time to make food.

**Infantino Squeeze Station homemade pouches:** requires food prep and filling time, reusable, $0.15–0.20 per fill. The right choice when you have a batch-cooking routine established.

The Squeeze Station does not replace store pouches for most parents — it supplements them. Having 10 pouches of homemade puree for weekday daycare while keeping some store pouches for travel and emergencies is a sensible hybrid approach.

## Comparing to Spooning Directly

For home meals where you have time and a high chair, spooning from a bowl is simpler and lets you observe how much your baby is eating. The Squeeze Station format shines for:

- Daycare (pouches go in lunch bags; bowls do not)
- Car rides and stroller walks
- Situations where you cannot guarantee a clean table surface
- Babies who have learned to self-direct the pouch

For regular sit-down meals at home, a bowl and soft-tip spoon is often easier than a pouch.

## Setup and Storage

The filling station has a footprint slightly smaller than a liter bottle. It fits on a counter but takes up space. Many parents store it in a cabinet and bring it out only on batch-cooking days.

The pouches, when empty and clean, flatten and store in a drawer or zip bag. They take up almost no space.

For travel, the pouches are extremely portable. The filling station is not — it stays home.

## Verdict

The Infantino Squeeze Station earns its 8.1/10 for a focused task: it lets parents who already make baby food deliver that food in the format babies love most, at a fraction of the cost of store pouches, while eliminating single-use plastic. The trade-off is cleaning discipline — pouches that are not cleaned immediately and thoroughly become a hygiene problem.

For parents who have a batch-cooking habit and hate the cost and waste of store pouches, this is excellent value at $22. For parents who prefer the simplicity of bowl-and-spoon feeding at home, the Squeeze Station adds a workflow step that may not be worth it.

Buy this if: you already batch cook, your baby has discovered the joy of self-directed pouch feeding, and $90/month on store pouches bothers you.
