## Safety Standards: What JPMA Certification Actually Means

Before discussing value, construction, or aesthetics, the safety question deserves a direct answer. The Delta Children Emery is JPMA certified, which means it has been independently tested by an accredited laboratory against ASTM F1169 (the standard for full-size cribs) and CPSC 16 CFR Parts 1219 and 1220. These standards are not optional — they govern slat spacing (maximum 2⅜ inches), prohibit drop-side rails, require hardware that cannot loosen under normal use, and mandate structural testing that simulates years of mechanical stress.

In the UK and Europe, the equivalent standard is BS EN 716, which covers the same fundamental requirements: no protrusions, no entrapment hazards, no side mechanisms that can fail. The Emery meets the US equivalents.

The mattress you pair with the Emery must be firm — a firm, flat sleep surface is non-negotiable for safe infant sleep. The AAP is explicit on this: no soft mattresses, no memory foam, no pillow-tops. Use a crib mattress that meets ASTM F2933 or CertiPUR-US standards and fits snugly — no more than two fingers should fit between the mattress edge and the crib rail on any side.

## The Real-World Cost Comparison

The Emery's headline number is $150. But headline prices on convertible cribs are often misleading because they omit the conversion hardware. The Graco Hadley is listed at $230 — but the toddler guardrail is sold separately at roughly $40, bringing the real first-conversion cost to $270. The DaVinci Kalani at $279 is similarly structured, with the toddler rail included but the full-size conversion kit at $99 additional.

The Emery includes the toddler guardrail in the box. The real comparison is: Delta Emery at $150 complete for stage one and two conversions, versus Graco Hadley at $270 for the same two stages. That $120 difference buys a second crib mattress, a full wardrobe of newborn clothing, or several months of diaper purchases.

The full-size bed frame conversion kit is sold separately for the Emery, as it is for every convertible crib on the market. Plan for approximately $80–100 when the time comes, likely three to five years from now.

## Assembly Experience

The Emery arrives in one box and assembles with standard hardware included. Plan for 45–60 minutes with one other adult — one person holds the side panels while the other tightens the bolts. The instruction manual is clear by flat-pack standards, with numbered steps and exploded diagrams that correctly show each hardware piece's orientation.

The engineered wood panels are lighter than the solid pine used in the DaVinci Kalani, which makes handling easier during assembly but does produce a slightly less substantial feel when you test the completed crib's rigidity. The Emery does flex slightly more under pressure than a solid-wood crib — this is detectable if you test it, but it remains within safety standards and does not affect structural integrity for a sleeping infant.

One assembly note specific to the Emery: the mattress support bracket alignment requires patience. The bolt holes on the two positions are close enough that mis-threading is easy on the first attempt. Take your time with this step; the bracket must sit perfectly level for even mattress support.

## The Two Mattress Positions

The Emery's two-position mattress adjustment covers the two situations parents actually encounter. Position one — highest — makes lowering a sleeping newborn into the crib easier and reduces the reach required for the parent recovering from a C-section or dealing with postpartum discomfort. This is where you'll spend the first four to five months.

Position two — lowest — prevents a mobile baby from pulling up on the rail and potentially toppling over. Lower the mattress before the baby can pull to standing, which typically happens between seven and nine months. The AAP recommends lowering the mattress to the lowest position as soon as the baby can sit unassisted — not when they first pull to stand, which is already too late.

The Graco Hadley's three-position system adds a middle position that some parents find useful for the transitional period — baby can push up but hasn't pulled to stand yet. The middle position brings the mattress edge to a height where a standing adult can still reach the baby without fully bending while reducing the fall distance for a climbing infant. For the Emery, you move directly from high to low. Most parents report this is not a meaningful limitation in daily practice.

## Finish Quality and the Visible Seams Con

The Emery is available in White, Grey, and Espresso. The White finish is the most popular and photographs well in nurseries. The paint application is even and chip-resistant in normal use.

The "visible seams" listed in the cons refers to the joints between engineered wood panels, which are more apparent on the Emery than on cribs using solid-wood construction or higher-grade engineered panels. On close inspection, particularly at the corners, the panel joints are visible. In a nursery where the crib sits against a wall and is typically viewed from more than three feet away, this is not practically noticeable during daily use. If you are building a nursery you plan to photograph extensively, the Babyletto Hudson or DaVinci Kalani will produce better images. If you want a safe, functional crib that looks appropriate in a well-kept nursery, the Emery finish is more than adequate.

After three months of use — wiped down weekly, cleaned for diaper blowouts — the finish holds well. The White finish shows scuff marks more readily than the Grey or Espresso, particularly at the corners where a mobile baby might grip. A damp cloth removes most marks; avoid abrasive cleaners that would damage the finish.

## The 4-in-1 Conversion Path

**Stage 1 — Crib:** Birth through approximately 24–30 months, or when the child first attempts to climb out. The Emery holds a standard full-size crib mattress (28" × 52").

**Stage 2 — Toddler Bed:** Remove the front rail and attach the included toddler guardrail. The crib mattress remains. The guardrail prevents roll-outs during the adjustment period from crib to open bed. This conversion takes approximately 15 minutes.

**Stage 3 — Daybed:** Remove the toddler guardrail. The rear panel and end panels form a sofa-style platform. Many families skip directly from toddler bed to a full-size bed frame and use this stage only briefly.

**Stage 4 — Full-Size Bed:** The Delta Children full-size bed conversion kit (sold separately, approximately $80–100) converts the end panels into a headboard and footboard for a standard full-size mattress. This extends the crib's useful life to the child's early teenage years in many households.

## Delta Children Brand vs. DaVinci and Graco

Delta Children occupies the same certified-budget tier as DaVinci and Graco but uses more engineered wood and less solid wood than either competitor at comparable prices. DaVinci adds GREENGUARD Gold certification — a chemical emissions standard that tests for volatile organic compounds across more than 360 compounds. Graco provides a broader retail distribution network and customer service infrastructure that some parents find reassuring.

The Emery does not have GREENGUARD Gold certification. If chemical emissions testing beyond JPMA's standard scope is important to your household — particularly if anyone has chemical sensitivities — the DaVinci Kalani at $279 with GREENGUARD Gold is the correct choice. If budget is the primary constraint and JPMA-level certification is sufficient, the Emery holds its ground.

## What Happens After Three Months

Most parents report that the Emery's daily use experience is unremarkable — and that is the correct outcome. The mattress support sits level. The hardware does not loosen under the mechanical stress of daily lowering and lifting. The rails do not wobble. The crib does what a crib should do: it keeps the baby safely contained on a firm, flat sleep surface.

The most common complaint from long-term Emery owners is squeaking as the baby becomes more mobile — bouncing, pulling on the rails, rocking. This typically begins around six to eight months and is more common in engineered-wood cribs than solid-wood construction. The fix is straightforward: apply a small amount of food-safe beeswax or unscented petroleum jelly to the bolt connections. This resolves the squeak in most cases.

By 12 months, the hardware should be checked and re-tightened. All cribs benefit from a quarterly hardware check once the occupant is mobile; the Emery is no exception.

## Longevity Assessment

The Emery is built for a full conversion lifecycle. The end panels, which become the headboard and footboard for the full-size conversion, are the most structurally demanding components and hold up well over the typical two- to three-year crib period. The lighter construction does mean the Emery is not a crib that will survive three children over eight years the way a solid-wood crib from a premium brand might. For one to two children over a standard crib-to-toddler-bed lifespan, it performs reliably.

The resale value is modest. Budget cribs of engineered wood do not command meaningful secondary-market prices, unlike solid-wood cribs from brands like Oeuf or Nuna. If you plan to resell after use, factor this in. If you plan to use all four conversion stages over a decade, the $150 purchase price divided by years of use is genuinely exceptional value.

The UK/EU standard **BS EN 716** covers the same fundamental requirements. **GREENGUARD Gold** certification means the crib has been independently tested for over 360 chemical emissions — important for a product your baby sleeps in 12–16 hours daily.
