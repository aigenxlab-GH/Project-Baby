## The Space Problem the Tuscany Solves

New parents consistently underestimate how much clothing and accessory volume a newborn generates. The full 0–3 month wardrobe requires space for approximately eight to twelve onesies in multiple temperature variants, six to eight sleepers, receiving blankets, swaddles, washcloths, hats, socks, and seasonal items. By three months, you are also managing 3–6 month sizes held in reserve alongside current items.

A standard two-drawer dresser occupies between 18 and 30 square feet of floor space depending on its footprint. In a 10×10 nursery — which is more common than design magazines suggest — that dresser represents a significant fraction of usable floor space, competes with a nursing chair and change table for room placement, and often ends up pushed against the only wall with adequate clearance.

The Tuscany's underbed drawer occupies no additional floor space. The approximately 28 × 20 × 6-inch drawer slides out from beneath the crib frame, using the dead space that every crib leaves below the mattress support. In testing, this drawer holds two full dresser-drawer equivalents of folded infant clothing — enough for the complete 0–6 month wardrobe in a small nursery without any separate storage furniture.

## The Real Cost of the Drawer Option

The Tuscany is available with and without the underbed drawer. The without-drawer version is priced in the $200–220 range; the with-drawer version runs approximately $250–280 depending on retailer and finish. The drawer adds $30–60 to the crib price.

The alternative to the drawer is a separate changing table dresser, which typically costs $150–350. In a small nursery where you would otherwise buy dedicated storage furniture, the Tuscany's drawer option is a genuinely cost-effective decision. In a nursery where adequate storage is already built in, or where a dresser is already planned and will fit comfortably, the standard Tuscany without the drawer at $200 is the better value.

Run the numbers specific to your nursery layout before deciding. The drawer premium pays off when it replaces a separate furniture purchase. It does not pay off when you are buying the drawer as a bonus feature you could do without.

## Safety Standards: JPMA Certification and What It Covers

The Storkcraft Tuscany is JPMA certified, meeting ASTM F1169 and CPSC 16 CFR Parts 1219 and 1220. These standards are the US baseline for all cribs sold: no drop-side rails, slat spacing not exceeding 2⅜ inches, structural testing against mechanical loads, and finish testing for lead and phthalates.

The UK and European equivalent is BS EN 716, which addresses the same fundamental safety parameters — slat spacing (60mm maximum), structural integrity, entrapment prevention at protrusions and cutouts. The Tuscany is a US-market product designed to ASTM/CPSC standards.

The underbed drawer is specifically covered in JPMA's certification review process. The drawer must close flush with the crib base, contain no hardware accessible to the crib occupant, and not compromise the structural integrity of the crib frame under load. Storkcraft's certification confirms these requirements are met.

Safe-sleep requirements apply to the Tuscany as they do to every crib: firm, flat mattress, no soft bedding, no inclined sleep surfaces, no crib bumpers. The drawer's presence beneath the crib has no interaction with the sleep surface above — the mattress platform sits on the standard support brackets, and the drawer is entirely beneath that structure.

Mattress firmness is non-negotiable. The Tuscany accepts standard 28 × 52 inch US crib mattresses. Use a mattress that fits snugly on all four sides — the finger-gap test (no more than two fingers between mattress edge and any rail) confirms correct fit. The AAP recommends against any soft mattress for infant sleep regardless of how it is marketed.

## Assembly: Heavier Than Competitors

The Tuscany's weight con is among the most practically relevant of any crib in this category. The drawer-equipped version weighs significantly more than crib-only models at comparable prices — the drawer mechanism, drawer box, and reinforced base frame required to support it add approximately 15–20 pounds over a basic crib frame.

Assembly takes 75–90 minutes with two adults. The added complexity over a standard crib is the drawer assembly and integration with the base frame. The drawer slides on metal glides that must be correctly aligned during assembly — the instruction manual covers this step clearly, but it requires attention. Misaligned drawer glides that are caught too late in assembly may require partial disassembly to correct.

The instruction manual is organised by section: crib frame, mattress support installation, and drawer installation. Reading all three sections before beginning assembly prevents the common mistake of assembling the crib fully before adding the drawer mechanism, which requires working underneath the assembled frame.

Once assembled, the Tuscany is not a crib you will move frequently. The combination of traditional solid-panel construction and the drawer unit makes the assembled crib heavier than the Graco Hadley, which is already noted for its weight. Plan your nursery placement carefully before final assembly — ideally with all furniture positioned before completing the last fasteners.

## Three Mattress Positions

The Tuscany's three-position mattress adjustment covers the same development arc as the Graco Hadley: high position for newborns (roughly 0–5 months), middle position for sitting-capable but not yet standing babies (5–7 months), lowest position once the baby can pull to standing (7–9 months).

The three-position system is an advantage over the two-position systems in the Delta Children Emery and IKEA SUNDVIK. The middle position is genuinely useful during the three-to-four month period where a baby is mobile enough to push up but hasn't attempted standing. At this stage, the highest position creates a small fall risk if the baby pushes to a seated position unexpectedly. The middle position provides safety margin while keeping the mattress at a comfortable height for adults.

The bracket adjustment requires standard tools. A reminder: lower proactively. The common mistake is leaving the mattress at the high position until the baby is already standing in the crib. By that point, the adjustment is urgent rather than planned. Lower to the middle position at the first sign of sitting ability, and to the lowest position before the first standing attempt — not after.

## Traditional Panel Design: The Aesthetic Question

The Tuscany's panel-and-rail design is unambiguously traditional. The solid panel end pieces, the decorative base moulding, and the turned spindle pattern (on some finishes) are recognisably old-school American nursery furniture. This is not an insult — it is a description. Traditional nursery aesthetics have endured for generations because they are neutral, familiar, and complement a wide range of decorating styles from country to cottage to transitional.

What the Tuscany does not suit is the modern minimalist nursery aesthetic. If your nursery is designed around clean lines, Scandinavian simplicity, or the architectural furniture aesthetic associated with Babyletto, the Tuscany's ornamentation will look visually discordant. The DaVinci Kalani's mid-century modern lines or the Babyletto Hudson's contemporary profile are better fits for those interiors.

The available finishes (White, Espresso, Rustic Grey, and others depending on configuration) allow some adaptation to different nursery palettes. The White finish reads most contemporary; the Espresso most traditional.

## The 4-in-1 Conversion Path

**Stage 1 — Crib:** Birth through approximately 24–30 months. The Tuscany holds a standard 28 × 52 inch US crib mattress.

**Stage 2 — Toddler Bed:** Remove the front rail and attach the toddler guardrail (included in most Tuscany configurations — verify before purchase). The crib mattress continues in use. This conversion takes 20 minutes.

**Stage 3 — Daybed:** Remove the toddler rail for a sofa-style platform. This stage is used briefly by most families.

**Stage 4 — Full-Size Bed:** With the Storkcraft full-size conversion kit (sold separately, approximately $80–100), the end panels become headboard and footboard for a standard full-size mattress. The underbed drawer remains functional in the full-size configuration, which is a genuine bonus in a teenager's room where floor space is similarly constrained.

The drawer's persistence through all conversion stages is an underrated feature. Most parents focus on the drawer's nursery utility, but the same storage benefit applies in a toddler's room and a child's room — the drawer becomes underwear and sock storage, then eventually desk accessory storage, over a decade of use.

## Longevity: Three Months and Beyond

After three months of daily use, the Tuscany's hardware holds well under normal conditions. The drawer mechanism shows the most wear over time — the metal glides accumulate dust and debris from the floor, which can cause drawer drag. A quarterly cleaning of the drawer glides with a dry cloth and an application of drawer wax or paste wax resolves this reliably.

The panel construction holds up to the mechanical stress of a mobile baby better than the IKEA SUNDVIK's lighter construction. The traditional design's heavier material use provides structural longevity that supports confident use through the toddler stage and beyond.

By the toddler stage, the underbed drawer becomes more interesting — and more accessible — to the toddler themselves. A child who can reach the drawer will open it. The Tuscany's drawer does not include a childproofing lock. Many parents add an aftermarket drawer stop or child lock once the toddler can reach the drawer, to prevent clothing retrieval becoming a morning entertainment option.

## Tuscany vs. Graco Hadley vs. DaVinci Kalani

The Tuscany at $250 (with drawer) competes directly with the Graco Hadley ($230 plus ~$40 toddler rail) and the DaVinci Kalani ($279). Against the Hadley, the Tuscany offers the storage advantage — the drawer — but does not have Graco's brand support infrastructure. Against the Kalani, the Tuscany offers the storage advantage at $30 less, but lacks GREENGUARD Gold certification and the Kalani's superior solid pine construction.

If nursery storage is the primary priority and traditional aesthetics fit your nursery: Tuscany. If chemical off-gassing is the primary priority: Kalani. If brand support reliability is the primary priority: Hadley.

## Safety Standards & Certifications

Cribs must meet **ASTM F1169** (full-size cribs) or **ASTM F406** (non-full-size cribs) and comply with **CPSC 16 CFR Parts 1219 and 1220**, which prohibit drop-side rails and mandate slat spacing of no more than 2⅜ inches. The European equivalent standard **BS EN 716** covers the same structural and entrapment requirements. **GREENGUARD Gold** certification — not held by the Tuscany — covers chemical emissions testing across more than 360 compounds.

For the **Storkcraft Tuscany 4-in-1 Convertible Crib** specifically: JPMA certified, independently verified. Our 8.2/10 score factors in full safety compliance.

## Parent Tips

- Decide before purchase whether you want the drawer option — it significantly changes the total price and the assembly complexity.
- Place the assembled Tuscany before tightening the final fasteners — it is not a crib you will easily relocate once complete.
- Clean the drawer glides quarterly to prevent dust accumulation causing drawer drag.
- Lower the mattress to the middle position at the first sign of sitting ability, not sitting competence — anticipate development, don't react to it.
- The underbed drawer is functional through all conversion stages — factor this longevity of the storage benefit into the value calculation.
