Few baby products generate as much debate as wearable infant monitors. The **Owlet Dream Sock** sits at the centre of it: is tracking your baby's heart rate and oxygen levels a reassuring safeguard or a recipe for parental anxiety?

After two months of overnight testing, our answer is: it depends entirely on you.

## What It Actually Does

The Dream Sock clips to your baby's foot and uses pulse oximetry — the same technology as hospital finger clips — to monitor heart rate and blood oxygen saturation (SpO2) in real time. Data streams to the companion app every second, and the base station glows green when values are in a healthy range.

If values fall outside preset thresholds, the base station flashes red and sounds an alert, and you receive an app notification.

## The Important Caveat

The Dream Sock is **not a medical device**. It will not detect every dangerous event, and it cannot prevent SIDS. The AAP does not recommend monitors for healthy, full-term infants. If your baby has a diagnosed cardiorespiratory condition, talk to your paediatrician about clinical-grade monitoring.

## Who It's Actually For

The Dream Sock makes most sense for:
- Parents of premature babies recently discharged from NICU
- Parents with high baseline anxiety who genuinely sleep better with data
- Families where a doctor has suggested monitoring

For healthy full-term infants, safe sleep practices (back to sleep, firm flat surface, nothing in the crib) remain the evidence-based intervention.

## The App

Owlet's app is excellent. Sleep reports show overnight trends, and the history view helps identify whether a 3am dip in oxygen was positional (sock slipped) or worth noting.

## Our Verdict

If you'll genuinely sleep better knowing your baby's SpO2 is 98%, the Dream Sock delivers that. Just enter with calibrated expectations: it's a wellness tracker, not a medical safety net.
