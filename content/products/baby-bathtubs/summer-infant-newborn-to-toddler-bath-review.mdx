When my daughter was three weeks old I was already dreading what came after every blowout diaper: a bath in a flimsy mesh sling draped over our bathroom sink. My husband would hold her head while I awkwardly sponged her down with one hand. We needed something better — and when a friend mentioned the Summer Infant Newborn-to-Toddler Bath Center, I figured $40 was worth trying if it meant both hands free during newborn bath time.

We used it from week three through her second birthday. That’s roughly 22 months of regular use. So everything below comes from actual experience, not a quick unboxing.

## What You’re Actually Getting

The Summer Infant Bath Center is a modular plastic-and-mesh system that converts between four configurations as your baby grows. The frame is a lightweight plastic shell with metal hinges and a rubberized non-slip base. Snap the included mesh sling into the frame and you have a newborn hammock. Remove the sling and reposition the insert and you get an infant recline. Add the molded bucket seat and it becomes a toddler seat. Remove everything and you have an open tub for an older toddler.

The silicone rinser cup — marketed as a "showerhead attachment" — clips to the frame. You fill it with warm water and squeeze it over your baby’s head to rinse shampoo without the full shower pressure that sends babies into meltdowns. It sounds minor. It is not minor. That rinser cup saved us from months of screaming shampoo rinses.

The whole thing measures about 22 inches wide in its standard configuration. It fits in most bathroom sinks, though not all kitchen single-bowl sinks. In the full toddler-tub configuration it stretches to about 30 inches — at that point you’re placing it on the bathroom floor or inside the big tub.

## Safety First: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Before we get into what we liked and what frustrated us, let’s cover the safety basics, because no baby bathtub review is complete without them.

Never leave a baby or toddler unattended near water. Not for a second. Not to grab a towel from the next room. Not to check your phone. Infants can drown in as little as two inches of water in seconds. Every single bath, from newborn to toddler, required one of us physically present and engaged.

For newborns, keep water depth at about two to three inches — just enough to cover their body when lying in the sling. Water temperature should feel warm but not hot on the inside of your wrist. The target is around 37–38°C. Tap water runs hotter than that. Fill the tub first, then test the temperature before lowering baby in.

If your baby still has a cord stump, stick to sponge baths until it falls off, usually within two to three weeks. You can use the Summer Infant’s mesh sling as a sponge bath support with the water mostly drained, keeping the stump dry. Do not submerge a newborn with an intact cord stump.

The mesh sling creates a slightly reclined position that keeps a newborn’s head above water and their airway clear. This is the correct angle for a newborn who lacks head control. Do not try to prop a newborn upright in the toddler seat configuration — that’s not designed for early infants.

## The Four Configurations in Practice

**Newborn Sling (Birth to about 3 months):** The mesh sling suspends baby at a diagonal angle. Their back is supported, their head is slightly elevated, and water can pool around them without submerging their face. The mesh is breathable and gentle on newborn skin. My daughter had no irritation from the fabric even with her sensitive skin. The sling detaches and goes in the washing machine on a gentle cold cycle, which is genuinely useful because newborns are messy.

One real problem: the sling takes four to six hours to fully air dry. We kept a small microfiber towel nearby to pat it before reuse if we needed to do back-to-back baths. In very humid bathrooms, the mesh can develop a slight musty smell if left folded while damp. Hang it stretched out, not bunched.

**Infant Recline (about 3 to 6 months):** Remove the sling and reposition the insert for a more reclined-but-not-flat position. This worked well for us through the stage where my daughter was building head control but wasn’t sitting independently. The transition between newborn sling and infant recline was straightforward and took about two minutes.

**Toddler Seat (about 6 to 18 months):** The molded bucket seat drops in to support an upright-ish seated position. This was probably our most-used configuration. My daughter hit this stage around seven months and we stayed here almost a year. The seat keeps them contained while you do hair washing. The rinser cup became essential here — she’d let me pour it over her head without protest.

**Open Toddler Tub (18 months to 3 years):** At this stage it’s basically a shallow plastic tub. It worked fine on the bathroom floor during the 18–22 month period before we transitioned her to the big tub with a non-slip mat. Honestly, most active toddlers will try to climb out. My daughter did. The sides are not high enough to contain a determined 2-year-old.

## The Rinser Cup Is the Underrated Star

I mentioned this above but it deserves its own paragraph. Rinsing shampoo off a baby’s head without getting soap in their eyes is one of the genuinely hard parts of early bath time. The rinser cup holds about a cup of warm water and squeezes with controlled flow. You can aim it precisely at the hairline. You can shield eyes with your other hand while you rinse. After months of struggling with a regular cup that would slosh unevenly, the rinser cup felt like a proper solution.

The cup clips to the frame and doesn’t rattle loose during a bath, which is more than I can say for cups I’ve tried resting on tub ledges.

## Storage Is the Biggest Real Complaint

The Summer Infant in its toddler configuration is bulky. There’s no way around it. We have a medium-sized bathroom with limited cabinet space, and we ended up propping this thing against the wall behind our toilet for about a year. It’s not cute. It takes up floor space. If you have a small bathroom you will notice it.

The Puj Flyte (8.4/10), which folds flat, wins on storage by a wide margin. The Puj is a single-fold piece of foam that hangs on a hook. The Summer Infant is a multi-piece plastic structure. If storage is your binding constraint, the Puj is the right call even though it lacks the longevity of the Summer Infant’s four stages.

## How It Compares to the Competition

A few honest comparisons to products at similar price points:

**FridaBaby 4-in-1 (8.6/10, $50):** The FridaBaby is slightly better executed — its non-slip textured seat bottom is notably more grippy than the Summer Infant, and the built-in rinse cup design feels more integrated. The FridaBaby loses to the Summer Infant on age range: the FridaBaby tops out at about 24 months while the Summer Infant is designed to 3 years. If you have a small bathroom, the FridaBaby’s sink-compatible design throughout all stages is also a meaningful advantage. For us, the Summer Infant’s extended longevity made it the better choice.

**Fisher-Price 4-in-1 Sling ‘n Seat (8.3/10, $35):** Very similar concept to the Summer Infant, slightly cheaper. The Fisher-Price is 31 inches long from the start — it doesn’t fit in most kitchen sinks at all. The Summer Infant is more flexible in where you can use it during the early stages. Both have mesh slings that require careful drying. The Summer Infant’s rinser cup is something the Fisher-Price doesn’t include.

**Angelcare Soft Touch Baby Bath Support (8.5/10):** The Angelcare is a single-stage mesh support designed for the newborn and early infant period only — typically used up to about four to five months. It does that one job extremely well. The mesh molds to baby’s shape and feels more ergonomic than the Summer Infant’s sling in the newborn stage. But then you need a different solution. For parents who want a dedicated newborn solution and are happy to buy a second tub later, Angelcare is worth considering. For parents who want one purchase, the Summer Infant wins.

**Primo EuroBath Plus (8.2/10, $25):** The EuroBath is the cheapest option that gives you genuine ergonomic design for both newborn and toddler stages, but it has no removable parts, no rinser cup, and a larger footprint that makes sink use hard. At $25 it’s an excellent budget option; at $40 the Summer Infant’s added features justify the difference.

**Puj Flyte Flat Fold Infant Tub (8.4/10):** Different category entirely. The Puj is foam, folds flat, hangs on a hook. It is the right choice for parents with very small bathrooms who want something they can hang on the back of a door. It works until about six months and then your baby has outgrown it. The Summer Infant beats it on longevity; the Puj beats it on storage.

## Key Takeaways

- **One tub, birth to 3 years** — if you commit to the system, you do not need to buy another tub
- **The rinser cup is genuinely useful** — more so than I expected going in
- **Machine-washable sling is a real advantage** — but factor in drying time
- **Plan for the storage reality** — this takes up space; have a spot for it before you buy
- **Toddler stage is functional but not exceptional** — dedicated toddler tubs offer more stability for active kids
- **Never leave baby unattended**, water temperature at 37–38°C, sponge baths until cord stump falls off

**Our Score: 8.4/10.** Strong mid-range choice for parents who want a single purchase to carry them through the full early bathing period. The Fisher-Price at $35 is the budget alternative with similar coverage; the FridaBaby at $50 is the premium step-up with better finish and grip. The Summer Infant sits comfortably in between and earns its score.
