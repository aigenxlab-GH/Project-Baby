The **Joovy Caboose Ultralight Graphite** exists because the double stroller problem is harder than it looks. You have two children who need pushing, but traditional double strollers are either side-by-side (30+ inches wide, won't fit through doorways) or front-and-back tandems (long, heavy, hard to maneuver). The Caboose takes a third approach: a sit-and-stand design where the toddler rides in the rear on a platform or jump seat, making the stroller dramatically shorter and narrower than a traditional tandem while keeping it within a manageable weight.

## The Weight Problem With Double Strollers: Context First

The Caboose Ultralight weighs 26 lbs. That sounds heavy until you look at the alternatives:
- BOB Revolution Flex Duallie (jogging double): 36 lbs
- Baby Jogger City Select 2 (tandem double): 29.5 lbs
- UPPAbaby Vista V2 with RumbleSeat loaded: 33+ lbs
- Graco Ready2Grow (budget tandem): 28 lbs
- Joovy Big Caboose (standard, non-ultralight version): 32 lbs

At 26 lbs, the Caboose Ultralight is the lightest double stroller that handles two children in the 0–45 lb range. The "Ultralight" designation isn't just branding — it's approximately 6 lbs lighter than the next lightest comparable double.

When you're already managing two children, that 6-lb difference in the stroller matters. Loading a 26-lb stroller into a car boot is manageable one-handed; loading a 36-lb jogging double one-handed while holding a toddler is a genuine challenge.

## The Sit-and-Stand Design: How It Actually Works

The Caboose's rear position has two options:
1. **Standing platform:** The toddler stands on a padded platform at the back of the stroller, behind the front seat. They hold onto a grab rail. Good for short distances and capable walkers who tire of walking but don't want to sit.
2. **Jump seat:** A fold-down seat attached to the platform. The toddler sits in a basic seat with no harness (the seat is designed for older toddlers who can sit independently). Suitable for approximately 18 months to 45 lbs.

The front seat is a full stroller seat with 5-point harness, multiple reclines, and 45-lb capacity. This is where the infant car seat clips in (via adapter) for the newborn-plus-toddler configuration.

The key width advantage: at 25.5 inches, the Caboose fits through standard 27-inch doorways. Most side-by-side doubles at 30–32 inches don't. For parents navigating shops, restaurants, public transport, and home doorways, this matters in every outing.

## The Newborn + Toddler Configuration

The most common Caboose purchase is triggered by the announcement of a second baby when a first child is still stroller-age (18 months to 4 years). The new configuration:
- Infant in the front seat via car seat adapter (Joovy sells universal and brand-specific adapters)
- Toddler in the rear jump seat or on the standing platform

The front infant car seat position works from birth with an appropriate adapter — check Joovy's compatibility list for your specific seat. Without the adapter, the front seat is rated from 6 months for independent sitting.

When both children are out of infant seats and using stroller seats, the rear jump seat accommodates the older child up to 45 lbs. Most parents use this configuration from approximately 18 months to 4 years for the rear child.

## Width Versus a Side-by-Side Double

The 25.5-inch width is the Caboose's most practically significant specification. Here's what it means in real locations:

- **Standard interior doorway (27–28 inches):** Caboose fits with 1.5–2.5 inches of clearance. Most side-by-sides don't clear this at all.
- **Accessible/widened doorway (32 inches):** Both fit.
- **Shop aisles:** The Caboose navigates most shop aisles where side-by-sides stall.
- **Supermarket aisles:** Caboose fits; most side-by-sides require backing down an aisle to turn around.
- **Lift doors:** The Caboose fits in a standard 36-inch lift; many side-by-sides don't.

For families in urban areas, apartment buildings with narrow corridors, or who regularly shop with the stroller, the 25.5-inch width is a genuine operational advantage over side-by-side doubles. For families with wide suburban house doorways and a big-box-store shopping style, the width advantage matters less.

## Boot Fit and Car Loading

The Caboose Ultralight folds in two steps — lift the fold lever, collapse the frame. Folded dimensions are approximately 35 × 25.5 × 16 inches. This is a large folded footprint — it fills the boot of a sedan substantially. In an SUV or minivan, it's manageable. In a compact car, it will be a tight fit.

The 26-lb weight is the lightest in this class but is still 26 lbs — a deliberate lift rather than a casual grab. With a toddler in one arm and a baby in a carrier or car seat, boot loading the Caboose requires setting someone down momentarily or having a second adult.

Loading tip from parents who use it: fold the stroller and load it into the boot before buckling the children. This sequence — stroller in boot, then children in seats — is much easier than trying to do it in reverse.

## Rear Seat Access: The Practical Limitation

The biggest day-to-day limitation of the Caboose is getting to the rear child. Because the jump seat is behind the front seat, the front seat passenger (or the car seat in the front position) partially blocks direct access to the rear. If the rear child needs something — drops a toy, needs their hat adjusted, has a diaper that needs checking — you reach around the side or from the rear of the stroller.

With the infant car seat in the front position, the rear toddler can be reached from behind or from the side. With a seated toddler in the front seat, the rear jump seat access requires either stopping and reaching around the side or asking the front child to lean forward momentarily. It's manageable but not seamless.

This is the fundamental geometry trade-off of a tandem design. Side-by-side doubles give equal access to both children; tandems give easy front access but require workaround rear access.

## Ride Quality: Honest Assessment

The Caboose Ultralight's ride is acceptable for a double stroller at this price and weight. The 7-inch front wheels handle smooth pavement and gradual terrain changes. For the range of daily urban use (pavement, parks with paved paths, shopping centres), it's fine.

On cobblestones, rough grass, or unpaved paths, the ride is noticeably less smooth than the Baby Jogger City Mini GT2 or the UPPAbaby VISTA V2 with RumbleSeat. The trade-off is explicit: the Caboose costs $279 and weighs 26 lbs; the VISTA V2 with RumbleSeat costs $1,329 and handles rough terrain better. For most families' daily use cases, the Caboose's ride quality is adequate.

The standing platform adds a slight wobble when a toddler shifts weight laterally while standing — noticeable but not unsafe. On slopes, the rear-weighted standing configuration requires more effort to push uphill than a traditional seated double.

## Caboose Ultralight vs. UPPAbaby VISTA V2 with RumbleSeat

The most common comparison for parents already owning a VISTA V2:

The VISTA V2 with RumbleSeat ($1,099 + $230 = $1,329) is a better stroller — smoother ride, better canopy, premium build, easier rear seat access. It's also 7 lbs heavier loaded and costs $1,050 more.

The Caboose Ultralight at $279 is lighter, narrower (25.5 vs. approximately 28 inches for the VISTA double), fits through more doorways, and costs a fraction of the price. For parents who don't already own a premium single stroller and need a double, the Caboose is an entirely rational primary stroller choice.

For parents who already own the VISTA V2 and are evaluating the RumbleSeat versus buying the Caboose as a second stroller: the RumbleSeat keeps everything on one frame (no second stroller to store or maintain); the Caboose is cheaper and more maneuverable but requires storing and maintaining an additional stroller.

## Long-Term Ownership: What Two-Child Families Say

The Caboose's most commonly reported long-term positive: the standing platform. Older toddlers who don't want to sit but can't walk the full distance of a shopping trip use the platform extensively. Parents describe it as the feature that extends the stroller's useful life beyond what a seated double would provide — a 4-year-old who is too big for a stroller seat and too tired to walk can stand on the platform.

Most commonly reported long-term negative: the rear jump seat becomes less useful as the second child grows past 2.5 years and wants to be more active. By age 3, most rear-seat children prefer the standing platform exclusively.

**Maintenance:** The front swivel wheels require quarterly cleaning of debris from axle housings — same maintenance as other strollers with small swivel wheels. The standing platform's locking mechanism should be checked monthly to confirm it locks securely.

## Who Should NOT Buy the Joovy Caboose Ultralight

**Families who need a truly flat recline for the rear child** — the rear jump seat has minimal padding and no recline. If the rear child needs to sleep in the stroller, they either sleep in the front seat or transfer to a pram setup. The Caboose is not a sleep solution for the rear position.

**Parents who need premium ride quality on rough terrain** — the Caboose's 7-inch wheels handle city use but not trails or rough park paths. For active outdoor families, the Baby Jogger City Select 2 (which folds into a double but is heavier) handles terrain better.

**Families where both children are under 3 years old simultaneously and both need full stroller seats** — the jump seat is most appropriate for a child who is partially capable of independent standing (18 months+). Twins under 2 years are better served by a side-by-side double with full seats and harnesses for both positions.

## What to Know Before You Buy

- **Buy the infant car seat adapter at the same time** — you can't use a car seat in the front position without it, and it's sold separately.
- **Measure your car boot before buying** — the folded dimensions are substantial; confirm it fits your specific vehicle.
- **The rear jump seat has no harness** — it's for children old enough to sit independently without restraint during movement. Don't use it for toddlers under 18 months who might fall forward.
- **The standing platform weight limit (45 lbs) is independent of front seat weight** — both positions carry their own capacity, not combined.
- **Check canopy on both positions** — the rear jump seat and platform positions have more limited canopy coverage than the front seat. In direct sun, rear-position children need hats and may need a clip-on parasol.
