## Buying Guide  
When choosing a toilet reducer seat, consider the following factors:  
- **Age Readiness**: Most toddlers are ready for the adult toilet between 18 and 24 months. If your child is toilet-confident and comfortable climbing onto the seat, a reducer like the Munchkin is a good fit. However, younger children or those struggling with balance may benefit from a standalone potty first.  
- **Safety Features**: Prioritize seats with non-slip pads and secure handles. Avoid flimsy models that wobble or lack grip, as instability can discourage use or lead to accidents.  
- **Step Stool Compatibility**: Always pair the reducer with a step stool to prevent strain on the child’s back and knees. A 9-inch stool is typically sufficient, but ensure it’s sturdy and fits under the toilet.  
- **Toilet Shape Compatibility**: Check if the seat fits both round and elongated toilets. Most homes have standard shapes, but it’s worth confirming if you frequently use public restrooms.  
- **Storage Needs**: If space is limited, opt for a foldable design like the Munchkin’s. Non-foldable models may take up more storage room.  
- **Common Mistakes**: Avoid skipping the step stool, as the height difference can be overwhelming for toddlers. Also, don’t force use if the child is anxious—transitioning to the adult toilet should be gradual and positive.

## The Step Stool Factor

The Munchkin seat alone is incomplete without a step stool. You cannot ignore this. A toddler climbing onto a standard toilet (17-19 inches high) without a stool is setting up a fall risk. The stool serves two functions: approach (getting up to the seat) and positioning (feet planted firmly during use for proper elimination mechanics).

Common step stool options that pair well:
- **Munchkin Step Stool** ($15-20): Simple, light plastic, portable
- **Generic 9-inch plastic stool** ($10-15): Works equally well, interchangeable
- **Wooden step stool** ($25-40): More aesthetically pleasing, slightly more sturdy

The most important feature: non-slip base. A stool that slides during use is a fall hazard. Test it before committing to any model.
