The **Lansinoh Signature Pro Double Electric Breast Pump** earns its place in the breast pump category primarily on insurance coverage and flange sizing. Most parents pay nothing for it, and the four included flange sizes address one of the most commonly overlooked factors in pumping performance. That combination makes it a smart choice for parents whose insurance plan covers it.

## The Insurance Reality First

Under the Affordable Care Act, most US health insurance plans cover at least one breast pump per pregnancy. The Lansinoh Signature Pro is on the covered formulary of a wide range of insurers. Before reading further, call the member services number on your insurance card and ask for their breast pump coverage — specifically which models are approved and whether you're eligible for a purchase or rental. At a $55 retail price, it is already inexpensive by breast pump standards; at $0 with insurance coverage, it is an obvious starting point.

The Spectra S2 is also commonly covered and scores slightly higher in our testing (9.1/10 vs. 8.3/10 for the Lansinoh). If both are available through your insurer, the Spectra S2's closed system and quieter operation give it an edge. But if the Lansinoh is covered and the Spectra is not, the Lansinoh is the right pump to use.

## The Flange Sizing Problem — And How Lansinoh Addresses It

The single most impactful variable in pumping output is flange fit. Most pumps include two flange sizes. The Lansinoh Signature Pro includes flanges in 21mm, 24mm, 27mm, and 30mm — the most comprehensive range in this price tier.

The correct flange size is determined by measuring your nipple shaft diameter (the shaft only, not the areola). Add 2–3mm to that measurement to get the target flange diameter. A nipple measuring 20mm needs a 22–23mm flange — the 24mm Lansinoh flange, erring slightly large.

**Why this matters so much:**
- Flange too large: excess areola tissue is drawn into the tunnel, breaking the suction seal and reducing output. Creates a characteristic "no-pull" feeling despite the pump running.
- Flange too small: nipple shaft rubs against the tunnel walls during each cycle — genuinely painful — and restricts milk flow out of the duct openings. Output drops significantly.

The impact: correct flange sizing improves output by 20–30% compared to the wrong size. This is a bigger variable than any pump setting, suction level, or pumping schedule adjustment. Lansinoh's four-size inclusion means parents are far more likely to start with the right size, which directly translates to better initial pumping experiences.

## 2-Phase Expression in Practice

Phase 1 (Stimulation): 60–90 cycles per minute at lower vacuum — rapid, light suction that mimics a baby's initial latch pattern to trigger the let-down reflex. Run this for 1–2 minutes at session start, or until you feel the milk begin to flow.

Phase 2 (Expression): 38–54 cycles per minute at higher vacuum — the active extraction phase. After let-down, switch to Phase 2 and adjust suction to the highest comfortable level. This is the phase that actually empties the breast.

The manual phase switch (you press a button to move from Phase 1 to Phase 2 after let-down) is how Lansinoh, Medela, and Spectra all operate. No consumer pump auto-switches; it is always user-initiated when you feel the let-down reflex. This is something to learn and anticipate, not a flaw in the pump.

Session length: 15–20 minutes per breast per session is typical. Pumping past the point of milk flow does not increase output and can cause nipple sensitivity.

## The Closed System Advantage

The Lansinoh Signature Pro is a closed-system pump, meaning there is a physical barrier (the membrane and valve system) between the milk collection area and the motor. Milk cannot travel into the motor tubing. This matters for hygiene: the pump parts that contact milk can be washed thoroughly, while the motor itself stays clean.

Compare this to the Medela Pump In Style with MaxFlow, which is an open system. The Medela's open design means milk can theoretically travel through tubing toward the motor if the pump is stored with wet parts or used at unusual angles. For single-user, well-maintained use this is rarely a practical issue — but the closed system is objectively better for long-term hygiene.

## Portability: The Battery Limitation

The Lansinoh Signature Pro runs on AC adapter only — no internal battery. For pumping at a desk at home or in an office with a plug, this is no constraint. For pumping in a car, at a conference, or in a room without consistent outlet access, the lack of battery is a genuine limitation.

The Spectra S1 Plus solves this: it adds a rechargeable battery (approximately 3 hours per charge) for approximately $50 more than the Spectra S2. If you regularly pump away from power outlets, the Spectra S1's battery is worth the additional cost. The Lansinoh has no equivalent battery option.

## What's Not Included

The Lansinoh Signature Pro does not include milk storage bottles. It includes flanges, tubing, valves, membranes, and the pump unit. You need to purchase collection bottles separately — standard wide-neck bottles fit, and Lansinoh's own 5 oz and 8 oz collection bottles are available for $10–15 per set.

This is a cost that catches parents off guard. Budget an additional $15–20 for bottles when purchasing. The Medela Pump In Style MaxFlow includes four 150ml collection bottles — a genuine kit advantage at its higher $199 price point.

## Performance for High-Demand Pumpers

For parents with average or moderate pumping needs — returning to work and pumping 2–3 times daily to maintain supply — the Lansinoh Signature Pro performs well. For exclusive pumpers (EP) who rely entirely on the pump to feed their baby and may pump 7–10 times daily, the Lansinoh's motor is less powerful than the Spectra S2. EP parents who need maximum sustained output for weeks or months typically find the Spectra S2 the better tool.

The practical test: if your first pumping sessions with the Lansinoh yield volumes you expect based on your feeding history, you're fine. If output seems lower than expected after 2 weeks of correct flange sizing and consistent pumping schedule, consider whether the Spectra S2's higher suction ceiling is the factor.

## Cleaning and Maintenance

Wash all parts that contact milk after every session in hot soapy water. The FDA's guidance is to wash pump parts in a basin dedicated to this purpose (not the kitchen sink, where other bacteria can transfer), rinse with clean water, and air dry on a clean paper towel or rack. Avoid the dishwasher for silicone membranes and valves — heat degrades them faster than hand washing.

Replace the membranes (small silicone pieces at the valve) every 2–3 months for daily use. Membrane wear is the most common cause of suction decline that parents attribute to pump failure. Keep a spare set; they cost $5–8.

## Sterilisation and NHS Guidance

NHS guidelines for sterilising breast pump equipment recommend steam sterilising or boiling pump parts after every use for babies under 6 months. For older babies, hot soapy water and thorough rinsing is sufficient. The Lansinoh's parts are steam steriliser compatible. Steriliser bags (Milton, Sangenic, or microwave bags) work effectively and are common in UK households.
