## Understanding Flange Sizing — The Factor That Overrides Everything Else

Before discussing the MaxFlow's features, there is one variable that matters more than the pump's motor, suction rating, or technology: flange fit.

The PersonalFit Flex connectors come in five sizes (21mm, 24mm, 26mm, 28mm, and 30mm). Most people assume the 24mm or 28mm connectors included in the box are correct and never check. This is the most common pumping mistake. The correct flange size is determined by measuring your nipple shaft diameter (not the areola — just the nipple) and adding 2–3mm. A connector that is too large draws in excess areola, breaks suction, and reduces output. One that is too small causes the nipple to rub against the tunnel walls — genuinely painful — and restricts milk flow.

Getting the flange size right can increase output by 20–30%. Before concluding that a pump is underperforming, verify connector size. The Medela MaxFlow's advantage here is that all five PersonalFit Flex sizes come in the box at $199, whereas competitors often include only two sizes and charge extra for others.

## 2-Phase Expression: What It Actually Does

Medela pioneered 2-Phase Expression in the 1990s based on research on how babies nurse. A nursing baby starts with rapid, shallow sucks to trigger letdown (the milk ejection reflex), then switches to slower, deeper expression once milk starts flowing. The Pump In Style MaxFlow's two-phase system mirrors this automatically.

Phase 1 (Stimulation): 55–78 cycles per minute at lower vacuum — rapid, light, triggering letdown
Phase 2 (Expression): 38–54 cycles per minute at higher vacuum — slower, deeper extraction

In practice, most parents switch from Phase 1 to Phase 2 after about 2 minutes or immediately after feeling letdown. The pump does not auto-switch — you press the button. This is a minor criticism; the Spectra S2 has a similar manual switch. Neither pump switches automatically, so this is industry-wide rather than a Medela-specific issue.

## PersonalFit Flex vs. Standard Flanges

Traditional breast pump flanges — including the older Medela Personal Fit original design — are rigid plastic. The PersonalFit Flex connectors have a flexible rim that bends and moves as you shift position. This matters more than it sounds. When pumping at a desk, in a car, or while leaning back in a chair, your body moves slightly. Rigid flanges can break the breast seal when you shift; the Flex connectors maintain contact through normal movement.

The shape itself is also different from the standard funnel: the Flex connector is angled at approximately 105 degrees rather than straight-line, which accommodates the natural position of the breast and nipple better for most body types. Women who found traditional flanges uncomfortable often report more comfort with the Flex connectors.

## ACA Insurance Coverage and NHS Provision

Under the Affordable Care Act, most US health insurance plans cover at least one breast pump per pregnancy. The Medela Pump In Style with MaxFlow is on the covered formulary of most major US insurers — Aetna, BlueCross BlueShield, UnitedHealthcare, Cigna, and others. Check with your insurer's durable medical equipment supplier (Aeroflow Breastpumps is the most commonly used) before purchasing out of pocket. You may receive the MaxFlow at zero cost.

In the UK, the NHS provides access to breast pump information through midwives and health visitors. Hospital-grade pump rental is available through NICU units and some community lactation services for babies with feeding difficulties. For personal daily pumping, the NHS does not typically provide consumer pumps; UK parents purchase independently.

For the parent returning to work and pumping in a conference room or office bathroom, the Medela kit is genuinely the most work-ready option available.

## The Open System Issue

The Medela Pump In Style MaxFlow is an open-system pump. This means there is no physical barrier between the milk collection area and the motor. Milk and moisture can theoretically travel through the tubing toward the motor during sessions. In practice, Medela's tubing is designed to prevent this, but if the pump is stored with wet tubing, moisture can accumulate.

The Spectra S2 and S1 are closed-system pumps with a backflow protector that physically blocks milk from entering the motor. This makes Spectra pumps safe to share between users (with new collection kits) and more hygienically robust for long-term use. For a single-user pump used for one child, the Medela's open system is unlikely to cause problems. For pumping over multiple pregnancies or sharing with a partner or friend, a closed system is better.

## Comparing to the Spectra S2

The Spectra S2 (our highest-rated breast pump at 9.1/10) scores slightly higher than the Medela MaxFlow (8.8/10) for three reasons: its closed system, its quieter operation (45 dB vs. 55 dB for the Medela), and its independent suction and cycle speed controls. The Spectra lets you set suction level and cycle speed completely independently — you can have high suction with slow cycles, or low suction with fast cycles. The Medela links these settings through predefined speed-suction combinations.

Where the Medela wins: the included work bag, the PersonalFit Flex connector flexibility, the brand ecosystem (parts available everywhere, from Target to hospital gift shops), and the broader insurance formulary presence for some plans.

## Long-Term Ownership Notes

The parts that wear first on the Medela MaxFlow are the membranes (small white silicone pieces on the valves). These should be replaced every 2–3 months for daily pumping users. Suction decline that appears to be a pump problem is usually membrane wear. Replacement membranes are widely available, inexpensive, and take 30 seconds to swap.

The tubing should be replaced if any milk or cloudiness appears inside. The tubing itself does not contact milk during normal use, but visible contamination is a sign to replace. Replacement Medela tubing is available everywhere, which is the biggest practical advantage of the Medela ecosystem: you can find parts at Walmart, Target, or the nearest drugstore. Spectra replacement parts require online ordering or specialty baby stores.

## Cleaning in Practice

Medela recommends washing all parts that contact milk after every session in hot soapy water. The FDA recommends hand-washing pump parts and allowing them to air dry on a clean paper towel rather than using a diaper changing surface. The dishwasher degrades silicone membranes faster than hand washing — use the dishwasher for occasional deep cleans, not daily.

Medela's Quick Clean Wipes (sold separately) are useful for cleaning parts at work when access to a sink is limited. They are not a substitute for full washing but maintain hygiene between sessions.

## Who Should Not Buy the Medela Pump In Style

Parents whose insurance covers the Spectra S2 at zero cost should verify before paying $199 for the Medela. If both are covered, the Spectra's closed system and quieter operation give it an edge for home use. The Medela's work-ready kit advantage matters primarily if you are pumping at an office — and even then, the Spectra plus a separately purchased Medela bag ($30) is competitive.

Parents who need maximum discretion at work should look at the Elvie Stride ($379, often covered by insurance) or Willow 3.0 ($499). Neither of those are as loud as a traditional pump; the Medela's 55 dB is noticeable in a quiet office.
