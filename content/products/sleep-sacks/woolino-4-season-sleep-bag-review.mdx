## What Merino Wool Actually Does

Merino wool's temperature-regulating properties come from the fiber's physical structure. Merino fibers are naturally crimped at a micro scale, creating tiny air pockets that trap warmth when temperatures drop. The fiber is also hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture vapor (from a baby's skin and the surrounding air) and releases it, which manages the microclimate around the baby's body actively rather than passively.

In practical terms: when your baby is sleeping in a 62°F room in January, the Woolino 4-Season Baby Sleep Bag holds warmth without the baby needing an additional layer. When that same room warms to 72°F by morning, the wool's moisture management prevents the overheating that a fixed-TOG synthetic sack would cause. You do not need to get up and switch your baby to a lighter sack at 5 AM.

This is fundamentally different from how cotton sleep sacks work. Cotton is breathable but passive — it does not actively respond to temperature change. A cotton sleep sack rated at TOG 1.5 is TOG 1.5 whether the room is 60°F or 75°F. The Woolino's effective warmth level adjusts with conditions, which is what "self-regulating" means in concrete terms.

The working range is 61–79°F. Within that range, the Woolino 4-Season Sleep Bag handles seasonal variation without intervention. Below 61°F, supplemental layering (a warmer footed pajama underneath) is needed. Above 79°F, the merino's warmth retention becomes a liability rather than an asset, and a lighter product is more appropriate.

## GOTS and OEKO-TEX Certifications

Both certifications appear on the Woolino 4-Season Baby Sleep Bag and are worth understanding because they address different concerns.

GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard) certifies that the merino wool is organic throughout the production chain — no synthetic pesticides on the sheep, no harmful chemicals in processing, environmentally responsible manufacturing. For parents concerned about what goes against a newborn's skin, this is meaningful.

OEKO-TEX Standard 100 certification tests the finished product for harmful substances — heavy metals, pesticide residues, formaldehyde, allergenic dyes. Even if a fiber starts organic, processing can introduce contaminants; OEKO-TEX tests what is actually in the fabric after manufacturing.

Together, these certifications mean the Woolino is among the most rigorously vetted sleep sacks on the market from a materials safety standpoint. The HALO SleepSack Swaddle and most cotton competitors do not carry both certifications. This matters especially for babies with eczema or sensitive skin — superfine merino (under 18.5 microns) is softer than most synthetic and cotton alternatives and less likely to irritate.

## Temperature Management in Everyday Use

The most consistent parent concern with the Woolino is whether to add a layer underneath. Here is the practical guidance:

For rooms at 61–65°F, dress the baby in a long-sleeve footed onesie or two-piece pajamas underneath the Woolino. The merino will manage the warm end of that range but needs help at the cold end.

For rooms at 66–72°F — the most common range for climate-controlled homes — a short-sleeve onesie or long-sleeve single-layer pajama is typically right. This is where the self-regulation shows its value most clearly: the wool handles the overnight temperature drift without you needing to intervene.

For rooms at 73–79°F, dress the baby in just a short-sleeve bodysuit. In hot weather, watch the nape of the neck and the chest for sweat — those are earlier indicators of overheating than flushed cheeks.

Above 79°F, a lighter alternative is appropriate. The Nanit Pro baby monitor, if you are using one for sleep tracking, can help you correlate room temperature data with your baby's sleep quality and identify patterns across seasons — useful context for dialing in the Woolino's optimal use range.

The Hatch Rest 2nd Gen serves a complementary role: its room temperature display and customizable nightlight can be set to alert you if the room drops below or rises above your target range, which takes the guesswork out of overnight temperature shifts.

## Washing and Care: The Real Talk

The con about gentle washing is understated in the specs. Merino wool requires consistent, careful care, and that discipline is non-negotiable over a two-year product lifespan.

Wash on gentle cycle in cold water only. Use a wool-specific detergent — standard laundry detergent strips the lanolin from the wool fiber, which degrades the temperature-regulation and softness over time. Lay flat to dry or tumble dry on the lowest heat setting (many parents skip the dryer entirely for merino). Never wring or twist the wet fabric.

Parents who are diligent about these steps report that the Woolino maintains its softness and performance through the full two years. Parents who put it through hot cycles or standard detergent start seeing pilling and fabric texture degradation within a few months. The care requirement is a real lifestyle consideration — if your household laundry system does not have room for a gentle-cycle, air-dry cadence on baby items, this may not be the right product.

One practical note: buy two. The Woolino at $109 is expensive, but having a backup while one is being washed and dried (which takes longer than a standard cotton sack) prevents the scenario where you're putting a wet sleep sack on a tired baby at 7 PM because the one sack is still in the dryer.
