The **Solly Baby Wrap** has achieved a devoted following among babywearing parents, particularly for the newborn stage — and the TENCEL modal fabric is the reason why.

## What TENCEL Modal Does Differently

Most baby wraps are cotton or cotton-blend. Solly uses TENCEL modal — a sustainably sourced fiber derived from beechwood that is noticeably softer, lighter, and more breathable than cotton. The result is a wrap that weighs practically nothing, breathes well in warm weather, and feels gentle against newborn skin that's still adjusting to the outside world.

## The Newborn Advantage

Wraps are uniquely suited to the newborn stage. The continuous fabric allows you to position a newborn in the curled fetal position — knees drawn up, back rounded — which is the most natural comfortable position for babies who spent nine months curled in the womb. Structured carriers typically can't achieve this same ergonomic position for very young babies.

## Carrying for Hours

With correct technique, the Solly distributes baby's weight evenly across both shoulders and your entire back. Many parents wear it for 2–4 hours at a stretch. The key is achieving even tension throughout the wrap — wrapping tutorials emphasize this.

## The Learning Curve

There is a learning curve. The first time you try to wrap, it feels like handling an impractical amount of fabric. After 2–3 practice sessions, it becomes intuitive. Solly's video tutorials are genuinely helpful, and many local babywearing groups offer free wrapping assistance.

## When to Transition

At around 5–6 months, when baby begins to sit and wants to see outward, most parents transition to a structured carrier (like the LILLEbaby Complete Airflow or Ergobaby Omni 360) that supports an outward-facing position.

## Safety Standards & Certifications

Soft baby carriers and wraps should meet **ASTM F2236** and are independently verified by the **JPMA**. The key safety principle is the **TICKS rule**: Tight, In view at all times, Close enough to kiss, Keep chin off chest, Supported back. Carriers that allow a chin-to-chest position are a suffocation risk for newborns.
