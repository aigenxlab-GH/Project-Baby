## What a Stretchy Wrap Actually Is (and Isn't)

Before getting into the Solly specifically, it helps to understand where it sits among carrier types. Baby carriers fall into three main categories: soft structured carriers (SSCs) like the Ergobaby Omni 360 or LILLEbaby Complete Airflow, which have buckles, padded waistbelts, and a rigid seat panel; ring slings, which use a pair of metal rings to adjust a single piece of fabric; and stretchy wraps like the Solly, which are a single length of fabric — no hardware at all.

Stretchy wraps are uniquely suited to the newborn stage because the fabric conforms to the baby's body rather than asking the baby to fit into a pre-shaped seat. A newborn can be positioned in the rounded fetal curl they were in for nine months — knees drawn up higher than the bottom, back slightly rounded, cheek resting against your chest. A structured carrier technically works from birth, but the rigid seat panel and adjustable waist system never quite achieve that same molded, held quality.

The trade-off is that stretchy wraps require learning — and they have a shorter useful lifespan. Once your baby is pushing 20 lbs and wants to see the world, you'll want a structured carrier. The Solly is a newborn specialist, and it excels at being exactly that.

## What TENCEL Modal Does Differently

Most baby wraps are 100% cotton — the Moby Wrap Original is the classic example — or a cotton-spandex blend like the Boba Wrap. Solly uses TENCEL modal: a sustainably sourced fiber derived from beechwood pulp that is genuinely softer, lighter, and more breathable than cotton.

Hold a Solly next to a cotton wrap and the difference is immediate. The TENCEL fabric has a slight natural sheen and drapes differently — softer, more fluid. Against newborn skin that is still adjusting to the outside world after nine months of amniotic fluid, this matters. Babies with sensitive skin respond well to TENCEL modal where cotton can feel slightly scratchy in comparison.

The breathability is the other meaningful difference. The fabric doesn't trap heat the way cotton does. In summer use or in warm indoor environments, cotton wraps can create a heat pocket against both your chest and the baby's back. The Solly's TENCEL construction allows much better airflow. It is the most comfortable warm-weather wrap available.

The material composition is 47% TENCEL, 47% cotton, and 6% spandex. The spandex gives it enough stretch to allow the wrapping technique without the elastic fatigue that higher-spandex blends can develop over time. The 6% is the right amount — enough to work with, not enough to cause the sag issues that affect cheap stretchy wraps.

## The M-Position and Why It Matters for Hip Health

Every carrier review mentions the M-position. It is worth being specific about what it actually means and why it matters, because the consequences of getting it wrong are genuinely significant.

The M-position — also called the frog-leg squat or the ergonomic position — requires the baby's knees to be higher than their bottom, with their legs spread around the carrier. When viewed from the front, the bent legs form the shape of an M. This position recreates the natural squat posture that babies maintain in the womb and positions the femoral head (the ball at the top of the thighbone) correctly within the hip socket.

Carriers that allow the baby's legs to hang straight down create a different hip geometry — the femoral head is pulled down and away from the socket rather than seated firmly within it. Over extended carries in this position, particularly in the first months when hip cartilage is still soft and developing, there is a genuine risk of contributing to hip dysplasia.

The International Hip Dysplasia Institute (IHDI) certifies carriers that achieve correct M-position. The Solly's wrapping technique, when done correctly, positions the baby in a deep M-position naturally — the fabric cradles the knees upward and inward. Solly's instructional videos demonstrate this clearly, and it is one area where getting a fit check from a certified babywearing consultant is genuinely worthwhile for first-time wrappers.

## The Newborn Advantage

Wraps are uniquely suited to the newborn stage for reasons beyond the M-position. The continuous fabric allows you to position a newborn in the full curled fetal position — back rounded, chin slightly tucked, body completely supported against your chest. Structured carriers technically work from birth, but their rigid seat panels and predefined proportions never quite achieve this same molded quality.

There is also the proximity factor. In a structured carrier, there is some distance between your body and the baby's. In a correctly worn stretchy wrap, the baby is directly against your chest — you feel every breath, every hiccup, every snuggle. For the first six weeks especially, this contact matters for both the baby's nervous system regulation and for nursing mothers working on milk supply and bonding.

Many parents with fussy or colicky newborns find the wrap to be the only thing that reliably settles them. The combination of warmth, movement, heartbeat proximity, and the contained feeling replicates the womb environment in a way that no other product achieves.

## The Weight Distribution Reality

The most important thing to understand about wearing a wrap correctly is that the weight distribution is determined almost entirely by your technique, not the carrier itself. Done correctly — with consistent tension across all layers, no twisted fabric, and the baby sitting in the deepest part of the pocket — the Solly distributes baby's weight across both shoulders and your entire back.

Many parents wear it for 2–4 hours at a stretch with a young baby (under 15 lbs) without back pain. The key is that the weight is spread across a much larger surface area than any buckle-based carrier can achieve, because the fabric covers your entire torso.

Done incorrectly — fabric too loose, baby sitting too low, tension uneven between layers — you carry most of the weight through the middle of your back, which causes pain within 20 minutes. This is why the learning curve matters. It is not about aesthetics; it is about whether the carry is actually functional.

## How to Learn the Solly (and How Long It Actually Takes)

The first time you try to wrap a Solly is always chaotic. Five meters of fabric, a wriggling or sleeping baby, and an instructional video on your phone propped against the bathroom mirror. This is normal and universal.

The basic newborn carry — Front Wrap Cross Carry — takes most parents 2–3 practice sessions to feel confident with, and 5–6 sessions to do without thinking. Solly's own YouTube channel has the clearest tutorials. Practice first with a stuffed animal or doll before using with your actual newborn — the muscle memory is the important part.

Specific tips that make the learning curve shorter: practice in front of a full-length mirror so you can see the back of the seat; start with the fabric pre-tied and cold, then practice putting the baby in and out without retying; recruit another adult for the first few live tries so you have a second pair of hands if needed.

Local babywearing groups offer free one-on-one fitting assistance and are listed through the Babywearing International directory. A 30-minute in-person session is worth more than three hours of video watching. Solly also offers virtual consultations.

## Wearing Safety: The TICKS Rule

With any wrap carrier, particularly for newborns, the TICKS safety principles govern every wear:

**T — Tight:** The fabric should be snug against your body with no slack. A baby in a too-loose carry can slump.

**I — In view at all times:** You should be able to see the baby's face at all times by simply looking down. Never cover the baby's face with the fabric.

**C — Close enough to kiss:** The baby's head should be close enough to your face that you can lean down and kiss their head without stretching.

**K — Keep chin off chest:** The baby's chin should not be pressed against their chest. A chin-to-chest position can restrict the airway. If you see the baby's chin touching their chest, adjust the carry immediately.

**S — Supported back:** The baby's back should be supported in its natural position — slightly rounded for a newborn. It should not be hanging or arched.

## When to Transition Out

At around 5–6 months, when baby begins pushing up, wanting to see outward, and approaching the 18–20 lb mark, the stretchy wrap becomes less practical. The Solly is rated to 25 lbs, but the honest transition point is 18–20 lbs — the stretchiness that makes wrapping easy in the newborn stage starts to create sag with a heavier baby.

The natural successor is a soft structured carrier with an outward-facing position. The LILLEbaby Complete Airflow offers six carry positions including front-facing outward and back carry, and works from 7 lbs without an insert — some parents use the Solly for 0–5 months and transition to the LILLEbaby at 5 months. The Ergobaby Omni 360 is another strong option, particularly if you want the most ergonomic seat panel and lumbar support for longer carries with a heavier baby.

## The Solly vs. Moby Wrap vs. Boba Wrap

The three most-compared stretchy wraps are the Solly, Moby, and Boba. All three do the same fundamental job. The differences are real but not dramatic:

**Moby Wrap Original** ($45): 100% cotton, heavier fabric, the most common reference point for stretchy wraps. Slightly more structured feel than the Solly. Many parents find the cotton fabric grippier when adjusting tension. Requires a pre-wash before first use.

**Boba Wrap** ($40): Cotton-spandex blend, pre-washed, wider color range. The spandex content is slightly higher than Solly's, which gives more stretch recovery. Marginally less soft than the Solly against newborn skin.

**Solly Baby Wrap** ($78): TENCEL modal blend, noticeably softer and lighter, most breathable of the three. The premium is the fabric experience — if your baby has sensitive skin or you're wrapping in warm weather, the Solly's TENCEL construction is the genuine differentiator. At nearly double the price of the Moby, it is a luxury wrap rather than a budget carrier.

## Caring for the Solly

Machine wash on cold, gentle cycle. Lay flat or hang to dry — tumble drying causes shrinkage over time in the TENCEL modal fibers. The fabric will soften further with each wash. Do not use fabric softener — it coats the fibers and reduces breathability over time. The Solly's pattern and color hold well through repeated washing; fading is minimal with cold wash.

Because the Solly holds up well over time, it has decent resale value — particularly the solid neutral colorways (cream, navy, sage). If you're considering buying secondhand, check for fabric integrity at the stress points (the crossing points on the back, the seat area) and ensure even stretch across the full length.

## Who Should Not Buy the Solly

If you are primarily looking for a carrier that will work from newborn through toddlerhood without buying a second product, the Solly is not it. It excels in the newborn-to-5-month window and then you will need a structured carrier. If your budget only allows one carrier purchase, the LILLEbaby Complete Airflow or Ergobaby Omni 360 covers a much longer age range.

If you are unwilling to invest time in learning the wrapping technique, a structured carrier is easier to use from the first session. The Infantino Flip 4-in-1 at $30 is the most accessible entry-level structured carrier for parents who want functionality without a learning curve.

If you will primarily carry in cold weather, a TENCEL modal wrap will feel cooler than cotton. Some parents layer a thin cardigan over the baby inside the wrap in cooler temperatures, but if you're in a cold climate year-round, a structured carrier with a covering panel works better.

## Safety Standards

Soft baby carriers and wraps should meet **ASTM F2236** safety standards and may be independently verified by the **JPMA**. The key safety principle remains the TICKS rule above. Carriers that allow a chin-to-chest position are a suffocation risk for newborns — this is the most important safety check with any wrap.

## How It Compares

Against the Ergobaby Omni 360 at the same price tier, the comparison is almost a false one — they serve different stages. The Solly is the best newborn carrier available. The Omni 360 is the best newborn-to-toddler structured carrier. Many parents own both and use the Solly for the first 4 months before transitioning. If you can only buy one, the Omni 360 covers a longer period; if you have the budget for the newborn specialist, the Solly's TENCEL fabric makes those first months dramatically more comfortable for both of you.

Against the BabyBjörn One Air at $250, the comparison is even clearer: the One Air is a structured carrier for 7 lbs to 33 lbs with four carry positions. It is not competing in the stretchy wrap category at all. For parents who want the best structured carrier for warm climates, the One Air is a legitimate choice; it simply doesn't replicate what the Solly does in the newborn phase.

## Parent Tips

- Practice the wrap technique before your due date. A stuffed animal of comparable newborn weight (7–8 lbs) works well for muscle memory.
- Find your local babywearing group through the Babywearing International directory. One in-person fitting session is more valuable than watching tutorials for hours.
- Pre-tie the wrap before the baby wakes for a feed. Having the wrap pre-formed on your body means you can put baby in without fumbling with fabric at 2am.
- Plan for the transition: at around 4–5 months, start researching your next carrier so you're not scrambling when the Solly stops working for your baby's size and stage.
- Cold wash only, hang to dry. Tumble drying will shrink the TENCEL fibers over time.
