Every high chair review eventually comes back to the IKEA Antilop — the $25 plastic chair that has outlasted, outsold, and out-performed chairs costing ten times as much.

It's not perfect. But in the world of baby gear where products routinely charge a premium for marginal benefits, the Antilop is a genuine anomaly: simple, brilliant design at an impossible price.

## Why It Works: The Design Philosophy

Most high chairs are packed with features: multiple height settings, recline positions, trays that expand and contract, cushions with removable covers, footrests that adjust, frames that fold. Each feature adds complexity — and complexity means more places for food to hide.

The Antilop strips everything back to what actually matters: a stable seat at table height, a secure harness, and surfaces you can clean in 30 seconds. The entire chair can be disassembled into three pieces (seat, tray, and four legs) that fit in your dishwasher.

## Cleaning: The Killer Feature

Ask any parent who's owned an Antilop and they'll tell you the same thing: cleaning it is genuinely satisfying. There are no crevices, no foam padding that absorbs puréed carrot, no folds of fabric that hide yesterday's lunch.

Wipe the seat, rinse the tray, put both in the dishwasher if needed. Done in 60 seconds.

Compare this to the 15-minute cleaning ordeal of most high chairs with multiple crevices, fabric inserts, and harness pieces that need to come apart. Over 2–3 years of daily meals, the time saved is significant.

## Safety

Despite its simplicity, the Antilop is extremely safe. The low centre of gravity makes it remarkably stable — it's very difficult to tip. The 3-point harness is secure and easy to buckle and release. The seat shape keeps babies in a good position.

IKEA's global safety testing standards for children's products are rigorous — this chair meets all EU and US safety requirements.

## The Limitations

**No height adjustment** is the most common complaint. The Antilop fits well at standard dining table height but may be too high or too low for some tables. Measure before buying if you're unsure.

**No padding** means you'll want a cushion for longer meals — particularly for younger babies with less body mass. The IKEA PYTTIG cushion ($4) solves this.

**The legs don't fold** — this isn't a travelling chair, and storage requires the legs to be removed.

**The tray is basic** — smaller than most high chair trays and without a built-in lip to catch rolling food. Many parents use the chair directly at the table without the tray.

## Who Should Buy One

Everyone who has a baby approaching solid foods (around 6 months). Even if you have a fancier high chair, an Antilop as a secondary chair — for grandparents' house, the kitchen when the dining room chair is in use, travel to self-catering accommodation — is worth every dollar.

For families on a budget, it's the primary recommendation. The money you save can go toward things that make more difference — quality food, more activities, or a better mattress.

## Safety Standards & Certifications

High chairs must meet **ASTM F404**, which covers stability, restraint systems, tray security, and folding/reclining mechanisms. **JPMA-certified** high chairs carry additional independent safety verification. Always use the provided 5-point harness — never the tray alone as a restraint. Check the base footprint for stability before purchasing for hard floors.

For the **IKEA Antilop High Chair** specifically: fits at most standard tables without a separate tray, which is independently verified. Our 8.8/10 score factors in full safety compliance.
