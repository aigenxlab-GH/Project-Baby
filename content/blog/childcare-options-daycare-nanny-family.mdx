# Childcare Options: Nursery, Childminder, Nanny, and Family Care Compared

Choosing childcare is one of the most significant decisions parents make in the first years of a child's life — and one of the most expensive. The decision involves cost, practicality, your child's temperament, your working pattern, and a genuine assessment of quality that goes well beyond Ofsted ratings or glossy brochures.

This guide covers the main options available in the UK, how each is regulated, what quality actually looks like in practice, what things cost in 2026, and what government support is available. The aim is to help parents make an informed choice rather than a rushed one.

## The Main Options and How They Are Regulated

### Nursery (Day Nursery or Day Care Centre)

Nurseries provide group care for children from birth to five years. They are required to register with Ofsted (the Office for Standards in Education) in England, and are inspected against the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) framework. Nurseries receive an Ofsted rating of Outstanding, Good, Requires Improvement, or Inadequate.

Most nurseries operate term-time or year-round, typically from 7:30am to 6pm on weekdays. They employ qualified early years practitioners and are required to meet minimum staff-to-child ratios: 1:3 for babies under 2, 1:4 for children aged 2, and 1:8 for children aged 3 and over. Many nurseries exceed these ratios.

Group care provides children with consistent peer interaction and exposure to structured activities, meals, and routines. For children aged 2 and older in particular, the social environment of a well-run nursery can strongly support language and social development.

### Childminder

A childminder is a self-employed person who provides care in their own home. They are also Ofsted registered and inspected in England. The typical group size is small — usually 3 to 6 children including their own — and the environment is more domestic in character than a nursery.

Childminders often offer more flexible hours than nurseries and may be more willing to accommodate irregular working patterns. Because the setting is home-based and the group is small, the experience can feel closer to family care — particularly for babies under 12 months who may find large group settings more unsettling.

The NICE guideline on social and emotional wellbeing in early years (PH40) notes that stable, responsive relationships with known carers are particularly important in the first two years of life. Small group size makes this more achievable in a childminding setting.

### Nanny

A nanny provides care in the family's own home, either for one family or — increasingly — as a nanny share between two families. Nannies are not required to hold an Ofsted registration unless they join the Ofsted Voluntary Childcare Register, which some families request in order to access the Tax-Free Childcare scheme. This means nannies are not subject to regular inspection in the same way as nurseries and childminders.

A nanny can follow the family's own routines, care for multiple children simultaneously, manage school runs for older siblings, and provide genuine flexibility around working hours. The one-to-one or one-to-two ratio provides individualised attention that group settings cannot match.

The significant practical challenge is cost. A nanny is not just a wage — the family becomes an employer, responsible for PAYE, National Insurance contributions, pension enrolment, and potentially paid sick leave and holiday. An agency fee for placement is also common. The true annual cost significantly exceeds the salary figure alone.

### Nanny Share

A nanny share involves two families hiring the same nanny, who cares for both families' children together — usually in one family's home on alternating weeks, or in a fixed location. This reduces the cost for each family while maintaining a low ratio of children to carer. Nanny shares require a clear written agreement between the families about logistics, expectations, and what happens if one family's needs change.

### Family Care: Grandparents, Relatives, and Informal Arrangements

Care provided by grandparents, aunts, uncles, or other relatives is not regulated by Ofsted. It typically has no formal inspection, no minimum qualification requirements, and no legal structure. This does not make it a worse option — a warm, attentive grandparent who knows the child and the family well provides a form of care that no regulated setting can replicate.

The practical considerations are different. Relatives providing regular substantial care should be supported to understand current safe sleeping guidance (NHS), up-to-date weaning guidance, and any health needs the child has. A written understanding — not necessarily a formal contract, but a clear conversation — about hours, cancellation, and expectations reduces the risk of arrangements breaking down.

Family care also has implications for the relative's well-being. Regular childcare can be physically demanding, and grandparents in particular should feel able to set limits on what they can manage.

## The Cost Reality in 2026

Childcare in the UK is among the most expensive in the OECD, and costs have continued to rise. The following are approximate 2026 averages for full-time care in England, and regional variation is significant (London costs are typically 20 to 30 percent higher):

- **Nursery for a child under 2**: approximately £15,000 to £20,000 per year for full-time (50 weeks, 40+ hours per week)
- **Childminder**: approximately £10,000 to £14,000 per year for full-time
- **Nanny (salary only)**: approximately £26,000 to £35,000 gross, plus employer on-costs (National Insurance, pension) of approximately £3,000 to £5,000 per year, making the total annual cost approximately £30,000 to £40,000+
- **Family care**: varies enormously; often informal and unpaid, or paid at a rate agreed between the family and the relative

## Government Funding and Support

### Free Early Education Hours

From September 2024, the UK government expanded free childcare entitlement in England significantly:

- **9 months to 3 years** (working parents): 15 hours per week of free childcare (expanded from 3 years)
- **3 to 4 years** (all families): 15 hours per week free
- **3 to 4 years** (working parents): 30 hours per week free (extended hours)

These hours are funded by the government and delivered through registered providers — nurseries, childminders on the Ofsted register, and some nannies on the Voluntary Register. Check current eligibility requirements and rates on the gov.uk childcare choices calculator, as rates and eligibility criteria continue to evolve.

### Tax-Free Childcare

Tax-Free Childcare allows eligible working parents to receive £2 for every £8 they spend on childcare, up to a maximum government contribution of £2,000 per child per year (or £4,000 for disabled children). The account is opened on the gov.uk website and payments must be made to registered providers.

Tax-Free Childcare and Universal Credit childcare support cannot be used simultaneously. Parents should check both options to determine which provides greater benefit given their circumstances.

## What Quality Actually Looks Like

Ofsted ratings are a useful starting point but not a sufficient guide to quality. A nursery rated Good four years ago may have had significant staff turnover since. A nursery rated Requires Improvement may be actively improving with strong leadership.

The research evidence on early years quality consistently identifies the same factors as most predictive of good outcomes for children:

**The warmth and responsiveness of adult-child interaction.** This is more important than the physical environment, the equipment, or the curriculum. A member of staff who gets down to a child's level, responds to their communication, uses their name, and engages with their interests is providing high-quality care. Staff who are present physically but not emotionally — scrolling phones, talking among themselves, moving children through activities without engaging with them — are not, regardless of the setting's appearance.

**The key person system.** Good nurseries and childminders assign a specific person to each child who takes primary responsibility for their care, learning, and communication with the family. The key person knows the child's interests, development, and family background, and is the person the child looks to for comfort. This relationship is central to secure settling and healthy social development. Ask specifically about the key person system at any setting you visit.

**How staff respond to distress.** Watch what happens when a child is upset during your visit. Is the response immediate and warm? Does the adult physically comfort the child, use their name, acknowledge their feelings? Or is the response to redirect and move on? The response to distress is the most revealing single observation you can make about a setting's emotional climate.

**Staff retention and stability.** High staff turnover undermines the key person relationship and the consistency that young children need. It is reasonable to ask how long the current team has been in post.

## What to Look for on a Nursery or Childminder Visit

Go beyond the tour. Ask to spend time in the room where your child would be cared for. Observe interactions, not just the physical environment.

Questions worth asking:

- How is the key person system structured in practice?
- How do you manage the settling-in period? What does a typical settling-in look like?
- What is your sickness policy — when can a child not attend, and what is the notice period for calling them sick?
- How do you communicate daily with parents about the child's day?
- What is your staff turnover, and how long has the current team been in post?
- What is the policy on mobile phones and screens for staff during working hours?
- How do you manage behaviour?

Ask also to see the most recent Ofsted report — but treat it as one data point alongside what you observe, rather than the defining factor.

## The Settling-In Period

Gradual settling-in — short visits that increase progressively before the first full day — is supported by the research on attachment and stress response in young children. Rushing the settling-in process because a start date is imminent does not work in the child's favour and typically prolongs the overall adjustment.

Most children settle into a new childcare setting within 2 to 6 weeks. The first few weeks often involve upset at drop-off, even for children who are happy once a parent has left. This is a normal expression of healthy attachment, not a sign that the setting is wrong for the child. A good setting will communicate with parents during the day and will be honest about how the child is really doing.

If a child continues to show significant distress at drop-off beyond 6 to 8 weeks — not just a brief upset but prolonged inconsolable crying or regression in development — it is worth reviewing whether the setting is meeting their needs, and whether a change of key person or setting might be appropriate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What age is best to start nursery?**

There is no single right age. The research on group childcare and outcomes shows that high-quality provision from around 2 to 3 years supports language and social development. For babies under 12 months, the quality of the individual relationship with a known carer matters more than the type of setting — a childminder with a small group or a nanny may be a better fit than a large nursery for a very young baby. The key factor at any age is quality of care, not the age of starting.

**Is an Outstanding Ofsted rating a reliable guide to quality?**

It is a useful signal but not a guarantee. Ofsted inspections are periodic — nurseries are not inspected constantly — and a setting's quality can change significantly between inspections, particularly if there has been leadership or staff change. An Outstanding rating from three years ago tells you something about the setting at the time of inspection. Visit in person, observe the environment and interactions, and supplement the rating with your own assessment.

**Can a nanny be paid informally, in cash?**

No. A nanny employed on a regular basis is a legal employee, and the family is their employer. This means PAYE registration with HMRC, deducting and paying income tax and National Insurance, and enrolling the nanny in a workplace pension scheme. Paying informally — in cash, without payroll — is illegal for both parties and carries significant financial risk if the arrangement is later challenged by HMRC or if the nanny makes an employment claim. A payroll service (there are several specifically for household employers) makes this straightforward and relatively inexpensive.

**How do I know if the government free hours apply to my child?**

Eligibility for the expanded free childcare hours (from 9 months for working parents) depends on both parents working at least 16 hours per week and earning above a minimum threshold, with certain exceptions. The gov.uk childcare choices calculator provides personalised eligibility guidance. Eligibility must be confirmed each term, and the hours must be used through a registered provider.

**My child cries every day at drop-off. Should I change settings?**

Not necessarily. Drop-off upset is common in the first weeks of starting childcare and does not, in itself, indicate a problem with the setting. The relevant question is: how is the child once the parent has left? If the setting reports that the child settles quickly, plays happily, eats well, and is comfortable with their key person, the drop-off distress is most likely a normal expression of the transition rather than unhappiness in the setting. If distress is prolonged, if the child is not settling or eating, and if the pattern continues beyond 6 to 8 weeks, that warrants a closer look.

## Key Takeaways

- The main regulated options in the UK are nurseries and childminders, both Ofsted registered and inspected. Nannies are not subject to mandatory Ofsted registration or inspection.
- Full-time nursery for a child under 2 costs approximately £15,000 to £20,000 per year in 2026; childminders approximately £10,000 to £14,000; nannies approximately £30,000 to £40,000 including employer on-costs.
- Government funding expanded from September 2024: working parents with children from 9 months can access 15 free hours per week; 30 hours for working parents of 3 to 4 year olds.
- Quality is best assessed by observing adult-child interactions during a visit — warmth, responsiveness, and how staff respond to distress are more predictive of outcomes than physical environment or Ofsted rating alone.
- The key person system — a named adult who takes primary responsibility for a child's care and development — is central to quality in any group setting.
- Gradual settling-in takes 2 to 6 weeks on average and is supported by the research on attachment; rushing the process typically prolongs distress rather than reducing it.
