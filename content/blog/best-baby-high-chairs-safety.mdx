# Best High Chairs 2026: Safety Standards, Top Picks, and What to Avoid

A high chair seems straightforward — it is a seat that gets the baby to table height. But the differences between models matter more than the marketing suggests. A poorly designed harness can allow a baby to slip down and become trapped. A seat with fabric padding looks comfortable but becomes a fermentation vessel for pureed carrot within a week. A chair without a footrest may seem like a minor omission, but it undermines the stability and posture that allows a young child to actually focus on eating.

This guide covers when to start using a high chair, what the safety standards require, which features actually matter, and where the main models sit against each other.

## When to Start Using a High Chair

The standard guidance is around six months, when a baby can sit with some support and when solid foods are typically introduced. The NHS and the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) both recommend starting solids at around six months. A baby who cannot yet maintain a seated position — even with support — is not developmentally ready for solid foods, and a high chair does not change that.

Some high chairs include a recline feature that allows younger babies to use the chair in a more laid-back position. This can be useful for babies who are on the earlier end of weaning, typically around five months on medical advice, but it should not be used to force feeding readiness before the baby is ready.

## Safety Standards to Check

In the UK, the relevant standard is **BS EN 14988**, which covers high chairs for domestic use. A chair that meets this standard has been tested for structural stability, harness strength, and potential entrapment hazards. Look for this marking on the packaging or product listing.

In the US, the relevant standard is **ASTM F404**, developed by ASTM International and administered with certification through the Juvenile Products Manufacturers Association (JPMA). A JPMA-certified high chair carries a seal indicating it has been independently tested against this standard.

Neither standard is a guarantee of perfect safety in all circumstances — they are minimum requirements. User behaviour, correct harness use, and the condition of the chair all affect real-world safety.

### The Harness Question

A 5-point harness — two shoulder straps, two hip straps, and one crotch strap, all meeting at a single buckle — is the correct harness for a high chair. A lap-only harness (two hip straps meeting at a crotch strap) allows a baby to lean forward and slide through the seat under the tray. Babies are surprisingly good at this and it can lead to entrapment. Avoid any chair with a lap-only harness for babies under twelve months.

## Key Features That Actually Matter

### Easy to Clean

This is the feature that parents who have owned a high chair consistently wish they had prioritised more. Food gets everywhere — in the crevices of padded seats, under the tray, in the hinge mechanisms. A wipe-clean hard plastic seat is dramatically easier to maintain than a padded fabric seat. If you want padding for comfort, look for a separately sold cushion that can be removed and machine washed, rather than a fixed fabric cover.

### Footrest

A footrest is not a luxury. When a child's feet are dangling, they use core strength to stabilise themselves, which competes with the concentration needed for eating. A footrest that can be placed at the right height for the child's leg length allows the child to sit with hips at 90 degrees, knees at 90 degrees, feet flat — a stable base that supports independent eating. Adjustable footrests are more useful than fixed ones as the child grows.

### Removable Tray

The tray should come off with one hand, reliably, so you can move the chair to the table without removing the baby. Trays that require two hands to remove are impractical when one hand is holding a baby. Trays that fit into the dishwasher are easier to clean than those that require manual scrubbing.

### Adjustable Height

If you eat at a table, the high chair should adjust so the tray sits at table height. This allows the baby to participate in meals rather than sitting at a different level, which matters both for feeding development and for family mealtimes.

### Recline for Younger Babies

A recline of approximately 30 degrees is useful for babies in the earliest weaning stages who do not yet have full trunk control. The chair should not recline to horizontal — that position is not appropriate for feeding solids.

### Compact and Foldable Options

Standard high chairs have a large footprint. In a small kitchen or dining room, a chair that folds flat for storage between meals can be important. The IKEA Antilop does not fold but is light enough to move easily. Some chairs fold to a relatively thin profile.

## Types of High Chair

### Traditional High Chair

The most common type. A full-height chair with legs, a seat, a tray, and a harness. Suitable from approximately six months. Takes up floor space but offers the most features. The IKEA Antilop and Tripp Trapp are both in this category.

### Space-Saving Clip-On Chair

A chair that clamps to the edge of a table rather than having its own legs. The Inglesina Fast Table Chair is the best-known example. These are lightweight and fold small, making them good for travel and eating out. They require a table of appropriate thickness and material to clamp safely — they cannot be used on glass or very thin table edges. They should not be used with a child who is large enough to push the table away with their feet.

### Booster Seat on a Dining Chair

A booster seat straps to the seat of a regular dining chair and raises the child to table height. These convert your existing dining chair and are good for families with limited space. They take more setup than a dedicated high chair and the harness situation varies by model. The Prince Lionheart booster seat and the Chicco Pocket Snack booster are well-regarded options at this end of the market.

### Low Weaning Table (Montessori-Style)

A floor-level table and small chair set that the child can get in and out of independently. These are designed to support child independence and are particularly popular in Montessori-influenced parenting approaches. They are not suitable for babies under twelve months in most cases. The IKEA FLISAT children's table is often used in this context.

## Top Picks

### IKEA Antilop — Under £30 / Under $25

The most popular high chair in the UK by a significant margin, and popular globally. It is made of injection-moulded plastic and steel tube legs. It wipes clean instantly. The tray clips on and off easily. The 5-point harness is included. The legs unscrew for easy transport or storage.

What it lacks: no recline, no height adjustment, very minimal padding (a separately sold cushion is available). The footrest is fixed and low, which is not ideal for older toddlers. For the price, the cleaning ease and simplicity make it difficult to beat. It does one job well.

### Tripp Trapp by Stokke — Around £250 / $330

The Tripp Trapp is a grown-with-you chair designed to convert from a high chair for a baby to a standard dining chair for an adult. The seat and footrest planks slide up and down the beech wood legs to adjust to any child (and adult) height. The build quality is excellent and many chairs are passed from one child to the next or bought second-hand in good condition.

The Baby Set accessory — sold separately for around £60 / $80 — is required to use the Tripp Trapp as a high chair. It adds a higher backrest and a front rail to contain a baby who cannot yet sit independently. The 5-point harness is a further separate purchase.

At full cost with the Baby Set and harness, the Tripp Trapp is over £300. This is expensive. However, it is genuinely functional as an everyday dining chair for older children and does not need to be replaced as the child grows. For families planning more than one child, the long-term cost per use is much lower than the upfront price suggests.

### Joie Multiply 6-in-1 — Around £120 / $160

A convertible chair that starts as a reclined baby seat and adjusts through multiple configurations as the child grows, finishing as a booster seat on a dining chair. The value proposition is real: one purchase covers several years of use. The build quality is mid-range. It wipes clean and the harness is 5-point. Not as minimalist as the Antilop or as premium as the Tripp Trapp, but a genuinely practical middle ground.

### Mamas and Papas Snax — Around £80 / $110

Compact, wipe-clean, adjustable height, 5-point harness. A solid mid-range option for UK buyers. The tray adjusts to three heights and removes with one hand. The seat surface is smooth plastic — good for cleaning. Reviews are generally positive for ease of use.

### Inglesina Fast Table Chair — Around £80 / $100

The best travel option. Clips to a table in seconds, folds flat, weighs around 2 kg. The fabric seat is machine washable. A 3-point harness is included — adequate but less secure than a 5-point harness for an older, more mobile baby. Most appropriate for children from six months who have good trunk control, up to around fifteen kilograms.

## What Not to Buy

Avoid any high chair with a lap-only harness for babies under twelve months. Avoid combination chair/bouncer products that are marketed as feeding chairs but are primarily bouncers — they are not designed for feeding use, typically do not have trays, and the reclined position is not appropriate for solid foods. Avoid chairs where the tray requires two hands to remove. Avoid second-hand chairs where the harness is damaged, frayed, or has been repaired — replace harness straps if in doubt.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**At what age can a baby start using a high chair?**

Around six months, when the baby can sit with support and solid foods are being introduced. A baby who cannot hold their head up steadily or who cannot sit with some support is not yet ready. Do not rush solids or high chair use for developmental reasons.

**Is a 5-point harness really necessary?**

Yes, for babies under twelve months. A lap-only harness allows babies to slide forward and become trapped between the harness and the tray or seat. A 5-point harness distributes restraint across the shoulders, hips, and crotch and prevents forward sliding.

**How do I clean a high chair with lots of crevices?**

For chairs with many joints and crevices, a soft toothbrush dipped in warm soapy water reaches into gaps that a cloth cannot. A steam cleaner works well on hard plastic surfaces. The practical lesson is to prioritise smooth, minimal-seam designs from the start — the IKEA Antilop's near-crevice-free shell is one of its biggest genuine advantages.

**Can I use a second-hand high chair?**

Yes, if the structure is sound and the harness is in good condition with no fraying, cuts, or repaired sections. Check that all adjustment mechanisms work correctly and that the tray clips securely. If the chair has a fabric seat that cannot be removed for washing, consider whether it has been cleaned thoroughly. Replace a worn harness rather than using a damaged one.

**What age do children stop needing a high chair?**

Most children transition to a booster seat on a regular dining chair between eighteen months and three years, depending on their size, development, and what your dining setup allows. The Tripp Trapp, by design, has no upper age limit — the child simply moves from the baby configuration to the standard chair configuration.

**Does the footrest really matter that much?**

It matters more than most people expect. A child sitting with feet unsupported uses core muscles to stabilise, which competes with the concentration and hand coordination needed to eat. Multiple occupational therapists who work in feeding therapy consistently identify footrest position as a contributing factor in children who are reluctant or distracted eaters.

## Key Takeaways

- The correct UK safety standard is BS EN 14988; the US equivalent is ASTM F404 with JPMA certification — check for these before buying.
- A 5-point harness is essential for babies under twelve months; lap-only harnesses allow dangerous forward sliding.
- Easy cleaning should be a top priority: smooth plastic seats that wipe clean are far more practical than padded fabric seats after the first week of weaning.
- A footrest at the right height supports posture and concentration during meals — adjustable footrests are better than fixed ones.
- The IKEA Antilop is the most popular choice for value and cleaning ease; the Tripp Trapp is the best long-term investment if budget allows.
- Avoid combination bouncer/chair products marketed as feeding chairs — they are not designed for solid food feeding and the reclined position is not appropriate.
