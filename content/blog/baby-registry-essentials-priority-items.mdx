# Baby Registry Essentials: What to Actually Prioritise Before Your Baby Arrives

Baby registries exist in a curious tension. On one side, they genuinely help friends and family buy useful things you actually need rather than duplicating soft toys you did not ask for. On the other side, the retailers who host them are incentivised to encourage as many items as possible, and most registry guides are written by people with affiliate links attached to every suggestion.

The result is that most new parents end up with too much of the wrong stuff and scrambling for the right stuff in the first days home. This guide structures the registry by when you actually need things, not by product category, and is honest about what is a gimmick.

## The Core Problem with Most Baby Registries

The average baby registry contains somewhere between 80 and 150 items. Research by Consumer Reports and by parenting communities consistently finds that first-time parents use roughly 20–30% of what they register for. The rest either goes unopened, gets returned, or sits in a cupboard until it is donated.

This happens because registry guides are structured around product categories (feeding, sleep, bathing, travel) rather than practical timelines, and because the assumption is that more is safer. It is not. Over-buying creates clutter, absorbs budget that could go elsewhere, and fills your home with things that actively get in the way.

A better approach is to think in three tiers: what you must have before birth, what is genuinely useful to add later, and what is not worth registering for at all.

## Tier 1: Must Have Before Birth

These are items you genuinely cannot manage without from day one or that must be installed and tested before you leave for hospital.

### Infant Car Seat

This is the single non-negotiable purchase. You cannot legally leave the hospital with a newborn without an appropriate car seat installed in the vehicle. It must be bought new or sourced from someone you trust completely — a seat involved in a crash may have invisible structural damage that compromises protection.

Look for seats that are independently tested, meet the current standard for your country (i-Size/R129 in the UK; FMVSS 213 in the US), and are installed correctly. The safest car seat is the one that fits your car and can be installed without errors. Chicco KeyFit 35, Maxi-Cosi Pebble 360, and Britax B-Safe are commonly cited for ease of correct installation. Prices range from around £150 to £350.

Register for a car seat installation check appointment through your local fire station or an approved technician — this costs nothing and is more valuable than the seat brand itself.

### Safe Sleep Space

This means a cot, crib, Moses basket, or cosleeper plus a new, firm, flat mattress and two to three fitted sheets. The safe sleep rules are clear: fitted sheet only, no pillows, duvets, bumpers, or toys in the sleep space. This is NHS and Lullaby Trust guidance based on SIDS research, not excess caution.

Two to three fitted sheets is the realistic minimum. Expect to need them at inconvenient hours. A fourth is not excessive.

A new mattress is recommended regardless of whether the cot itself is new or second-hand. Second-hand mattresses are not recommended by the NHS because of bacterial retention and potential softening.

### Nappies and Wipes

Buy a starter pack of newborn nappies and a pack of size 1 nappies. Do not over-buy newborn size — some babies arrive large enough to skip that size entirely, and you will not know your baby's size and brand preference until they are here.

Own-brand supermarket wipes (Aldi Mamia, ASDA Little Angels, or equivalent) perform identically to branded wipes in independent comparisons and cost significantly less. Register for a reasonable quantity of both and plan to buy more once you know what works.

### Clothing

The minimum wardrobe for a newborn:

- 5–7 sleepsuits (also called babygrows) in newborn and 0–3 months sizes
- 3–5 short-sleeved vests (bodysuits) to layer underneath
- 2 hats for hospital
- 2–3 pairs of scratch mitts

That is genuinely all you need for the first week. Babies grow fast enough that the 0–3 month size becomes the main wardrobe within days or weeks. Avoid registering for large quantities of any single size and avoid outfits with complex fastenings — you will be dressing a baby in the dark.

## Tier 2: Helpful Additions for the First Few Months

These items make life meaningfully easier but are not urgent before the birth. They can be added to the registry for people who want to give something beyond the Tier 1 essentials, or purchased in the first couple of weeks once you have a sense of what your baby needs.

### Nursing Pillow

A nursing pillow — the curved or C-shaped pillow that props the baby at the right height during feeding — makes a significant difference whether you are breastfeeding or bottle-feeding. It reduces strain on arms, shoulders, and back during what can be 10–12 feeds per day in the newborn period.

The Boppy (around £35–£45) and Ey! Bean Stalk are popular options. The My Brest Friend pillow (around £40–£50) has a firmer construction that some breastfeeding parents prefer for positional stability.

### Baby Carrier

A well-fitted carrier or sling frees both hands and allows you to keep a baby settled while doing other things. It is particularly useful in the "fourth trimester" when babies want constant contact.

The important safety note: cheap carriers with a narrow crotch piece do not support the hip-healthy M-position (knees higher than bottom). The Ergobaby Embrace (around £80–£100), BabyBjorn Mini (around £60–£80), or a stretchy wrap (Moby, Solly Baby — around £40–£60) are widely used starting options. A stretchy wrap is the lowest cost entry point for the newborn stage.

### Bouncer Seat

A bouncer seat gives you a safe, hands-free place to put your baby when they are awake and you need both hands. The BabyBjorn Bouncer (around £100–£200 depending on model) is the most commonly recommended for its gentle bounce-with-baby-weight mechanism. Budget bouncers with vibration functions also work well — the Chicco Hoopla costs around £35–£50.

### Swaddle Blankets

Swaddles help many newborns sleep better by mimicking the contained feeling of the womb. Muslin swaddles (Aden + Anais, around £30–£45 for a set of four) are versatile, breathable, and useful as burp cloths, sunshades, and surface covers as well. Easy-fasten swaddle wraps like the Love to Dream Swaddle Up (around £30) are an easier option for parents who find traditional swaddling hard to learn quickly.

### Nasal Aspirator

The Frida NoseFrida (around £20) is consistently recommended by paediatricians for clearing blocked infant noses. Babies cannot blow their noses and nasal congestion significantly disrupts feeding and sleep. This looks alarming, works extremely well, and is genuinely one of the best value purchases on a baby registry.

### Baby Nail File

Newborn nails grow fast and are sharp. A fine nail file is safer for very young babies than clippers and requires less nerve than scissors. Add a pair of round-ended scissors for a few weeks later.

### Thermometer

A digital rectal thermometer is the most accurate for under-three-month-olds. The AAP recommends rectal temperature for infants under three months because accuracy matters — a high temperature in a newborn under eight weeks requires immediate medical attention. An ear or temporal (forehead) thermometer is more convenient for older babies. Braun and Kinetik both make reliable options in the £20–£40 range.

## Tier 3: Things to Wait On

These items are genuinely useful eventually but you do not know yet which version will work for your baby, and registering for them prematurely often means getting the wrong version.

**Highchair.** You will not need this until around six months when solid foods begin. Test-drive different models and check the fit with your dining table before buying. The IKEA Antilop (£25) is widely used for ease of cleaning. The Stokke Tripp Trapp (£260) grows with the child into adulthood. Both are defensible choices for different reasons.

**Activity gym.** Useful from around eight to twelve weeks, but your baby's preferences are unknown in advance. It can safely wait until after birth.

**Nursery decor.** Wall art, name letters, and room decorations are completely elective. They serve no functional purpose and can be bought (or gifted informally) once you know your space and preferences.

## What Not to Register For

These items are heavily marketed and consistently regretted:

**Wipe warmer.** Warm wipes feel marginally more pleasant. A wipe warmer is a device that uses electricity to marginally warm wipes, can dry them out, and in some cases creates a breeding ground for bacteria. Every wipe change from birth onwards will involve a room-temperature wipe. Babies adapt.

**Bottle warmer.** A jug of hot water from the tap does the same job. Formula guidelines specify warming in hot water, not in a dedicated appliance with uneven heating.

**Baby food maker.** A blender purees food. Any blender already in your kitchen works identically. The Beaba Babycook (around £100–£150) is a blender with a steamer attachment sold at a premium.

**Changing table.** A waterproof changing mat on a dresser, bed, or floor works identically to a dedicated changing table. A changing table is large, expensive, difficult to store when outgrown, and awkward for taller parents because the height is not adjustable. Register for a good changing mat instead.

**Baby shoes.** Babies do not walk. Shoes before cruising age (roughly nine to twelve months) serve no developmental purpose and often restrict natural foot movement. Socks with grips are the practical alternative for the pre-walking stage.

**Matching nursery sets.** Boxed nursery sets typically contain a duvet, pillow, and bumper that the NHS advises against using. The aesthetic appeal is real; the safety is not there.

## How to Share the Registry

Most major retailers (Amazon, Mothercare, John Lewis, Target, Babylist) allow you to create and share a registry link. Babylist is increasingly popular because it aggregates items from multiple retailers into a single list and shows group gifting options for higher-priced items.

Share the link at or after the baby shower invite stage. Some families prefer not to share until directly asked — both approaches are reasonable.

## The Pram Question

The pram or pushchair is often the biggest single expense in baby preparation and is deliberately not included in this registry guide for one reason: you need to test-drive it in person before buying.

The folding mechanism is the most underrated decision criterion. The weight matters more than most reviews acknowledge. The handlebar height makes a significant difference if you and your partner are different heights. None of this can be assessed from a product listing or review. Go to a store, try folding several models one-handed, put them in the boot of your car, and make the decision from there.

If you want to register for it, register for a gift card toward the pram of your choice rather than a specific model selected by someone who has not driven it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When should I start the baby registry?**

Around 20–28 weeks is the most common time, after the anatomy scan and once the pregnancy is more established. Starting earlier is fine; leaving it until the third trimester creates time pressure.

**How many items should be on the registry?**

A functional registry with genuine use cases typically has 30–50 items. If yours is approaching 100, that is a signal to reassess what is genuinely needed versus what a retailer prompted you to add.

**Can I register for both in-person and online retailers?**

Yes. Babylist allows you to aggregate items from any retailer. Sticking to one or two retailers makes it simpler for gift-givers who prefer physical shops.

**Should I register for clothing in specific sizes?**

Focus on 0–3 months and 3–6 months. Newborn size is outgrown fast and some babies skip it entirely. Size up when in doubt — an outfit that is slightly big will get worn; one that is too small will not.

**Is it rude to return registry gifts?**

No. Retailers that host registries expect a proportion of gifts to be returned or exchanged. If you receive multiples of something or an item that does not suit your circumstances, return it without guilt. The gift-giver did their part.

**What if my budget is very limited?**

The true minimum to bring a baby home safely costs around £200–£300: a car seat, a Moses basket or cot with a new mattress, two fitted sheets, nappies, wipes, and a few sleepsuits. Everything beyond that list can be acquired gradually or sourced second-hand (excluding the car seat and mattress, which should be new).

## Original Insight: Your Registry Should Reflect Your Actual Life, Not Generic "Needs"

Most registry articles provide comprehensive lists of categories and items based on what parents might theoretically need. But the best registry is one that reflects your specific circumstances: your living space size, your lifestyle, your partner arrangement, whether you're working, your support system. A parent returning to full-time work at 12 weeks has a completely different registry priority than a parent with a flexible schedule or one parent at home. Someone in a flat with limited storage has different needs than someone in a house with a nursery. If you're planning exclusive pumping, your registry looks different from direct breastfeeding. If you have older children, many items are already owned. The problem with generic registry lists is that they create guilt when you don't buy everything, or waste when you buy things your life doesn't actually accommodate. A better approach: think about your daily routines and register for items that make those routines easier. If you plan to leave the house frequently, a travel-friendly carrier matters more than a bouncer. If you're bedsharing initially (which millions of families safely do), investing in side-sleeping solutions is more relevant than a bedside crib. If cost is a constraint, prioritise what you'll use daily (nappies, clothing, a safe sleep space) over items you'll use occasionally. A minimal functional registry is genuinely sufficient; everything else is optimization based on your specific life.

## Key Takeaways

- The most essential items before birth are the infant car seat, a safe sleep space with a new mattress, nappies, and basic clothing — everything else can wait
- Register by when you need things, not by product category, and keep the list to 30–50 genuinely used items
- A nasal aspirator, nursing pillow, stretchy wrap, and bouncer are the highest-value additions beyond the essentials
- Do not register for a changing table, wipe warmer, bottle warmer, baby food maker, or newborn shoes — none are necessary
- Test the pram in person before buying; register for a gift card toward it rather than a specific model
- A functional starter registry can be completed for around £200–£300 at minimum; everything beyond that is optional
