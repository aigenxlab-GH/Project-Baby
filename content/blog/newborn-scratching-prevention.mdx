# Newborn Scratching: Why It Happens and How to Prevent It Safely

A scratch on a newborn's face is one of those discoveries that stops a new parent cold. The baby looks healthy, the baby seems content, and yet there is a fresh red mark across the cheek that was not there an hour ago. For most families, newborn facial scratching becomes a recurring feature of the first weeks — something that catches you off guard the first time, and requires a consistent response thereafter.

Understanding why it happens and which prevention methods are genuinely safe will make the experience less alarming and help you develop an approach that protects your baby without interfering with their early development.

## Why Newborns Scratch Themselves

The explanation is straightforward, and it has nothing to do with distress, discomfort, or anything you have or have not done.

Newborn nails grow quickly — faster than most parents expect. They begin growing in utero and by the time of birth are often already long enough to cause scratches, with edges that are thin and relatively sharp despite being soft. Immediately after birth, the nails are pliable but fine-edged. Within the first week or two, as they begin to dry and harden slightly, they become capable of marking the delicate skin of the face.

At the same time, newborns have no voluntary motor control over their arms. The neurological pathways that allow purposeful, controlled movement take months to develop. What a newborn experiences instead is a range of reflexive, involuntary arm movements — most visibly the Moro reflex, or startle reflex, which causes the arms to fling outward and then draw back in response to sudden sensory input. These movements are uncontrolled, unpredictable, and bring the hands into contact with the face repeatedly throughout the day.

Because the newborn cannot yet direct their hands away from their face — that intentional hand control typically develops around three months as the corticospinal tract matures — every involuntary arm swing carries the risk of a sharp fingernail making contact with the skin.

The combination of fast-growing sharp nails and uncontrolled arm movement is what produces newborn facial scratches. It is not a sign of distress, not a sign that the baby is uncomfortable with eczema or skin irritation (though those can cause scratching in older babies), and not something you caused or failed to prevent.

## Prevention Options and Their Trade-Offs

There are several approaches to preventing newborn scratches. Each has genuine advantages and some limitations. Understanding the trade-offs lets you make an informed choice rather than following the first advice you receive.

### Scratch Mitts

Scratch mitts are small fabric coverings worn over the newborn's hands. They are widely available, inexpensive, and effective at their stated purpose — when the hand is covered, the nail cannot make contact with the face.

The limitation is developmental rather than safety-related. Newborns experience the world substantially through touch, and the palms are particularly rich in sensory nerve endings. Tactile input to the palms in the first weeks and months contributes to sensory processing development and to the baby's early attempts at grasping — reaching for and holding objects is one of the first deliberate motor acts, and it begins with the hands.

Wearing mitts continuously restricts this sensory input and covers the hands during the period when early grasping reflexes are active. The grasping reflex — where the baby's hand closes around anything placed in the palm — is present from birth and is a precursor to intentional grip.

For these reasons, scratch mitts are most appropriately used in specific situations rather than continuously. During sleep is the most logical time, when the hands are not engaged in sensory exploration and when you cannot monitor closely. During periods of wakefulness when the baby is supervised, mitt-free time allows normal tactile and motor development.

### Fold-Over Cuffs on Babygros

Many modern sleepsuits and babygros have built-in cuffs that fold over the hands. This is a more convenient alternative to separate mitts — there is nothing to lose, nothing to keep track of, and the coverage integrates with a garment the baby is already wearing.

The developmental trade-off is essentially the same as scratch mitts: covered hands reduce tactile sensory input and grasping opportunity. The same guidance applies — fold-over cuffs during sleep are practical; during waking periods when the baby is alert and supervised, folding them back to allow hand exposure is worth doing.

From a comfort perspective, fold-over cuffs in soft jersey fabric are generally gentle against newborn skin. Separate scratch mitts sometimes slip or bunch, which can be uncomfortable if a finger gets folded back inside. Integrated cuffs sit more smoothly.

### Nail Filing

Filing is the gentlest approach to nail management and is recommended by many midwives and health visitors for the first few weeks when the nails are soft enough to be managed without cutting tools. A fine emery board or a dedicated baby nail file creates a smooth, blunt edge without the risk of cutting the skin that comes with clippers.

The technique: hold the finger gently but firmly, pressing the fingertip pad slightly away from the nail to expose the free edge, and file in one direction. Circular or back-and-forth motion is less effective on soft nails. Focus on the corners — these are where the sharpest points develop. Filing does not need to be a formal procedure; many parents incorporate it into a quiet moment during feeding or during sleep.

Filing frequency depends on how quickly the nails grow. In the first weeks, every four to five days is often sufficient. As the baby grows and nails harden, the interval may extend.

### Nail Clipping

As nails harden over the first few weeks — typically by three to four weeks, though this varies — clipping becomes more reliable than filing. Dedicated baby nail clippers have small blades and often a curved tip guard. Scissors designed for baby nails are an alternative, though some parents find clippers easier to control.

The timing trick widely recommended by health visitors is to clip the nails during sleep. A deeply sleeping newborn does not flinch or withdraw the hand, making the job significantly easier than attempting it when the baby is awake and unpredictably moving. The first few times feel nerve-wracking; it becomes routine quickly.

When clipping, cut straight across and do not follow the curve of the fingertip too closely. Cutting at the sides of the nail increases the risk of an ingrown nail as growth continues. If you accidentally nick the skin — something most parents do at least once — apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth. Do not use plasters on babies as they are a choking hazard if they come loose.

## Treating an Existing Newborn Facial Scratch

If your newborn already has a scratch, the most reassuring thing to know is that newborn skin heals with remarkable speed. The same properties that make newborn skin vulnerable — thin, soft, and fine-structured — also allow rapid cell turnover and healing. Most superficial facial scratches in newborns are gone within forty-eight to seventy-two hours.

**Clean gently.** Use cool boiled water and a soft clean cloth or cotton wool. Pat rather than wipe. There is no need for antiseptic cream on a clean superficial scratch — this can actually delay healing in some cases by disrupting the skin's own healing processes.

**Do not cover with a plaster.** This applies to any scratch on a baby. Plasters are a choking hazard if they come loose, and they are not necessary for superficial scratches that will heal quickly with normal care.

**Monitor for signs of infection.** These are: increasing redness around the scratch (spreading rather than fading), yellow or green discharge, warmth and swelling around the site, and — in more serious cases — fever. If any of these develop, a GP review is appropriate. Infected facial wounds in young infants need prompt assessment because the skin and structures are delicate and the immune system in early infancy is still developing.

In practice, the vast majority of newborn facial scratches heal without any complication. The risk of infection is low in a clean home environment, and a fresh scratch with nothing more than a thin red line does not require treatment beyond gentle cleaning.

## How Long Does the Scratching Phase Last?

The peak of involuntary face-scratching is in the first six to eight weeks. By around three months, most babies are beginning to develop the early stages of intentional hand movement — watching their hands, bringing them together deliberately, reaching toward objects. This intentional motor control means the random, uncontrolled arm movements of the newborn period begin to give way to more purposeful actions, and the frequency of accidental self-scratching typically reduces significantly.

This does not mean that all scratching ends at three months. As babies develop the ability to reach and grab, they may inadvertently scratch themselves with nails that catch against the face during vigorous grabbing movements. But the involuntary startle-and-swipe pattern of the newborn period does diminish as voluntary motor control develops.

Regular nail maintenance — filing or clipping every four to five days — continues to be the most effective preventive measure throughout the first year.

## A Note on Infant Skin Sensitivity

Occasionally parents interpret facial scratches as a sign of eczema or skin irritation, particularly if the scratches appear frequently despite nail management. In older babies, eczema does cause intense itching that leads to scratching. But in the newborn period, the scratching described in this article is motor-driven rather than itch-driven — the baby is not scratching because something itches; the hand is making contact with the face because of reflexive arm movement.

Infant eczema, if it develops, typically presents as dry, red, scaly patches — often beginning on the cheeks and forehead — and appears more commonly after the first few weeks, usually from two to six months. If you notice persistent dry, red, or scaly patches alongside scratching, and if the scratching behaviour seems different from the involuntary newborn arm movement (i.e., repetitive, directed, and occurring mainly at the site of the rash), raise this with your health visitor or GP.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**At what age can I start clipping my newborn's nails?**
You can clip as soon as you are comfortable doing so and as soon as the nails are firm enough to clip cleanly, which is typically by three to four weeks. Many parents prefer filing in the first two to three weeks when nails are softer, then transition to clipping. There is no minimum age; the key is using appropriately sized baby nail clippers and cutting straight across.

**Should I use scratch mitts all the time?**
No. Scratch mitts are most appropriate during sleep or brief unsupervised periods. During waking, supervised time, allowing the hands to be uncovered gives the baby access to normal tactile stimulation and allows the grasping reflex to function. A balanced approach — mitts when sleeping, uncovered hands when awake and supervised — is practical and developmentally sound.

**The scratch on my newborn's cheek looks deeper than a surface mark. Should I take them to the GP?**
If the scratch is bleeding and does not stop with gentle pressure within a few minutes, or if it appears deep rather than superficial, a GP or minor injury unit review is sensible. A wound that requires closure in a newborn is rare from a fingernail scratch but not impossible. If it is superficial and stops bleeding quickly, home management is appropriate.

**My baby keeps getting new scratches despite nail maintenance. What am I doing wrong?**
Almost certainly nothing. The startle reflex and involuntary arm movements in early infancy are powerful and frequent. Even well-filed nails can occasionally catch the face during a vigorous Moro reflex. The frequency of new scratches usually declines as the nails become more routinely maintained and as the baby develops slightly better postural control. If scratch mitts at night are not already part of the routine, adding them is the most effective additional step.

**Is it safe to bite off a newborn's nails?**
No. Biting transfers oral bacteria to the raw nail edge and to any skin it contacts. While this was a common practice historically, it is now discouraged because of infection risk. Using a file or baby nail clippers is safer and produces a cleaner edge.

**Do premature babies have the same scratching pattern?**
Premature babies may have even softer and more fragile skin, making scratch management important from an earlier corrected age. However, premature babies also have different reflex patterns depending on their gestational age at birth. If you have a premature baby, follow the guidance of your neonatal care team on nail care and the appropriate use of hand coverings.

## Key Takeaways

- Newborn facial scratching is caused by fast-growing sharp nails combined with involuntary arm movements — it is not distress-driven and is not a parenting failure.
- Scratch mitts are effective but should not be worn continuously; uncovered hands during supervised waking time support normal tactile and motor development.
- Filing with a fine emery board is the gentlest approach in the first few weeks; clipping with dedicated baby nail clippers becomes easier from around three to four weeks.
- Most newborn facial scratches heal within forty-eight to seventy-two hours; clean with cool boiled water and do not cover with plasters.
- Signs of infection — spreading redness, yellow discharge, warmth, fever — require GP review.
- The peak of involuntary face-scratching typically reduces by around three months as early voluntary hand control develops.
