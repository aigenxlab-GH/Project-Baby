The baby product industry is worth billions of pounds. Marketing is sophisticated, parental anxiety is real, and the result is that many new parents spend between £2,000 and £4,000 on products in the first year — most of which they will use infrequently or not at all. This guide strips that back.

The goal here is not to be minimalist for its own sake. Some baby products genuinely make life safer, easier, or more comfortable. Others are clever solutions to problems that do not exist. This guide tells you which is which, what the safety standards mean, and how to avoid the most expensive common mistakes.

## The Problem With Baby Product Overwhelm

A first pregnancy triggers a flood of product recommendations — from apps, social media, friends, and well-meaning relatives. The result is a registry or shopping list that contains items no one has interrogated.

The rational approach to buying for a baby is:

1. Safety first — some items have legal or physical safety implications
2. Fit for your specific situation — your house, your lifestyle, your birth plans
3. Price — only after the above two are resolved

Buy only what you need before birth. A significant proportion of what you will actually use becomes apparent in the first few weeks, and most baby products are available for next-day delivery.

## Before-Birth Essentials

These are the items you genuinely need before or on the day of birth.

### Infant Car Seat

An infant car seat is the single non-negotiable purchase. You cannot legally bring your baby home from hospital in a car without one. Key points:

- **Must be new or crash-free with verified history**: A car seat that has been in a crash may look undamaged but its structural integrity may be compromised. If buying second-hand, you need to know the full history of the seat with certainty
- **i-Size (R129) certification** is the current standard, superseding the older Group 0/0+ system. It provides better rear-facing protection and is tested at higher speeds
- **Rear-facing is significantly safer**: Extended rear-facing seats keep children rear-facing for longer (up to 105cm or 18kg). This is the approach recommended by child road safety organisations and the NHS

### Safe Sleep Space

A cot, crib, or Moses basket with a firm, flat, waterproof-covered mattress. The Lullaby Trust recommends a new mattress for each baby — second-hand mattresses have been associated with higher SIDS risk in some studies, and they may have softened or developed indentations that are not visible.

The sleep space needs:

- A firm, flat surface (not a bouncer, car seat, or swing)
- A well-fitted mattress — no gaps between mattress and sides
- No pillows, duvets, or bumpers
- A firm place in the room where the primary caregiver sleeps

### Clothing

Newborns need very little clothing, and they grow rapidly.

- **5–7 sleepsuits**: These are the workhorses of newborn dressing. Buy a range: some size "newborn" (for babies up to about 3.5kg), mostly "0–3 months"
- **3–5 short-sleeved vests/bodysuits**: To layer underneath sleepsuits in cold weather
- **A few scratch mitts**: Most sleepsuits have fold-over cuffs that serve the same purpose
- **1–2 cardigans or zip-up tops**: For layers
- Do not overbuy — babies spend much of the early weeks in sleepsuits, and they outgrow sizes quickly

### Nappies

Have a starter pack at home before birth. Do not buy dozens in newborn size before the birth — some babies go straight to size 1. Start with 1–2 packs of newborn, 1–2 packs of size 1.

### Feeding Equipment

If planning to breastfeed exclusively, you will need very little at first. However, having a manual breast pump available means you can express if needed (engorgement, latch issues, returning to work). If planning to formula feed or combination feed:

- Bottles (start with 2–3 of a single type before buying a set)
- An electric steam steriliser
- Bottle brushes
- Formula (seek advice from your midwife on brand if uncertain)

## The Second Tier: Useful but Not Day-One Essentials

These are items many parents find invaluable but which you can acquire in the first days or weeks after birth.

**Baby carrier or sling**: One of the most useful baby products. Hands-free carrying supports parent-infant bonding and is often effective for settling a fussy baby. Ring slings work from birth. Soft-structured carriers typically have a newborn insert for use from birth. Wait until the baby arrives before choosing, as preferences vary significantly.

**Bouncer**: A place to put a baby down that is not the floor. Useful for the short periods when you need two hands. Should not be used as a sleep space.

**Nursing pillow**: Supports arm positioning during breastfeeding and reduces strain on shoulders and back. Particularly useful in the first few weeks.

**White noise machine**: Strong evidence supports white noise for soothing newborns. A dedicated machine is useful; a fan or app on your phone also works.

**Baby nail file**: Newborn nails grow surprisingly fast and are sharp. A glass or crystal nail file is the safest option for newborns.

**Digital thermometer**: Essential health equipment. An ear thermometer (tympanic) or axillary (under-arm) thermometer is appropriate for babies; forehead strip thermometers are not accurate enough.

**Nasal aspirator**: Babies cannot blow their noses. A nasal aspirator helps clear congestion, particularly during illnesses. Bulb aspirators are less effective than mouth-suction types (such as the Frida NoseFrida).

## What to Buy Second-Hand Safely

Buying second-hand is sensible for the vast majority of baby equipment. Well-made products from reputable brands hold up well and represent significant savings.

Safe to buy second-hand:

- **Prams and strollers**: Check for wear on the harness, functioning brakes, and that the frame is not bent. Check for any recalls (gov.uk/product-safety-alerts). A parent-facing option for newborns is beneficial
- **Baby clothing**: Check fastenings, seams, and wash regularly
- **Highchair**: Acceptable second-hand if the harness works correctly and there are no cracks in the seat that could trap fingers or pinch. Clean thoroughly
- **Play equipment**: Baby gyms, activity centres, rattles. Check for paint chips and loose parts

## What Must Be New

- **Car seat**: Non-negotiable, unless you have verified crash-free history from someone you completely trust
- **Cot mattress**: A new firm mattress for each baby; The Lullaby Trust recommendation
- **Breast pump parts that contact milk**: If buying a second-hand pump, the tubing, valves, and breast shields that contact milk should be replaced with new parts

## Safety Certifications to Know

Knowing what markings matter helps you evaluate products with confidence:

- **i-Size / R129**: The current European car seat safety standard, tested at higher crash speeds than the older system. This is what to look for on any infant or convertible car seat
- **BS EN 1930**: The standard for baby safety gates. Look for this on any stair gate or room barrier you purchase
- **BS EN 716**: The European standard for children's cots. Look for this when buying a new cot
- **GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard)**: Certifies that textiles are made from organic fibres using restricted chemical processes. Relevant if you are concerned about pesticide residue in clothing and bedding
- **OEKO-TEX Standard 100**: Tests finished textiles for harmful substances. A lower bar than GOTS but widely applied and reassuring for products in contact with baby's skin

When buying second-hand, search the product name on gov.uk/product-safety-alerts to check for any product recalls before purchasing.

## The Most Common Expensive Mistakes

**Wipe warmer**: Solves a problem that does not need solving. Room-temperature wipes are perfectly adequate.

**Bottle warmer**: A bowl or jug of hot water achieves the same result without a dedicated appliance.

**Dedicated baby food maker**: A regular blender or hand blender does the job. A steamer and blender combination is unnecessary for the months of weaning they cover.

**Shoes before walking**: Pre-walking "shoes" have no function and may impede natural foot development. Bare feet or soft fabric socks are appropriate until a child is walking confidently outdoors.

**Elaborate cot bedding sets with bumpers**: Cot bumpers are unsafe (suffocation and strangulation risk) and are banned in some countries. Any set that includes a bumper means paying for something that should not be used.

**Top-of-the-range travel system as first purchase**: Test in person before buying. A pram that does not fit in your car boot or through your front door will not be used, regardless of its review scores.

## How to Check for Product Recalls

Before buying any baby product second-hand — and before registering any new product — check for safety alerts and recalls:

- **UK**: gov.uk/product-safety-alerts — the government's official product safety database
- **US**: CPSC.gov — the Consumer Product Safety Commission database
- **EU**: ec.europa.eu/consumers/consumers_safety/safety_products/rapex — the RAPEX rapid alert system

Register new baby products with the manufacturer so they can contact you directly in the event of a recall.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to buy everything before the baby arrives?**
No. The only items you genuinely need from day one are a car seat (if travelling by car), a safe sleep space with a new mattress, basic clothing, and nappies. Most other items can be acquired in the first days or weeks based on what actually suits your baby and your situation.

**Is it safe to use second-hand baby equipment?**
Many items are fine second-hand with appropriate checks. Car seats and cot mattresses should always be new (or you must have complete, trusted knowledge of their history). For all second-hand items, check for product recalls before purchasing.

**What does i-Size mean for car seats?**
i-Size is the current European regulation (R129) for child car seats. It requires seats to be tested at higher speeds than the older Group system and mandates rear-facing travel to a minimum height of 105cm. It also simplifies compatibility checking between seat and car. It is the preferred standard for new car seat purchases.

**How much should I budget for baby essentials before birth?**
A realistic, non-excessive budget for genuine essentials (car seat, safe sleep setup, clothing, nappies, basic feeding equipment) is typically £300–600. Adding a pram/travel system brings this to £500–1,200 depending on what you choose and how much you buy new vs second-hand.

**Are own-brand formula and nappies as good as branded products?**
In the UK, infant formula must meet the same strict compositional standards regardless of brand — the difference between own-brand and premium branded formula is price, not nutrition. For nappies, independent testing has found comparable performance between supermarket own-brand and branded options for most babies.

**Should I buy a travel system (pram + car seat combo)?**
Travel systems are convenient if the specific seat and pushchair combination suits your lifestyle. The most important check: test with your actual car before purchasing, and check the pram folds to a size that fits your car boot and home storage. Buy neither the pushchair nor the car seat without doing these checks first.

## Key Takeaways

- An infant car seat, a safe sleep space with a new firm mattress, basic clothing, and nappies are the only genuine before-birth essentials; most other purchases can wait until after the baby arrives.
- Car seats must be new or have verified crash-free history — structural damage from a collision is not visible to the eye and renders the seat unsafe.
- i-Size (R129) is the current European standard for car seat safety and should be the starting point for any car seat purchase.
- Cot bumpers are unsafe and should not be used; any bedding set that includes them means paying for something that must be discarded.
- Check gov.uk/product-safety-alerts before buying any second-hand baby product to ensure there are no outstanding recalls.
- The two largest ongoing costs in the first year are nappies and formula; own-brand alternatives are nutritionally and functionally equivalent to premium branded products.
