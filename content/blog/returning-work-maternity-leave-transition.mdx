# Returning to Work After Maternity Leave: A Practical Transition Guide

The weeks before returning to work after maternity leave tend to carry a specific kind of dread — a mixture of logistical anxiety, guilt, and the low-level fear that both the job and the baby will somehow suffer. Most of this is manageable with planning, and some of it dissolves on its own after the first week back. What follows is practical guidance on the legal rights, childcare logistics, breastfeeding considerations, and emotional reality of going back to work.

## Know Your Rights Before You Go Back

In the UK, employees returning from statutory maternity leave have the right to return to the same job on the same terms and conditions. If you took more than 26 weeks of Additional Maternity Leave, you are entitled to return to the same job, or — if that is not reasonably practicable — a suitable alternative role on no less favourable terms. The NHS and government guidance are clear that any detriment or dismissal related to maternity leave is unlawful.

You also have the right to request flexible working after 26 weeks of continuous employment. This covers changes to hours, location, or pattern of work. Your employer must consider the request and can only refuse on specific business grounds. Submit the request in writing and keep a copy.

In the US, the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) entitles eligible employees to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave. On return, you have the right to the same or an equivalent position. Several states offer additional protections and paid leave provisions beyond federal law.

## Start the Childcare Search Early

Three to six months before your return date is not too soon to begin researching childcare. Good nurseries and childminders fill up. In many urban areas, waiting lists for the most popular settings run to a year or longer. The NHS recommends visiting at least three settings before deciding, looking specifically at staff-to-child ratios, Ofsted ratings (in England), the key person approach, and how the setting handles the settling-in period.

Most nurseries offer settling-in sessions — typically two or three short visits over one to two weeks — before full-time attendance begins. Schedule these to end a week before you return, not the day before. You want time to problem-solve if the transition is rocky.

## Consider a Phased Return

Many employers will agree to a phased return — starting three or four days per week for the first month, or working shortened hours in the first week. This is worth negotiating even if it is not offered. A phased return gives you time to manage early childcare anxieties, re-establish pumping routines if you are breastfeeding, and rebuild cognitive bandwidth for work without being immediately at full capacity.

## Breastfeeding at Work

The legal position in the UK is that employers are required to provide a suitable, private space for breastfeeding or expressing milk — not a toilet. They should also allow reasonable breaks for this purpose. In the US, the PUMP Act (2022) extended break time and private space protections to most employees, including those previously excluded.

Practically, plan to pump at roughly the same times your baby would usually feed. Most mothers returning to work find that two pumping sessions in an eight-hour day is sufficient to maintain supply, combined with feeds at home. A hospital-grade double electric pump significantly reduces the time each session takes. Expressed milk stored at room temperature is safe for four hours; in the fridge for up to five days; in the freezer for up to six months, according to NHS guidelines. Label everything with the date.

Supply can dip in the first week back as stress affects let-down and sessions become rushed. This usually stabilises. If supply drops significantly, contact a lactation consultant before assuming breastfeeding must end.

## Do a Mock Run Before Day One

The week before you return, do a full trial run on at least one day: get up at the time you will need to, get yourself and the baby ready, drop off at childcare, and travel the commute. This surfaces problems — a bag you cannot carry while pushing a buggy, a morning routine that takes twenty minutes longer than you planned, a childcare drop-off that runs long — before they happen on day one when you have a meeting at nine.

## Talk to Your Manager in Advance

Request a conversation with your manager before your first day back, not on it. Agree what your workload will look like in the first month. If you were covering projects during your leave, clarify the handback plan. Set the expectation that there will be a ramp-up period. Most managers who have been through this themselves will be reasonable; most who have not will be reasonable if you frame it professionally and in advance.

## The Guilt Is Normal and the Evidence Is Reassuring

Almost every parent who returns to work describes guilt at the point of returning. It is worth knowing what the research actually shows. Multiple large studies, including data reviewed by the AAP, find that children of working mothers do not have worse developmental outcomes than children of non-working mothers, particularly when childcare quality is good. Children of mothers who work and find their work fulfilling show, on average, slightly higher academic outcomes and — specifically for daughters — higher professional aspirations. Guilt is a feeling. It is not evidence of harm.

The first separation is almost always harder on the parent than the child. Most children settle in unfamiliar environments within a few days. Many are cheerful at nursery within a week and teary at drop-off for another two weeks — the distress is real but brief. Day three is typically worse than day one. Day seven is almost always better than day three.

## The First Week Back

Prepare everything the night before: bag packed, clothes laid out, food organised. Give yourself fifteen minutes more than you think you need. At the end of each day that first week, take ten minutes to debrief with your partner or a trusted friend — not to process the guilt, but simply to note what worked and what to adjust tomorrow. Most parents find that by the end of the second week, the new normal begins to feel like a normal.
