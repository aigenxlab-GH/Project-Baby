Hair appointments often feel like a small act of self-care — but once pregnancy is confirmed, many people find themselves wondering whether the chemicals involved are safe for a developing baby. It is a reasonable question, and the evidence is more reassuring than you might expect.

## Why the Concern Exists

The worry about hair dye in pregnancy centres on the chemicals absorbed through the scalp during the colouring process. Studies conducted on animals have shown that very high doses of hair dye chemicals can cause harm, which is what prompted the original caution. However, the doses used in those animal studies were far higher than any human would be exposed to during a normal hair appointment.

Human evidence is considerably more reassuring. Multiple epidemiological studies have not found a clear link between standard personal use of hair dye and adverse pregnancy outcomes. The challenge is that conducting controlled trials on pregnant women is not ethically possible, so researchers rely on observational data — which, while not perfect, does not show a meaningful pattern of harm.

## What the NHS Says

The NHS advises that hair dye is generally considered safe to use during pregnancy, but recommends waiting until after the first 12 weeks as a precaution. The first trimester is when the baby's major organs and neural tube are forming, so most healthcare professionals support being more cautious during this window, even when the actual risk is thought to be low.

After the first trimester, the NHS position is that you can colour your hair, provided you take sensible precautions. Most NHS trusts reflect this guidance in their antenatal information.

## The Chemicals of Concern

### Permanent Hair Dye

Permanent dyes contain the chemicals most frequently raised in pregnancy discussions:

**Para-phenylenediamine (PPD)** is the primary colouring agent in most permanent dyes. It penetrates the hair shaft and, during the dyeing process, a small amount can be absorbed through the scalp. PPD is also the compound most likely to cause allergic contact dermatitis — a risk that exists whether you are pregnant or not.

**Hydrogen peroxide** is used to open the hair cuticle and activate the colour. It is also a component of bleach. The scalp absorbs a small amount during application.

**Ammonia** raises the hair's pH to allow colour to penetrate. Like hydrogen peroxide, only trace amounts pass through the scalp.

The key point is that the amount absorbed systemically through the scalp during a typical hair treatment is very small. The concern is not zero, but neither is the evidence that this small amount causes harm in human pregnancies.

### Semi-Permanent and Demi-Permanent Dye

These dyes sit on or just below the cuticle rather than penetrating deep into the hair shaft. They do not require the same level of hydrogen peroxide and generally do not contain ammonia. Less chemical activity means less scalp absorption, making them a lower-exposure option for those who want extra reassurance.

## Options That Involve Less Scalp Contact

### Highlights and Balayage

Both highlights and balayage techniques involve applying colour to the hair lengths and tips rather than directly onto the scalp. Because the dye does not make significant contact with the scalp, systemic absorption is much lower than with an all-over colour. Many hair colourists and midwives consider these techniques particularly suitable during pregnancy for this reason.

### Plant-Based Dyes — Henna and Beyond

Pure henna — made from the Lawsonia inermis plant — has a long history of use and is not associated with the same chemical concerns as synthetic dyes. It produces a reddish-brown tone and can be a reasonable option for those who want to avoid synthetic chemicals entirely.

One important caution: "black henna" is not pure henna. Black henna typically contains PPD at concentrations far higher than those found in commercial hair dye. It is associated with severe allergic reactions and skin damage. Avoid black henna during pregnancy — and in general.

Other plant-based options such as indigo, cassia, and beetroot root powder exist, though the colour results are more limited than synthetic alternatives.

## Practical Precautions to Take

Even if your overall risk from hair dye is low, there are straightforward steps that make any colouring session safer:

**Ventilation matters.** Do the treatment — or have it done — in a room with good airflow. Open windows, switch on an extractor fan. Minimising the time you spend inhaling chemical fumes is sensible regardless of pregnancy.

**Minimise scalp contact.** Opt for techniques like highlights, balayage, or root-free colour application where possible. If using an all-over colour, ask your colourist to avoid the scalp line as much as possible.

**Always do a patch test.** Pregnancy can change your skin's sensitivity and immune response. A dye you have used for years without reaction can cause an allergic response during pregnancy. Apply a small amount behind the ear or on the inner elbow 48 hours before your appointment. The NHS routinely recommends patch testing before every colour application, but it is especially important during pregnancy.

**Wear gloves when applying at home.** If you are colouring your hair yourself, gloves reduce skin contact with the dye. Most home kits include them.

**Rinse the scalp thoroughly.** After colour application, ensure the scalp is rinsed completely. Leaving dye residue on the scalp extends contact time unnecessarily.

**Wait until after 12 weeks if possible.** Even if the risk is small, waiting until the second trimester aligns with NHS guidance and means the most sensitive period of fetal development has passed.

## Hair Treatments During Pregnancy

### Keratin Straightening Treatments

Keratin treatments are a different matter from standard hair dye. Many keratin formulations contain formaldehyde — or compounds that release formaldehyde when heated — at levels that exceed what is considered safe for repeated occupational exposure. Formaldehyde is classified as a known carcinogen, and there are concerns about its effects during pregnancy.

The NHS and occupational health guidelines advise against keratin treatments during pregnancy, and hairdressers who are pregnant are advised to avoid performing them professionally. If you are considering a keratin treatment, this is one to discuss with your midwife and delay until after delivery.

### Bleach

Bleach used for full-head lightening involves hydrogen peroxide at higher concentrations than standard colour. The guidance mirrors that for permanent dye: waiting until after 12 weeks is recommended, good ventilation is essential, and scalp contact should be minimised. Highlights that use bleach but keep it off the scalp are a more cautious approach.

### Perms and Relaxers

Chemical perms and relaxers change the hair's protein structure. The chemicals involved — including ammonium thioglycolate in perms and sodium hydroxide or guanidine in relaxers — have not been studied as extensively in human pregnancy as hair dyes. Given the limited evidence and the fact that these treatments involve significant scalp contact and fume exposure, most midwives advise waiting until after the first trimester at minimum, and discussing with your healthcare provider before proceeding.

## What to Do If You Coloured Your Hair Before Knowing You Were Pregnant

This is one of the most common anxieties raised in antenatal appointments. Many people colour their hair in early pregnancy before they have a confirmed positive test, often without knowing the pregnancy exists.

The evidence is reassuring here. A single hair colouring treatment in early pregnancy — before you knew you were pregnant — is very unlikely to have caused harm. The exposure from a single salon visit or home colour is small. If this applies to you, speak with your midwife for personalised reassurance, but there is no reason to assume something has gone wrong.

## When to Speak with Your Midwife

Discuss hair colouring with your midwife if:

- You colour your hair very frequently (every 3–4 weeks) and want to understand whether reducing the frequency is advisable
- You experience scalp irritation or an allergic reaction after a treatment
- You are considering a keratin or relaxer treatment
- You have any skin conditions that affect the scalp
- You work in a hairdressing environment with regular chemical exposure

Your midwife can offer guidance tailored to your individual health history and is the right point of contact for any concerns that are specific to your pregnancy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it safe to dye hair in the first trimester?**

The NHS recommends waiting until after 12 weeks if possible, as the first trimester is when major fetal development takes place and it is sensible to reduce any unnecessary chemical exposure. That said, the actual risk from a single hair colouring treatment in the first trimester is considered low. Discuss with your midwife if you are concerned.

**Are highlights safer than all-over colour during pregnancy?**

Yes, in terms of scalp exposure. Highlights and balayage techniques apply colour to the hair lengths away from the scalp, which significantly reduces the amount of chemical that can be absorbed. Most midwives and hair professionals consider them a lower-risk choice during pregnancy.

**Can hair dye affect the baby's development?**

Animal studies using very high doses showed potential harm, but human evidence at the exposure levels involved in normal hair colouring has not demonstrated a clear risk to fetal development. This does not mean the risk is zero, but it is considered small enough that the NHS deems hair dyeing safe after the first trimester with appropriate precautions.

**What about henna during pregnancy?**

Pure henna (from the Lawsonia inermis plant) is generally considered safe during pregnancy and has a long history of traditional use. Avoid "black henna," which contains PPD at high concentrations and can cause severe skin reactions.

**Do I need to patch test even if I have used the same dye before?**

Yes. Pregnancy can alter skin sensitivity and immune responses. The NHS recommends patch testing before every colour application, and this is especially important during pregnancy when sensitivity changes are more likely.

**Are keratin treatments safe in pregnancy?**

Most keratin treatments contain formaldehyde or formaldehyde-releasing compounds and are not recommended during pregnancy. This is one treatment where the consensus is clearly to wait until after delivery.

## Key Takeaways

- The NHS considers hair dye generally safe after the first 12 weeks; most guidance recommends waiting until the second trimester as a precaution
- Animal studies raised the original concern, but human evidence at real-world exposure levels is reassuring
- Highlights and balayage involve significantly less scalp contact than all-over colour, making them a lower-exposure option
- Always patch test before colouring during pregnancy — sensitivity can change
- Work in a well-ventilated space and rinse the scalp thoroughly after any treatment
- Keratin treatments containing formaldehyde are not recommended during pregnancy; discuss with your midwife before proceeding
- If you coloured your hair before knowing you were pregnant, a single treatment is very unlikely to have caused harm — speak with your midwife for personalised reassurance
