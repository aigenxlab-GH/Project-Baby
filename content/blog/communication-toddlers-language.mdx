# Toddler Language Development: How Children Learn to Talk

Language development in the toddler years follows a predictable arc, even though the pace varies considerably from child to child. Understanding what drives that development — and what the research says about what helps — gives parents a clearer basis for decision-making than generic reassurances that "all children are different."

## Receptive Before Expressive

The most important thing to understand about early language is that children understand far more than they can say. This distinction — between receptive language (comprehension) and expressive language (speaking) — matters because it means a quiet toddler is not necessarily a child with language difficulties. A 14-month-old who responds accurately to "where's your nose?" or "go and get your shoes" has robust receptive language even if they are not yet producing many words. Concerns arise when both receptive and expressive development are delayed, or when there is regression in either.

## What Language Development Looks Like Month by Month

By 12 months, most children have one to three recognisable words used consistently with meaning. "Dada" said while looking at their father is different from "dada" said as a general babble sound — the former counts as a word, the latter does not.

By 18 months, the AAP and NHS expect most children to have a vocabulary of 10 to 20 words. Pointing to show interest, rather than just to request, is established by this age. Children this age also follow simple two-step instructions reliably.

By 24 months, most children have at least 50 words and are beginning to combine two words into short phrases: "more juice," "daddy gone," "big dog." The WHO notes that this two-word combination stage is a significant marker of grammatical development beginning, distinct from simply accumulating vocabulary.

By 30 months, vocabulary has typically expanded to 200 or more words, and strangers should be able to understand about 75% of what the child says. Sentences grow to three and four words, and questions begin to appear.

By 36 months, three-word sentences are the norm and speech is clear to most people outside the family. Narratives — simple accounts of recent events — begin to emerge.

## What Drives Language Growth

The most consistently supported driver of early language development is what researchers call "serve and return" conversation: the child makes a sound, gesture, or comment, and the adult responds, then the child responds again. This back-and-forth, even before children have many words, builds the neural pathways that support language. NHS speech and language therapy services consistently recommend narrating daily activities ("now I'm pouring the water," "you're putting on your coat") as one of the simplest and most effective strategies available to parents.

Reading aloud matters. Children who are read to regularly develop larger vocabularies, better phonological awareness, and stronger comprehension, even when the reading sessions are short. The books do not need to be educational — any picture book that sparks a genuine "what's that?" conversation is doing its job.

Limiting passive screen time in the under-twos has a research basis. The AAP recommends avoiding screens other than video calls for children under 18 months, and limiting high-quality co-watched content to one hour per day for children aged 2 to 5. The mechanism is straightforward: screen time displaces the serve-and-return interaction that language acquisition depends on.

## What Does Not Help

Exaggerated baby talk — the kind that involves deliberate mispronunciation and invented words — does not support language development. Simplified vocabulary and short sentences are appropriate and helpful; saying "baba" instead of "bottle" is not. Correcting and drilling ("say bottle, come on, say bottle") creates pressure that tends to reduce, not increase, a child's willingness to attempt new words. Modelling the correct form naturally ("yes, you want the bottle") is more effective.

## Bilingual Children

Children growing up with two or more languages may start producing words slightly later than monolingual peers, but research consistently shows they catch up rapidly. Mixing languages — code-switching — is normal and not a sign of confusion. Parents should not be encouraged to abandon their home language; bilingualism is a cognitive asset and a cultural one.

## When to Seek an Assessment

The following signs warrant referral to a speech and language therapist (SALT) without delay:

- No words at 16 months
- No two-word phrases by 24 months
- Any loss of language skills previously present, at any age

Regression — a child who had words and has stopped using them — is the most urgent of these. It should trigger prompt medical review, not reassurance that development is variable. In the NHS, a GP referral to community SALT services is the standard route, though many areas also accept self-referrals directly from parents. A SALT assessment identifies whether delay is isolated to speech production, involves broader language comprehension, or is associated with other developmental differences that warrant further investigation.

Early intervention in speech and language difficulties is significantly more effective than later intervention. If there is doubt, an assessment early is always preferable to waiting.
