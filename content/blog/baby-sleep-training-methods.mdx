Every parent eventually reaches the point where something needs to change about sleep. Waking every 45 minutes, or spending 90 minutes settling your baby every night, takes a real toll on physical and mental health.

Sleep training can help — but with so many methods and such strong opinions, it's hard to know where to start. Here's an honest, research-grounded overview of all the major approaches.

## What Is Sleep Training?

Sleep training is the process of helping your baby learn to fall asleep independently at bedtime — and return to sleep independently when they naturally wake between sleep cycles during the night.

It does **not** mean forcing your baby to sleep, ignoring their needs, or abandoning them. Every method involves your presence and responsiveness — they differ in how that presence is expressed.

## Who Is Ready for Sleep Training?

Before starting any method, confirm:
- Your baby is **at least 4 months old** (corrected age for premature babies)
- Your baby is **healthy and gaining weight well**
- Your baby no longer **needs night feeds for nutrition** (confirm with your paediatrician)
- Your baby's sleep environment is **safe** (firm flat surface, on their back, room sharing)
- You and your partner are **aligned and consistent** — inconsistency is worse than no training

## Method 1: Graduated Extinction (Ferber Method)

**How it works:** Put baby down awake. Check in at gradually increasing intervals (3 min, 5 min, 10 min...) without picking up or feeding. Intervals increase over several nights.

**Best for:** Parents who can tolerate some crying with check-ins; babies who self-soothe once they learn how

**Timeline:** Significant improvement typically in 3–7 nights

**Research says:** One of the most-studied methods; consistent evidence of effectiveness and no long-term harm

**Full guide:** [The Ferber Method Explained](/parenting/sleep/ferber-method-explained)

## Method 2: Full Extinction (Weissbluth / "CIO")

**How it works:** Put baby down awake. Leave the room. Do not return until morning (or a set wake time). No check-ins.

**Best for:** Parents who find check-ins increase their baby's distress; babies who are more upset by brief parent appearances

**Timeline:** Often the fastest method — many babies adapt in 2–4 nights

**Research says:** Same evidence base as Ferber; effective and no evidence of harm. Harder for parents than for babies in most cases.

**Caveat:** Requires complete parental commitment and agreement. Any inconsistency makes it significantly harder.

## Method 3: Chair Method (Sleep Lady Shuffle)

**How it works:** Sit in a chair next to the cot until baby falls asleep. Every few nights, move the chair further away — to the middle of the room, then the doorway, then outside. Baby falls asleep with your presence but without being held.

**Best for:** Parents who cannot tolerate any crying; babies with significant separation anxiety

**Timeline:** 2–3 weeks to complete the process

**Research says:** Less studied than extinction methods; effective for many families; slower process

**Caveat:** Some babies become more upset with a parent present but not responding. If this is your baby, Ferber may work better.

## Method 4: Pick Up/Put Down (PUPD)

**How it works:** When baby cries, pick them up and comfort until calm. Put back down. Repeat as needed until asleep.

**Best for:** Younger babies (4–6 months); parents who want to comfort without creating a long-term feeding association

**Timeline:** Variable — can take 1–2 hours per night initially; improves over 1–2 weeks

**Research says:** Less formal research; works for some babies but can be overstimulating for others (makes them more awake, not less)

**Caveat:** Can be exhausting if your baby is frequently put down and picked up. Works best with younger babies.

## Method 5: Fading

**How it works:** Gradually reduce the level of parental involvement over time without a sharp withdrawal. If you currently feed to sleep, you reduce the feed duration slightly each night. If you rock to sleep, you gradually reduce the rocking. The sleep association is faded rather than removed abruptly.

**Best for:** Families who want a gradual transition; parents who can commit to slow, incremental change

**Timeline:** 3–6 weeks depending on how much the sleep association needs to change

**Research says:** Limited formal study, but the approach is grounded in classical conditioning principles and is widely used in paediatric sleep psychology. Less crying than extinction methods, more time than them too.

**Caveat:** Requires very consistent small steps. If the nightly change is too small, progress stalls. If it is too large, the baby resists.

## Method 6: No-Cry Sleep Solutions

Elizabeth Pantley's approach focuses on gradually changing sleep associations without extinction:

**Key techniques:**
- The "Pantley Pull-Off" — detach from breast or bottle just before fully asleep, repeatedly until baby accepts falling asleep without the prop
- Consistent sleep cues and routine
- Gradual reduction of feeding duration

**Best for:** Parents completely opposed to any crying; families with flexible timelines

**Timeline:** 6–8+ weeks to see full results

**Research says:** Limited formal research; results are more gradual and less predictable than extinction methods

**Caveat:** Requires enormous patience and consistency. Some families find it works beautifully; others find the extended timeline makes sleep deprivation worse.

## Method Comparison at a Glance

Choosing a method is easier when the key differences are laid out side by side. The table below summarises the six main approaches across five dimensions that matter to most families. Note that timelines and crying levels are approximations — every baby responds differently.

| Method | Typical timeline | Parental involvement | Age range | Crying expected | Evidence base |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Graduated Extinction (Ferber) | 3–7 nights | Timed check-ins, no pick-up | 4 months+ | Moderate; reduces nightly | Strong; multiple RCTs |
| Full Extinction (CIO) | 2–4 nights | Leave at bedtime; no returns | 4 months+ | High initially; drops quickly | Strong; same body of evidence as Ferber |
| Chair Method / Shuffle | 2–3 weeks | Present in room; gradually withdraw | 5 months+ | Low to moderate | Moderate; fewer formal studies |
| Pick-Up/Put-Down | 1–2 weeks | High — repeated pick-ups | 4–6 months (less effective older) | Moderate; variable | Limited formal research |
| Fading | 3–6 weeks | High; gradually reducing | 4 months+ | Low | Limited; based on conditioning principles |
| No-Cry | 6–8+ weeks | High throughout | 4 months+ | Minimal | Limited; anecdotal and small studies |

The evidence base for extinction methods (Ferber and full CIO) is the strongest. A landmark 2016 randomised controlled trial published in *Pediatrics* by Gradisar et al. found that both graduated and full extinction produced faster sleep onset and fewer night wakings than control conditions, with no detectable differences in cortisol levels, stress hormones, or attachment security at 12-month follow-up. The AAP acknowledges these findings while noting that parental choice and comfort with a method matters significantly to how consistently it is applied.

## The Bedtime Routine: Non-Negotiable for Any Method

Regardless of which method you choose, a consistent bedtime routine is the foundation:

1. Bath (or warm wash)
2. Massage or lotion
3. Pyjamas and sleeping bag
4. Final feed (not to sleep if sleep training)
5. Story or song
6. Into cot awake — with goodnight words

The routine should take 20–30 minutes and be the same every night. Predictability signals your baby's brain that sleep is coming.

## Setting Up the Sleep Environment

- **Darkness:** Blackout blinds are highly effective for both naps and night sleep
- **White noise:** A white noise machine helps mask household sounds and creates a consistent sleep association. See our review of the [LectroFan White Noise Machine](/products/white-noise/lectrofan-classic-review)
- **Temperature:** 16–20°C (60–68°F) is ideal
- **Sleep sack:** A sleep bag appropriate for room temperature replaces loose blankets safely

## How to Choose the Right Method

| If you... | Consider... |
|-----------|------------|
| Can handle some crying with reassurance check-ins | Ferber/Graduated Extinction |
| Find check-ins make baby more upset | Full Extinction |
| Cannot handle any crying | Chair Method or No-Cry |
| Have a younger baby (4–6 months) | PUPD or Ferber |
| Want the fastest results | Full Extinction |
| Have tried other methods without success | Full Extinction |

The "best" method is the one you can implement **consistently**. An imperfectly implemented gentle method is less effective than a consistently implemented firmer one.

## Managing the Difficult Nights

The first 1–3 nights of any sleep training method are the hardest. Things to do:

- **Have a plan and commit to it** — decide in advance what you'll do if crying lasts 45 minutes
- **Tag team with your partner** — take turns so neither of you is alone with it
- **Keep a sleep log** — see objectively that things are improving (even when it doesn't feel like it)
- **Trust the process** — it almost always gets better after night 3

## After Sleep Training: Maintaining Good Sleep

Sleep training is not a one-time fix. Maintain good sleep habits by:
- Keeping the bedtime routine consistent
- Watching for [sleep regressions](/parenting/sleep/sleep-regressions-guide) at 8–10 months and 18 months
- Putting baby down awake (the core skill they've learned)
- Not reverting to old associations when travelling or during illness — or at least having a plan to return to independent settling afterwards

Whatever method you choose, a well-rested family is a better-functioning family — and a well-rested baby is a healthier, happier baby.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I switch methods if the first one isn't working?**

Yes, but give any method at least five to seven consistent nights before evaluating. Switching too quickly prevents the pattern from being established and often leaves families more exhausted. If after a solid week there is no progress whatsoever, it is reasonable to reassess.

**What if my baby cries until they vomit?**

This is distressing but is more common than parents expect during the first few nights. Clean up calmly, resettle with minimal fuss, and continue. Paediatricians generally advise that a brief comforting response to clean up is appropriate and does not undermine training. The vomiting typically stops after the first couple of nights.

**Does sleep training work for breastfed babies?**

Yes. Sleep training addresses the sleep association (how the baby falls asleep), not the feeding relationship. Many breastfed babies sleep train successfully while continuing to breastfeed. You may choose to keep one overnight feed if your baby still needs it nutritionally — the skill being trained is falling asleep independently, not eliminating all feeds.

**My baby has started sleep training but keeps getting ill. Should I pause?**

It is reasonable to offer extra comfort during illness — sick babies need responsive care. Once well, return to your method promptly. Most families find they need a night or two to re-establish the pattern, rather than starting from scratch.

**Is there evidence that sleep training harms babies emotionally?**

Current evidence does not support long-term harm. A five-year follow-up study (Price et al., 2012, published in *Pediatrics*) found no differences in emotional or behavioural outcomes, sleep, stress, or parent-child attachment between children who underwent behavioural sleep interventions and those who did not.

**What if my partner and I disagree about sleep training?**

This is very common and is worth resolving before starting, not during. Inconsistency between caregivers — where one responds and one does not — is genuinely worse for the baby than either approach applied consistently. Discuss, read together, and agree on a method and a commitment before the first night.

## Key Takeaways

- Sleep training begins when a baby has the neurological maturity to self-soothe, typically from four months, and should be confirmed appropriate with your paediatrician
- Extinction methods (Ferber and full CIO) have the strongest evidence base; gentler methods work for many families but require more time
- The method comparison table shows that faster results typically involve more crying, while lower-crying approaches require longer timelines — neither is universally superior
- Consistency is the single most important factor: any method applied inconsistently is less effective than a consistently applied alternative
- Long-term research shows no evidence of harm to attachment, emotional wellbeing, or stress hormones from any validated sleep training approach
- A predictable bedtime routine is the non-negotiable foundation that supports every method equally
