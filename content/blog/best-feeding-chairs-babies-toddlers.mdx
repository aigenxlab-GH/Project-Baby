# Best Nursing and Feeding Chairs 2026: What to Look For and Top Picks

A nursing or feeding chair is not a strictly necessary purchase. Plenty of parents feed successfully from a sofa, a bed, or an armchair. But a chair designed for the purpose has features that make a real difference over the course of six to twelve months of frequent feeds — including multiple feeds in the night when support and stability matter most.

This guide explains what those features are, who needs them, how to evaluate the options across different price points, and which specific chairs are worth considering in 2026.

## Do You Need a Dedicated Nursing Chair?

The case for a dedicated chair is strongest if you are planning to breastfeed. Breastfeeding requires the parent to maintain a stable, supported position — with forearms supported, back aligned, and ideally feet slightly elevated — for sustained periods multiple times per day and night. Doing this from an unsupportive surface creates back, shoulder, and neck strain that accumulates over weeks. Physiotherapists who work with postpartum patients frequently cite poor feeding posture as a contributor to the upper back pain common in new mothers.

If you are formula-feeding or combination-feeding, the ergonomic argument is softer but still present: a dedicated chair in the nursery means feeds happen in a calm, consistent environment, away from the stimulation of living spaces, which can help with night settling.

The honest caveat is that any comfortable, well-supported chair — including a quality armchair — can serve the purpose if the dimensions are right. What matters is the specific combination of features, not whether the product is marketed as a nursing chair.

## Key Features to Prioritise

### Back Support

Lumbar and mid-back support are the most important ergonomic features. Breastfeeding positions place sustained load on the lower back. A chair that does not support the lumbar curve pushes the sitter into a slouched position, which causes the shoulders to round forward and the neck to extend downward. After a few weeks of three-hour night feeds, this creates significant cumulative strain.

The back of the chair should maintain contact with the mid and lower back throughout the sitting position — not just when you lean fully back. Chairs that curve away from the lower back when you sit upright are not suitable for sustained feeding use.

### Arm Height

The arms of the chair need to be at a height that allows the forearms to rest flat and fully supported while holding a baby at breast or bottle height. Arms that are too low — a common issue with general armchairs — mean the parent's arms cannot rest supported, and the entire weight of the baby must be held by the shoulder and arm muscles throughout the feed.

The ideal arm height brings the forearms parallel to the floor when seated, with a nursing pillow used if needed to bring the baby level with the breast or bottle.

### Footstool

A footstool that raises the feet by 15 to 25 cm changes the geometry of the feeding position significantly. With the feet elevated, the knees come up, the thighs support more of the baby's weight, and the pressure on the lower back is reduced. For shorter parents especially, a footstool matched to the chair height is not optional — it is functional.

Most dedicated nursing chairs are sold with a matching footstool. If you are buying a non-specialist chair for feeding, budget separately for an appropriate footstool.

### Motion Type

Motion is commonly included in nursing chairs as a soothing mechanism for both parent and baby. The two main types are:

**Glider motion** — a forward-and-backward glide on a bearing mechanism. Gliders move smoothly and quietly, stop quickly when you stop pushing, and present no risk of the chair travelling across the floor. The mechanism is enclosed and not accessible to a young child's fingers, making it safer when children become mobile.

**Rocking chair motion** — a curved rocker beneath the chair legs. Rocking chairs provide a more variable, responsive motion but can travel across hard floors if not used carefully, and a rocked-forward position on a rocker presents a different body geometry than a glider in the same position. Some parents prefer the more natural feel of a rocking chair; others find the controlled mechanism of a glider easier to use while holding a baby.

Both motion types are effective. Gliders are generally favoured in purpose-built nurseries; rocking chairs appear more frequently as part of existing bedroom or living room furniture that doubles as a nursing space.

### Fabric and Cleanability

Upholstered nursing chairs will encounter milk leaks, spit-up, and general infant mess. This is not occasional — it is frequent. A chair with fully wipeable fabric — textured vinyl, microfibre that wipes clean, or a removable washable cover — is significantly more practical than one with a traditional woven upholstery that absorbs stains.

If you are drawn to a chair with fabric that cannot be wiped clean, purchase a separate chair cover or throw that can be washed easily.

## Chairs to Avoid

A few common choices do not work well for sustained feeding use:

**Dining chairs or kitchen chairs** — no arm support, no cushioning, and typically no back support at the right height. Unsuitable for feeding sessions of more than a few minutes.

**Deep sofas without firm back support** — a soft sofa that you sink into removes lumbar support and makes it difficult to maintain a feeding hold. Useful for occasional daytime feeds but not ideal for the night feed routine.

**Any chair you cannot enter and exit one-handed** — during feeds you will frequently have a sleeping baby in one arm. A chair that requires both hands to stand up from — pulling on both arms, or rising from a very low seat — becomes impractical daily.

## Matching Chair to Feeding Method

The features that matter most shift slightly depending on your feeding method.

**Breastfeeding** — Lumbar support and arm height are the highest priorities. A nursing pillow (such as the My Brest Friend or Boppy in the US, or the Widgey or My Brest Friend in the UK) compensates for arm height gaps. Glider motion can help with night settling.

**Bottle feeding** — Slightly less critical but the same ergonomic principles apply. Arm support matters during extended feeds. A chair that reclines slightly can make bottle-feeding a drowsy baby more comfortable.

**Both** — Prioritise the features that are hardest to compensate for: genuine lumbar support and arm height that works for your body dimensions. These cannot be corrected with accessories in the way that baby height at breast can be corrected with a pillow.

## Setting Up the Chair in the Nursery

Position the chair so that you can see the door from your seated feeding position without turning your head — this reduces alertness during night feeds and helps with settling. Adequate lighting nearby that can be dimmed to a low level matters for night feeds without full wake-up. A small side table or shelf within arm's reach for water, burp cloth, phone, and anything else needed without standing is genuinely useful.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a nursing chair or will any armchair work?**

Any chair with good lumbar and arm support, and that allows you to rise with one arm occupied, will serve the purpose. A dedicated nursing chair has features optimised for extended feeding sessions — particularly back support geometry and arm height — that many standard armchairs lack. If you have an existing chair that meets those criteria, it is worth trying before purchasing separately.

**What is the difference between a glider and a rocking chair?**

A glider moves forward and backward on an enclosed bearing mechanism that produces smooth, controlled motion and stops when you stop pushing. A rocking chair moves on curved runners beneath the legs, producing a more variable motion that can travel across the floor. Gliders are generally considered quieter and safer when children become mobile. Rocking chairs are often preferred for their traditional feel and lower cost.

**Is it worth buying a chair with a matching footstool?**

For most parents, yes. The footstool raises the feet, bringing the knees up and reducing pressure on the lower back during feeds. For shorter parents this is particularly important. Chairs sold with matched footstools are designed to work at the correct relative height — buying a mismatched footstool independently may not achieve the same postural benefit.

**How long will I use a nursing chair?**

Most parents use a nursing chair intensively for six to twelve months. After the feeding period, the chair often stays in the nursery as a reading chair for the toddler years. Investing in a chair you will still want in the room when it is no longer needed primarily for feeding is worth considering. Chairs with nursery-appropriate aesthetics that also function as reading or play chairs for the toddler stage represent better long-term value.

**Are wipeable fabrics comfortable?**

Textured vinyl and wipeable microfibre have improved significantly in quality over recent years. Most modern wipeable nursing chairs are comfortable for sustained sitting — the days of sticky, hot vinyl are largely behind the mainstream nursery furniture market. If you are unsure, visit a stockist to sit in the chair before purchasing.

## Key Takeaways

- Lumbar support and arm height are the most critical features in a nursing or feeding chair — these cannot be compensated for with accessories in the way that baby height can be adjusted with a nursing pillow.
- A matching footstool is functionally important, particularly for shorter parents, as it reduces lower back load by raising the knees during feeding.
- Gliders offer smoother, quieter, more controlled motion than rocking chairs and present less risk of floor travel or child access to the mechanism.
- Choose wipeable fabric — spit-up and milk are constant companions for the first six to twelve months.
- The IKEA POANG with cushion is a practical budget option; the Ickle Bubba Nursing Chair represents the best value among dedicated nursing chairs in the UK market.
- Position the chair so you can see the door from your seated position, with dimmable lighting and a small side table within reach — the environment matters for calm night feeds.
