# Teaching Toddlers to Share and Take Turns: What the Research Says

Every parent has lived through it. The playdate is going well until one child picks up a toy the other wants. Within seconds there is screaming, grabbing, tears. Your instinct is to intervene with "You need to share." But developmental science tells a more complicated story — and understanding it makes the whole thing far less frustrating.

## Why Toddlers Cannot Truly Share Yet

Genuine sharing requires three cognitive abilities that toddlers are still developing.

The first is theory of mind — the ability to understand that another person has thoughts, feelings, and desires that are different from your own. Most children do not develop reliable theory of mind until age 3 or 4. Before that point, a toddler simply cannot consistently grasp that the other child also wants the toy and has feelings about it.

The second is impulse control. The prefrontal cortex, the part of the brain responsible for regulating impulses and delaying gratification, is not remotely mature in a two-year-old. Expecting a toddler to willingly hand over something they want requires exactly the kind of self-regulation their brain is not yet wired to produce.

The third is trust that the object will come back. Toddlers live very much in the present. When something leaves their hands, it may as well be gone forever. The abstract reassurance that "you'll get it back in a minute" does not land the way it does for an older child.

None of this means your child is selfish or badly behaved. It means they are neurologically typical. The AAP notes that parallel play — playing alongside rather than cooperatively with other children — is developmentally normal through age two and even into age three. True cooperative play, which requires the give-and-take of sharing, comes later.

## Sharing vs Turn-Taking: An Important Distinction

These two terms are often used interchangeably, but they describe very different things.

Sharing means giving something up — handing over your toy so another child can use it while you wait. This is genuinely hard for toddlers and should not be the primary goal in the early years.

Turn-taking means each person gets a go in sequence. The toy stays in play; it just moves between children in an ordered way. This is far more achievable because the child can see the toy, understand that it is coming back, and engage with a concrete structure rather than an abstract idea.

When you reframe the goal from "share" to "take turns," you lower the developmental bar to something within your toddler's reach.

## What Forced Sharing Actually Teaches

Research consistently shows that forcing a child to hand over a toy they are currently using does not teach generosity. It does not build empathy. What it teaches, instead, is that adults have the authority to remove things from children's hands — which undermines the child's sense of ownership and safety, and can increase hoarding behaviour rather than reducing it.

A study published in the journal Psychological Science found that children who were prompted to give away stickers (forced to share) were actually less likely to share spontaneously than children who chose to give stickers away voluntarily. Coercion and genuine generosity pull in opposite directions.

The better goal is to create conditions in which sharing and turn-taking feel natural, safe, and rewarding — not compelled.

## What Actually Helps

### Turn-Taking with a Timer

This is one of the most effective tools for toddlers because it is concrete, fair, and predictable. Set a visual timer for two to three minutes. When it goes off, it is the next child's turn. Crucially, the timer — not you — is the authority. This removes the parent from the position of adjudicator, which reduces resentment.

Say: "Two more minutes, then it's Mia's turn." Then step back and let the timer do the work.

### Acknowledge Ownership Honestly

Many parenting scripts require children to share toys they own. But if a toy belongs to your child, they do not have to share it. Acknowledging this is actually more effective than pretending otherwise.

You might say: "That's your truck. You don't have to let Leo use it right now. You can choose to give him a turn when you're ready."

This approach, backed by research from the University of Chicago, consistently produces more voluntary sharing than demands. Children who feel their ownership is respected are more willing to extend access voluntarily.

At playdates, consider putting away a small number of genuinely special toys before guests arrive — toys the child finds particularly hard to share. Then the remaining toys are available for turn-taking without that particular emotional charge.

### The Parallel Resources Approach

One of the simplest interventions with the most evidence behind it: provide two of the same item. Two shovels at the sandbox. Two ride-on toys. Two identical stacking cups. Research shows conflict over toys drops dramatically when resources are duplicated. You are not teaching sharing this way, but you are reducing the number of conflicts while children are still developing the capacity to manage them.

### Descriptive Praise When It Happens

When a child spontaneously hands something to another child or waits for their turn unprompted, name it specifically. Not "good sharing" — but "You noticed Leo wanted a turn and you let him have one. That was really kind."

Descriptive praise helps children build an internal identity around the behaviour ("I'm someone who shares") rather than just performing for the praise itself.

### Scripts for the Moment of Conflict

When two children both want the same toy, try:

- "It looks like you both want the blue car. Let's use the timer. Finn, you have it for two minutes, then it's Amara's turn."
- "I can see you're not done with that yet. How many more minutes do you need before you pass it over?"
- "You both want a turn. Let's find something else for Leo to use while he waits."

Avoid: "Be nice and share." It is vague, moralistic, and does not give the child a concrete action.

## Age-Appropriate Expectations

It helps to calibrate what you can reasonably expect at each stage.

At 18 months, children can hand an object to an adult when asked and understand a simple "your turn / my turn" back-and-forth. Sharing with peers is not realistic.

At 2 years, children are beginning to understand the concept of turns and may comply with turn-taking when an adult mediates actively. Spontaneous sharing is rare but not impossible.

At 3 years, most children can take turns with coaching and a timer. They understand the idea that the toy comes back. They still need adult support to manage the waiting.

At 4 years, children can share more independently and are beginning to factor in fairness — "I had it longer, so it's your turn now." This is when true cooperative play becomes more consistent.

The WHO notes that social-emotional development follows a broad trajectory, and individual variation is significant. A three-year-old who struggles with turn-taking is not behind — they may simply be at the earlier end of a normal range.

## Setting Up Playdates for Success

Rather than setting up conditions that maximise conflict and then managing the fallout, it is worth arranging playdates with sharing challenges in mind.

Keep the group small. Two toddlers is easier than four.

Provide side-by-side activities where parallel play is natural: playdough at the table, a sensory bin with enough tools for both children, a water play table with two sets of cups and jugs.

If conflict does arise, stay close enough to mediate but far enough not to hover. Hovering creates an expectation of adult intervention that actually reduces children's ability to work through minor conflicts themselves.

## When to Seek Advice

If your child is significantly past four years old and still cannot manage any turn-taking even with adult support, and if this is combined with other social communication challenges, it is worth raising with your GP or health visitor. Difficulties with theory of mind can sometimes indicate additional support needs that are worth exploring early.

For the vast majority of toddlers, though, the difficulty with sharing is simply developmental. It resolves with time, modelling, and structure — not with pressure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**At what age should I start expecting my child to share?**

True voluntary sharing is not reliably possible until around age 3 to 4, when theory of mind and impulse control have developed enough to support it. Before that, turn-taking with adult support is a more appropriate goal. Expecting sharing from an 18-month-old will consistently lead to frustration on both sides.

**Does forcing a toddler to share help them learn generosity?**

Research suggests not. Studies on voluntary versus coerced sharing consistently show that children who are forced to give things away are less likely to share spontaneously in future. Genuine generosity develops through autonomy, not compulsion.

**Should I let my child bring their favourite toy to a playdate?**

Many parents find it helpful to leave a small number of especially beloved toys at home, or to put them away before guests arrive. This removes the highest-conflict items from the equation. Toys the child is less attached to are easier to take turns with.

**My child shares fine at nursery but not at home. Is that normal?**

Yes. Children often perform better in structured environments with familiar routines and neutral adults mediating. At home, especially with siblings, the emotional stakes feel higher and the child is more relaxed — which can paradoxically mean less regulated behaviour. This does not indicate a problem.

**How do I handle a child who always grabs from others?**

Stay physically close at playdates so you can intercept grabs before they happen. Offer a substitute: "This toy isn't free right now — but look, this one is." Name the behaviour without shaming: "Grabbing hurts. You can ask for a turn or wait for the timer." Consistency matters more than any single response.

**What if the other parent expects immediate sharing and I disagree?**

This is a genuinely awkward situation. You can acknowledge both children's feelings while introducing turn-taking language without directly contradicting the other parent. If you have a close enough relationship, it is worth having the conversation about developmental expectations away from the children — the research is clear enough that most parents find it reassuring rather than challenging.

## Key Takeaways

- True sharing requires theory of mind and impulse control — neither of which is reliably present before age 3 or 4. Toddlers who struggle are developmentally normal.
- Turn-taking (each person gets a go in sequence) is more achievable than sharing and is the appropriate goal for most of the toddler years.
- Forced sharing does not teach generosity and may reduce it. Children share more voluntarily when their ownership is respected.
- A visual timer is one of the most effective tools available: concrete, fair, and removes the parent from the role of adjudicator.
- Providing duplicate items at playdates significantly reduces conflict without requiring turn-taking at all.
- Descriptive praise for spontaneous sharing — naming specifically what the child did and its effect — builds the internal identity of a generous person over time.
