# Toddler Milestones 12–24 Months: What to Expect Month by Month

The second year of life brings more visible change than almost any other period in childhood. A baby who crawls into their first birthday can be running, talking, and demanding their own shoes by their second. Understanding what development looks like across these twelve months helps parents know when things are on track — and when something is worth discussing with a doctor.

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) is clear that milestones are ranges, not deadlines. Children develop at different rates, and hitting a skill two months later than average is rarely cause for alarm. What matters is the direction of progress — skills should accumulate over time, not plateau or disappear.

## 12 Months

Most 12-month-olds are either taking their first independent steps or pulling to stand and walking while holding furniture (called cruising). Both are normal at this age. Language centres on a few meaningful words — "mama" and "dada" used specifically for the right person, not just as sounds. Toddlers this age use pointing to show what they want, wave bye-bye, imitate actions like clapping or banging, and enjoy putting objects into containers and tipping them out. Social referencing — looking at a parent's face to check whether something is safe or interesting — is well established.

## 15 Months

By 15 months, most children are walking independently, though falls are frequent and balance is still developing. Vocabulary grows to between 5 and 10 words. Toddlers this age use a spoon, though messily, and drink from a cup with help. They begin playing near other children (parallel play) without yet interacting with them, and they point not only to get things but to share interest — holding up a toy to show you, for instance. Following simple one-step instructions is reliable at this age.

## 18 Months

Running appears around 18 months, typically with an unsteady, wide-legged gait. Vocabulary expands to between 10 and 25 words. Simple shape sorters and two-piece puzzles become manageable. Children this age can follow a two-step instruction such as "get your shoes and bring them here." Imitating household tasks — sweeping, wiping surfaces, "cooking" — reflects the growing drive to copy the adults around them. Pretend play is beginning but remains simple.

The AAP and NHS both identify 18 months as a key checkpoint. Red flags at this stage that warrant a GP or paediatrician referral include: fewer than six words, no pointing to show interest (as opposed to pointing to get something), and not yet walking independently.

## 21 Months

Motor skills become more coordinated. Kicking a ball forward is typical at this age, as is climbing stairs with support. Vocabulary reaches up to 50 words for many children, though wide variation is normal. Parallel play continues, but toddlers begin watching other children more closely and showing interest in joining in. Short back-and-forth exchanges — pointing, responding, pointing again — lay the groundwork for later conversation.

## 24 Months

By their second birthday, most toddlers run with confidence and can jump with both feet leaving the ground. The vocabulary milestone shifts from individual words to combinations: two-word phrases such as "more milk," "daddy go," and "big dog" indicate that grammar is beginning to develop. Imaginative play takes off — a cardboard box becomes a car, a wooden spoon becomes a microphone. Children this age are also increasingly aware of other people's feelings, though emotional regulation remains limited.

Red flags at 24 months that should prompt a professional assessment include: fewer than 50 words, no two-word phrases, and — most importantly — any loss of skills previously present. Regression in language or motor skills at any age warrants prompt review, not a wait-and-see approach.

## How to Support Development

Research consistently shows that the quality of interaction matters more than any toy or programme. Talking to your toddler throughout the day — narrating what you are doing, naming objects, asking simple questions — builds language more effectively than screen exposure. Reading aloud daily, even briefly, develops vocabulary and attention. Outdoor time supports gross motor development and sensory processing. Unstructured play with simple materials (blocks, containers, sand, water) drives cognitive growth in ways that structured activities cannot replicate.

Paediatricians and health visitors use developmental checks as an opportunity to identify concerns early, when intervention is most effective. If something feels off between scheduled appointments, there is no reason to wait — a phone call to your GP practice is always appropriate.
