# How to Bathe a Newborn Safely: Temperature, Technique, and What to Avoid

Bathing a newborn for the first time is one of those moments that feels simultaneously special and terrifying. A tiny, slippery baby, water at just the right temperature, and the nagging worry that you might be doing something wrong. The good news is that newborn bathing is straightforward once you understand the basic rules — and there are only a few rules that truly matter.

This guide covers everything from when to start proper baths, how to hold your baby safely, the correct water temperature, which products to use, and the non-negotiable safety rules that NHS guidance consistently emphasises.

## When to Start Bathing a Newborn

For the first week or two after birth, a full immersion bath is not recommended. The NHS advises waiting until the umbilical cord stump has dried, shrivelled, and fallen off before placing your baby in water. This usually happens between one and three weeks after birth.

Until the stump falls off, top-and-tailing is the recommended approach. This means washing your baby's face, neck, hands, and nappy area each day using cotton wool and warm water, without submerging them in a bath. It keeps your baby clean without risking infection or prolonged moisture around the umbilical stump.

Once the stump has gone and the area has healed, you can introduce a proper bath. There is no rule that says you must bathe your baby daily — in fact, bathing two to three times per week is entirely sufficient for a newborn. Daily baths can strip the natural oils from newborn skin, which is already adjusting to life outside the womb. On non-bath days, continue with top-and-tailing for the face, neck folds, armpits, and nappy area.

## How to Prepare the Bath

Preparation matters more with a newborn than with any other stage of babyhood. Once your baby is undressed and in your arms, you cannot leave them to fetch something you forgot. Gather everything before you start.

You will need:

- A baby bath tub, or a large plastic tub placed in the main bath
- A non-slip mat if using the full-size bath
- Two or three soft cloths or cotton wool pads
- A pre-warmed hooded towel laid out flat on a safe surface nearby
- Clean nappy, barrier cream, and clothes ready
- Mild, fragrance-free baby wash if you are using any

Position the baby bath on a stable surface at waist height if possible, or place it in the bottom of the empty main bath. Kneeling beside it reduces back strain and gives you full control.

### Getting the Water Temperature Right

The NHS recommends a bath water temperature of 37 to 38 degrees Celsius — this is close to body temperature and will feel warm but not hot when you test it. The correct way to test the water is with your elbow or the inside of your wrist, not your hand. The skin on your hand is much less sensitive to heat than the skin on these areas.

Fill the bath to a depth of approximately 5 to 8 centimetres. This is shallow enough to keep your baby safe while still allowing you to wash them effectively.

Critically: never add hot water while the baby is already in the bath. Hot water poured into a bath that contains an infant can cause scalding even if you intend to mix it in. If the water has cooled and you need to top it up, lift the baby out, add the hot water, re-test the temperature thoroughly, and then return the baby to the bath.

The room itself should be warm — around 20 to 22 degrees Celsius — so your baby does not become cold when they leave the water.

## How to Hold Your Baby in the Bath

Holding a wet, soapy newborn safely is a skill that takes a few baths to feel natural. The most secure and recommended grip is the cross-cradle hold.

With your dominant hand, support your baby's bottom and thighs. With your non-dominant arm, slide it beneath their back so that your hand cups the back of their head and neck firmly. Your forearm runs along their spine. This position keeps their head well above the water at all times.

To lower your baby into the bath, support them in this hold and lower them feet-first. Feet going in first is gentler and less startling than lowering them back-first, and it allows you to feel the water temperature on their feet before the rest of their body is submerged.

Your non-dominant arm stays under their head throughout the entire bath. You wash with your free hand only.

## The Washing Sequence

### Face First

Begin with the face using plain warm water — no soap. Use a separate piece of damp cotton wool for each eye, wiping from the inner corner outward. This prevents cross-contamination between eyes. Use a fresh piece of cotton wool for the rest of the face, working around the nose, cheeks, and chin.

Pay attention to the neck folds and chin area where milk residue accumulates. Gently turn the head to access the folds but keep the rest of your arm firm and secure.

### Body Next

Wash the body, including the armpits, chest, tummy, legs, and between the toes. If you are using baby wash, a very small amount is all that is needed. The NHS advises using plain water for the first month of life to protect the skin's developing barrier. After one month, a mild, fragrance-free wash is fine if you choose to use one.

Clean the nappy area last. For girls, always wipe front to back. For boys, simply wash around the genitals without retracting the foreskin — it is not designed to be pulled back at this age.

### Hair Last

Not every bath needs to include hair washing. Washing your newborn's hair two to three times per week is sufficient.

When you do wash hair, tilt your baby's head gently backward over the water. This directs water away from the face when you rinse. Pour warm water over the scalp with your cupped hand or a small cup, and rinse from the back of the head toward the forehead — never the other way, as water running over the face distresses most newborns and can cause them to jerk suddenly.

Use a tiny amount of mild baby shampoo if desired, and rinse thoroughly.

## Getting Baby Out of the Bath

Lift your baby out using the same cross-cradle hold used to lower them in. Have the pre-warmed towel on a firm, flat surface ready so you can lay them down immediately.

Pat the skin dry rather than rubbing — newborn skin is delicate. Pay particular attention to the skin folds: neck, armpits, behind the knees, groin, and between the fingers and toes. Moisture trapped in folds can lead to irritation or thrush.

If the umbilical cord stump is still present but drying, keep it as dry as possible. Fold the front of the nappy down below the stump to allow air circulation and avoid friction.

## Newborn Skin and Products

Newborn skin is not the same as adult skin. It is thinner, absorbs substances more easily, and its protective acid mantle is still developing. For these reasons, the NHS recommends using plain water for newborn bathing during the first month.

After one month, if you choose to introduce products, look for those labelled as fragrance-free and specifically formulated for newborns. Avoid bubble baths, heavily scented washes, and products containing alcohol. Essential oils — however "natural" — are not appropriate for newborn skin.

Cradle cap, which appears as yellowish crusty patches on the scalp, is extremely common and harmless. Do not scrub or pick at it. A small amount of baby oil applied and left for a short period before bathing can help loosen it gently.

## The Safety Rules That Cannot Be Skipped

Drowning is one of the leading causes of accidental death in children, and it can happen in seconds and in very small amounts of water. The following rules are non-negotiable.

Never leave your baby unattended in the bath, even for one second. If the doorbell rings, if another child calls out, if your phone buzzes — ignore it. Wrap your baby in a towel and take them with you if you must leave the room. There is no legitimate reason to leave an infant alone in water.

Have everything ready before you undress your baby. Once you are committed to the bath, you should not be retrieving items from across the room.

Turn your phone to silent before you start. Even a brief glance at a notification while holding a wet baby over water increases risk.

Bath support seats — the moulded chairs that sit inside a bath or baby bath — are only an additional aid, not a replacement for parental supervision. The NHS and most paediatric safety organisations are explicit: these seats can tip, and a baby sitting in one is not safer than a baby being held. Never treat a bath seat as permission to step away.

Never add hot water while the baby is in the bath.

## After the Bath: Cord Care and Nappy Application

Apply barrier cream to the nappy area before fastening the nappy. Zinc oxide cream provides effective protection against nappy rash. Avoid talcum powder — the NHS advises against it as the fine particles can be inhaled.

Dress your baby in warm layers suitable for the room temperature. Newborns lose heat quickly after bathing, so moving promptly from towel to clothing is important.

If the umbilical cord stump is still healing, keep the area clean and dry, allow air circulation, and fold the nappy below it. Contact your midwife or GP if the area around the stump becomes red, swollen, or smells unpleasant — these are signs of infection.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When can I bathe my newborn for the first time after birth?**

The NHS recommends waiting until the umbilical cord stump has dried and fallen off, which usually happens between one and three weeks. Before that, top-and-tailing with warm water and cotton wool is the appropriate approach.

**How often should I bathe my newborn?**

Two to three times per week is sufficient. Daily bathing is not necessary and can dry out newborn skin by stripping the natural oils. Top-and-tail daily for the face, neck, hands, and nappy area on non-bath days.

**What temperature should newborn bath water be?**

Between 37 and 38 degrees Celsius — close to body temperature. Test with your elbow or inner wrist rather than your hand. The room should be around 20 to 22 degrees Celsius so your baby stays warm when out of the water.

**Can I use baby bath products from day one?**

The NHS advises using plain water only for the first month. After that, a small amount of mild, fragrance-free baby wash or shampoo is acceptable. Avoid bubble baths, products with fragrance, and anything not specifically formulated for newborns.

**What is the safest way to hold a baby in the bath?**

The cross-cradle hold keeps your non-dominant arm under the baby's back and head throughout the bath, with your hand firmly supporting the back of the head and neck. Your free hand does all the washing. Lower the baby in feet first.

**Is it safe to use a baby bath seat?**

Bath support seats are an additional comfort aid only. They are not a safety device and should never be used as a reason to leave the baby unattended. Both the NHS and paediatric safety organisations are clear that constant parental supervision is required at all times during bathing.

## Key Takeaways

- Wait until the umbilical cord stump has fallen off — usually one to three weeks — before giving a full immersion bath; use top-and-tailing until then.
- Bath water should be 37 to 38 degrees Celsius; test with your elbow or inner wrist, never your hand.
- Two to three baths per week is enough for a newborn; daily bathing dries out developing skin.
- Use the cross-cradle hold throughout the bath, lowering feet first, and keep your supporting arm under the head and neck at all times.
- Never leave a baby unattended in the bath, even for a second — have everything prepared before you start.
- Use plain water for the first month; introduce fragrance-free, mild baby wash after that if you choose.
