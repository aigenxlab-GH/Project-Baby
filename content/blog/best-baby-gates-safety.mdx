# Best Baby Gates 2026: What to Use at the Top of Stairs vs Doorways

A baby gate is one of the most safety-critical purchases you will make. Unlike a bouncer or a monitor, getting the wrong type of gate — or installing the right gate incorrectly — can result in a serious fall. The single most important thing to know before you buy: the gate you use at the top of the stairs must be wall-mounted with screws. Pressure-mounted gates at the top of stairs have been responsible for serious injuries and deaths. Everything else in this guide follows from that foundational rule.

## Pressure-Mounted vs Wall-Mounted: The Critical Distinction

### Pressure-Mounted Gates

A pressure-mounted gate uses rubber-tipped expansion bars that press against the walls or door frame to hold the gate in place. No drilling or screws are required.

**Advantages:** Quick to install and remove, no wall damage, can be repositioned easily around the house.

**Where to use them:** At the bottom of stairs, in doorways between rooms, at the entrance to kitchens, or anywhere you want a soft barrier between spaces on the same level.

**Where never to use them:** At the top of stairs. A pressure-mounted gate can be pushed out of position by a baby leaning against it or by gradual use loosening the tension. A fall from the top of a staircase is a medical emergency.

### Wall-Mounted Gates

A wall-mounted gate uses a bracket system that is screwed directly into the wall studs or into a specialist wall anchor. The gate is secured with hardware that cannot be dislodged by force.

**Where to use them:** At the top of every staircase in your home. This is a non-negotiable requirement.

**Trade-off:** Installation requires drilling. If you are renting, you will need to discuss this with your landlord — the safety of your child takes priority, and most landlords will agree when the reason is explained clearly. Wall fixings can be filled and painted over when the gate is removed.

## When to Install Gates

The typical window for crawling onset is seven to ten months, but some babies begin moving significantly earlier through rolling and scooting. Do not wait until you see your baby moving — gates should be installed before mobility begins. The same logic applies to baby-proofing more broadly: it is much easier to do it during pregnancy or in the first few months than to scramble once a mobile baby has already identified every hazard.

## Safety Standards to Look For

In the UK, look for gates certified to **BS EN 1930:2011**. This is the primary European standard for child safety barriers and specifies requirements for gate height, bar spacing, latch design, and resistance to force. Gates that meet this standard will state it on the packaging.

In the US, look for **JPMA (Juvenile Products Manufacturers Association) certification**. JPMA-certified gates have been tested by a third-party laboratory against ASTM International standards. Certification is voluntary, so the absence of a JPMA seal does not necessarily mean a gate is unsafe, but its presence is a positive indicator.

Do not assume that a gate is safe simply because it is sold in a major retailer. Standards vary internationally, and some products sold online do not meet UK or US certification requirements.

## Key Features to Prioritise

**Minimum height of 60 cm (24 inches).** Gates shorter than this can be climbed by determined toddlers.

**One-hand operation.** You will frequently be carrying a baby in one arm when you need to open a gate. A gate that requires two hands to unlatch is a practical problem that leads to parents leaving gates open.

**Auto-close.** A gate that closes behind you automatically significantly reduces the risk of forgetting to close it. This is particularly valuable at the top of stairs.

**Low step-over bar.** Many gates have a bar at floor level that forms part of the frame. A high bar is a trip hazard for parents carrying a baby in low light. Look for gates with the lowest possible threshold bar, or a walk-through design that lifts clear.

**Adjustable width.** Most gates include adjustable panels or extension pieces to fit openings from approximately 60 cm to 90 cm. Measure your opening before buying and check whether extensions are included or must be purchased separately.

**Bar spacing.** The gap between vertical bars should be no more than 45–55 mm (approximately 1.75 inches). Wider gaps create a head entrapment risk.

## Pets and Baby Gates: An Important Note

Baby gates and pet gates are sometimes sold as interchangeable products, and it is true that many pet gates look identical to baby gates. They are not the same. Pet gates are typically not manufactured to infant safety certification standards, do not undergo the same pressure and bar-spacing testing, and may have latch mechanisms that are easier for a determined child to open than a baby gate.

If you have both children and pets, use a gate that is certified to BS EN 1930 or JPMA for child use — it will safely contain most pets as well. Do not go in the other direction and use a pet gate to contain a child.

## Common Installation Mistakes

**Not wall-mounting at the top of stairs.** The most consequential error. Even if a pressure-mounted gate feels solid, it is not appropriate at the top of stairs.

**Mounting into plasterboard rather than studs.** Standard plasterboard fixings will not hold under the lateral force of a child leaning on or falling against a gate. Locate wall studs (a stud finder costs around £10–£20) and screw into them. If studs are not in the right position, use a gate-specific wall anchor kit, which is designed for this load.

**Installing too low.** The gate must be positioned so the bottom bar sits at floor level. A gap under the gate is a climb-through point for persistent toddlers.

**Ignoring extension panel sizing.** If your opening is wider than the gate's standard range and you do not use a correctly-sized extension, the installation will be insecure regardless of the gate quality.

## When Children Start Opening Gates Themselves

Most children figure out standard gate latches by around age four to five, depending on the mechanism's complexity. From this point, the gate functions as a reminder rather than a hard barrier for most children, though some remain an effective deterrent longer.

The developmental milestone that matters here is not latch-opening but safe stair use — by age four, most children can navigate stairs independently with the rail and appropriate supervision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a pressure-mounted gate at the top of stairs if I secure it very tightly?**
No. The design of pressure-mounted gates means they can be displaced by significant lateral force — exactly the kind that a child falling against the gate would generate. Tightening the pressure mechanism does not change this fundamental safety limitation. Wall-mount at the top of stairs without exception.

**How do I install a gate into a banister post rather than a wall?**
Most gate manufacturers sell banister adaptor kits that clamp around a banister post and provide a flat mounting surface for the gate brackets. Check that the kit is compatible with your specific gate model and your banister diameter. These adaptors are specifically designed for this purpose and are safe when used correctly.

**What width extensions do I need?**
Measure the opening at the widest point from wall to wall (or wall to banister). Most standard gates cover approximately 60–90 cm without extensions. Extensions are typically sold in 7 cm or 9 cm increments. If your opening is between standard extension sizes, round up and trim if necessary — or choose a gate with a wider adjustable range.

**Do I need a gate at the bottom of stairs as well as the top?**
A gate at the bottom of stairs prevents climbing access and is recommended, particularly for young toddlers who can climb quickly. However, the safety priority is the top of stairs — a fall from the top is far more dangerous than a supervised climb attempt from the bottom.

**Are retractable baby gates safe?**
Retractable mesh gates can be wall-mounted and meet safety standards. They are more convenient than rigid gates in some locations but typically cannot be used at the top of stairs due to lower strength ratings. Always check the manufacturer's specific guidance and certification for the installation point you have in mind.

**When should baby gates come down?**
There is no fixed age. The right time is when your child can reliably navigate the relevant hazard independently and safely — typically three to five years for stairs, depending on the child. Remove gates when they become an obstacle rather than a protection.

## Original Insight: Gate Installation Location Determines Safety More Than Model Choice

Baby gate articles compare brands and features, but the critical variable for safety is installation type and location. A top-of-stairs gate that is pressure-mounted rather than wall-fixed (even if the gate itself is a good product) is unsafe—a child leaning on or crashing into it can dislodge it, and if a gate fails at the top of stairs, the consequences are catastrophic. Wall-mounted gates screwed into studs are non-negotiable at the top of stairs, full stop. At the bottom of stairs or in doorways, pressure-mounted gates are acceptable. Many parents buy a gate, see that it can mount either way, assume top-of-stairs installation is fine with pressure mounting, and create a genuine hazard. Additionally, the "best" gate is not the same across all locations. A gate that's perfect for a doorway may be inadequate for a banister. An auto-closing feature is helpful for doorways but not essential. Height, stability, and one-hand operation matter more than bells and whistles. Finally, many parents install gates and then leave them indefinitely, even after children grow old enough to navigate stairs safely. Gates become obstacles and create different hazards (younger siblings being trapped, older children jumping over). Remove gates when they're no longer the primary safety solution for your specific child and environment.

## Key Takeaways

- Wall-mounted gates (screwed into studs) must be used at the top of every staircase; pressure-mounted gates are not safe at the top of stairs under any circumstances.
- In the UK, look for BS EN 1930:2011 certification; in the US, JPMA certification is the relevant standard.
- Install gates before your baby starts moving — typically before seven to eight months.
- Key features to prioritise: minimum 60 cm height, auto-close, one-hand operation, low step-over threshold.
- The BabyDan Premier (£85–110) is the best UK wall-mounted gate; the Munchkin Auto Close (£35–55) is the best pressure-mounted option for doorways.
- Pet gates are not manufactured to infant safety standards — always use a baby-certified gate for child containment.
