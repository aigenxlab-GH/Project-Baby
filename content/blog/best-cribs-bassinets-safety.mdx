# Best Cribs and Bassinets 2026: Safety Standards, What to Look For, and Top Picks

A safe sleep space is one of the most consequential purchases you will make before your baby arrives. The NHS and the Lullaby Trust — the UK's leading sudden infant death charity — are explicit: where and how a baby sleeps directly affects the risk of sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Getting this right means understanding the different types of sleep space available, what the safety standards actually require, and how to evaluate individual products against those benchmarks.

This guide covers everything from the difference between a crib, bassinet, and Moses basket to detailed reviews of the best-rated products at a range of price points in 2026.

## Types of Baby Sleep Spaces

Not all infant sleep products are the same. Each type serves a different purpose, fits differently into your home, and suits a different stage of infancy.

### Standard Crib (Cot)

A full-size crib is the most common long-term sleep solution. In the UK, standard cot dimensions are 120 x 60 cm or 140 x 70 cm. A well-made cot will last from birth to around age two or three, when most children transition to a toddler bed. The NHS recommends that babies share a room with their parents — in their own sleep space — for at least the first six months, and ideally the full first year. A full-size cot works for this if your room is large enough.

### Mini Crib

A mini crib uses a smaller footprint than a standard cot, making it better suited to smaller bedrooms or flats. The trade-off is that children outgrow the sleeping surface sooner, typically by 6 to 9 months depending on the child's size. If space is a significant constraint, a mini crib can be a practical solution, but factor in the earlier transition cost.

### Bassinet

A bassinet is a smaller, elevated sleep space — typically mounted on a stand with legs or a frame — designed specifically for the newborn period. Most are suitable for the first three to four months, or until a baby reaches the manufacturer's stated weight limit (usually around 9 kg). Many models include wheels, making it straightforward to move the bassinet between rooms. The elevated height also makes it easier to reach a sleeping newborn without bending deeply, which matters in the early weeks of postpartum recovery.

### Moses Basket

Moses baskets are compact, portable, and typically wicker or fabric-constructed. They sit low on a stand or on the floor and are best suited to the first three to four months. Unlike some bassinets, Moses baskets do not rock or glide. They are a popular choice in the UK because of their lightweight portability — you can carry a sleeping baby from room to room — but they are outgrown quickly and do not offer the longevity of a full-size cot.

### Bedside Crib (Side-Car Crib)

A bedside or side-car crib attaches directly to the adult bed at mattress height, with the side nearest the parent either open or lowered. This design allows a breastfeeding parent to reach the baby without getting out of bed and without bringing the baby into the adult bed — which the NHS advises against due to the associated risk of accidental overlay. A bedside crib is not a substitute for a full-size cot; it covers the same first three to six months that a bassinet covers but does so while keeping the baby directly adjacent to the parent.

## Safety Non-Negotiables

Before evaluating any specific product, understand what the relevant safety standards require.

### Certification Standards

In the UK, cots for domestic use should meet **BS EN 716**, the British and European standard for children's cots. This covers structural integrity, slat spacing, finish, and component safety. In the US, the relevant certification body is the **Juvenile Products Manufacturers Association (JPMA)**, which tests products against American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) standards and Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) regulations. Drop-side cribs — which had a retractable lowering side — were banned in both the US (2011) and UK following deaths attributed to structural failures. Any product offered second-hand with a drop-side mechanism should not be used.

### Mattress Requirements

The mattress is as important as the crib frame. The NHS and the Lullaby Trust are unambiguous on this point: the mattress must be firm and flat. A soft mattress can allow a baby's face to press into it, restricting breathing. The mattress must fit snugly within the cot frame — no gap greater than 2 cm on any side. A gap larger than this creates an entrapment risk.

If you are using a second-hand cot frame, the Lullaby Trust recommends buying a new mattress regardless of the apparent condition of the old one. Mattresses degrade in ways that are not always visible, and previous use — including any milk or urine contamination — creates conditions in which harmful bacteria can develop.

### What Must Not Go in the Sleep Space

The NHS guidance is clear: the only item in the cot or bassinet should be a firm mattress with a single fitted sheet. The following must be excluded:

- Cot bumpers — including the newer "breathable" mesh versions. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) updated its guidance in 2022 to recommend against all cot bumpers. The Lullaby Trust in the UK has maintained this position for many years.
- Duvets, quilts, or any loose bedding
- Pillows
- Positional wedges or sleep positioners
- Soft toys while the baby is sleeping unsupervised

A sleeping bag (baby sleep sack) with a fitted neck and armholes is the recommended alternative to loose bedding and is safe from birth when the appropriate tog rating and size are used.

## What to Look For When Buying

### Height-Adjustable Base

Most full-size cots offer two or three base height settings. Starting at the highest position makes it easier to place a sleeping newborn in the cot without bending far over the rail. As the baby becomes able to sit and then pull to stand, lowering the base prevents climbing out. This adjustment is not a luxury feature — it genuinely affects how manageable the cot is in daily use and reduces back strain for parents during a period when they are already sleep-deprived.

### Slat Spacing

The maximum safe gap between cot slats is 4.5 cm (approximately 1.75 inches). Any wider and there is a risk of a baby's head, limb, or neck becoming trapped. BS EN 716 enforces this in the UK; ASTM standards enforce equivalent spacing in the US.

### Conversion Options

Some cots convert to a toddler bed or junior bed by removing one side and adding a toddler rail. This extends the product's usable life significantly. Whether the conversion option is worth paying for depends on how long you anticipate using the same bedroom furniture. Brands like Boori and Silver Cross build long-term convertibility into several of their ranges.

### Assembly and Quality of Locking Mechanisms

A cot that is difficult to assemble correctly is more likely to be assembled incorrectly. Check reviews specifically for comments about assembly complexity, and ensure any locking or fixing mechanisms are solid once the cot is assembled. Loose components in a sleep structure are a structural risk.

## Original Insight: The Mattress Matters More Than the Cot Frame

Cot reviews often focus on the frame—wood type, finishes, assembly difficulty, aesthetic appeal. But for SIDS prevention and basic infant safety, the mattress is more load-bearing than the cot itself. A premium cot frame with a soft, dipped, or unsuitable mattress carries more risk than a budget cot with the correct mattress. Firmness is the key specification. The mattress must be flat and rigid enough that a newborn's face pressed against it cannot compress the surface significantly. A mattress that passes safety standards in one cot may not work in another if the cot dimensions are slightly different—gaps larger than 2cm between the mattress and cot frame create entrapment hazards. This is why buying a new mattress for a second-hand cot (even if the cot is in excellent condition) is genuinely important. Used mattresses degrade internally in ways that aren't visible. Additionally, the cost of the mattress represents a significant portion of the budget for budget-range cots (a £90 IKEA cot might require a £60-80 mattress), so factor that into your total cost equation. A cot that costs £90 but requires a mattress that costs £75 is actually £165, not £90.

## Buying Second-Hand

A second-hand cot frame in good structural condition is a reasonable option. Check that all components are present and undamaged, slat spacing is within the safe limit, there are no splintered or rough surfaces, and the frame is stable and rigid when assembled. As noted above, always purchase a new mattress regardless of what is offered with the frame.

Second-hand bassinets and Moses baskets carry the same mattress replacement requirement. Given that the bassinets are used for only a few months, the economics of buying second-hand and replacing the mattress can make them very cost-effective.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it safe to use a second-hand crib?**

A cot frame in sound structural condition can be used safely, provided it meets current safety standards (BS EN 716 in the UK; JPMA/ASTM in the US) and has no missing or damaged parts. The mattress must always be new. The Lullaby Trust provides clear guidance on this: never reuse a mattress even if it appears clean and undamaged.

**How long does a baby use a bassinet before moving to a crib?**

Most babies outgrow a bassinet between 3 and 6 months, either by reaching the weight limit or by becoming mobile enough that the shallow-sided bassinet is no longer safe. Transition timing varies by product and by individual baby. Most full-size cot manufacturers state their products are suitable from birth, so the bassinet and cot phases can overlap if preferred.

**Are cot bumpers safe if they are the breathable mesh type?**

No. The AAP updated its safe sleep guidance in 2022 to recommend against all cot bumpers, including breathable mesh versions. The Lullaby Trust has not endorsed any type of cot bumper. The evidence base for any benefit is absent; the evidence of risk from entrapment and strangulation remains.

**What is the correct mattress fit for a cot?**

The gap between the mattress and the cot frame on any side should not exceed 2 cm. A larger gap creates a space where a baby's head, arm, or leg can become trapped. When purchasing a replacement mattress, measure the internal cot dimensions carefully — even within the same cot brand, dimensions can vary slightly between product versions.

**Can a baby sleep in a Moses basket overnight?**

Yes, provided the Moses basket meets safety standards, has a firm flat mattress, and the basket is placed on a stable surface — either its own stand or a firm flat floor. The same sleep environment rules apply: no loose bedding, no bumpers, and the baby placed on their back. Moses baskets are suitable only for the first three to four months of life.

**Do I need both a bassinet and a full-size cot?**

Not necessarily. Many parents use a bedside crib for the first six months and then transition directly to a full-size cot. Others use a Moses basket initially and move to a cot at three to four months. A bassinet or Moses basket is convenient but not obligatory — a full-size cot can be used from birth if room-sharing arrangements allow it.

- A firm, flat mattress with a snug fit (no gap over 2 cm) is non-negotiable; always buy a new mattress, even for a second-hand cot frame.
