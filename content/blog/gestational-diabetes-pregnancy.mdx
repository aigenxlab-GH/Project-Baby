# Gestational Diabetes: Diagnosis, Diet, and What It Means for Your Birth

Gestational diabetes (GD) affects approximately 1 in 20 pregnancies in the UK, making it one of the most common pregnancy complications. According to NICE (the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence), rates are rising alongside increasing rates of obesity and older maternal age. A diagnosis can feel alarming, but the condition is manageable — and understanding it fully puts you in a much stronger position.

## What Gestational Diabetes Is

Gestational diabetes is a type of high blood sugar that develops during pregnancy and usually resolves after birth. During pregnancy, the placenta produces hormones that help the baby grow but also make your cells more resistant to insulin. Normally, your pancreas compensates by producing more insulin. In gestational diabetes, it cannot keep up, and blood glucose rises above safe levels.

It is distinct from pre-existing type 1 or type 2 diabetes, though the management during pregnancy has significant overlap. The condition typically develops in the second or third trimester and is detected through a glucose tolerance test (GTT).

## Who Is Offered the Glucose Tolerance Test

NICE recommends that a GTT be offered to women with one or more of the following risk factors:

- BMI above 30
- Previous gestational diabetes in a prior pregnancy
- A parent, sibling, or child with type 2 diabetes (first-degree family history)
- A previous baby weighing more than 4.5 kg at birth
- South Asian, Black African, African Caribbean, or Middle Eastern family background

The GTT is typically offered between 24 and 28 weeks of pregnancy. If you had gestational diabetes in a previous pregnancy, your midwife will usually offer the test earlier — around 16 to 18 weeks — and then again at 24 to 28 weeks if the first is normal.

The test itself is straightforward but does require planning. You need to fast for 8 to 10 hours beforehand. At the clinic, a fasting blood sample is taken, then you drink a glucose solution (usually 75g), and a second blood sample is drawn two hours later. The results are compared against NICE diagnostic thresholds.

## Understanding the GTT Results

NICE sets specific thresholds for a gestational diabetes diagnosis. A fasting glucose level of 5.6 mmol/L or above, or a two-hour level of 7.8 mmol/L or above, indicates gestational diabetes.

It is worth knowing that some units apply slightly different thresholds based on local protocols, which is why your care team will interpret your results in full context. One abnormal value is sufficient for a diagnosis — you do not need both to be raised.

If your results are in the normal range but you remain at risk, your midwife will monitor you through the rest of pregnancy.

## What Happens After Diagnosis

A diagnosis of gestational diabetes means your care moves to a joint obstetric-diabetic team. This is a specialist clinic that includes obstetricians, specialist midwives, and diabetes nurses. Appointments typically become more frequent from this point.

You will be given a blood glucose monitor to take home and shown how to use it. NICE recommends testing fasting levels before breakfast and one hour after each main meal. The targets are:

- Fasting: 5.3 mmol/L or below
- One hour after eating: 7.8 mmol/L or below

These targets help ensure your baby receives consistent, stable glucose levels rather than the spikes that cause complications.

## Managing Gestational Diabetes Through Diet

Dietary changes are the first-line treatment for most people with gestational diabetes, and for around a third of those diagnosed, diet alone is enough to keep blood glucose within target range.

The goal is not to eliminate carbohydrates entirely. Carbohydrates are the macronutrient that most directly affects blood glucose, so the strategy involves choosing the right types and spreading them across the day.

**Reduce refined carbohydrates.** White bread, white rice, sugary drinks, pastries, biscuits, and fruit juice cause rapid glucose spikes. Swapping to wholegrain alternatives — wholegrain bread, brown rice, oats, lentils — produces a slower, flatter rise in blood sugar.

**Think about glycaemic index.** Low-GI foods release glucose more slowly. Basmati rice has a lower GI than white rice. Sourdough bread has a lower GI than standard white bread. New potatoes have a lower GI than mashed potato. These distinctions matter.

**Pair carbohydrates with protein or fat.** Protein and fat slow glucose absorption. A slice of wholegrain toast eaten with a boiled egg raises blood glucose far less than toast alone.

**Eat regular, evenly spaced meals.** Skipping meals or leaving long gaps between eating can cause blood glucose swings. Three balanced meals plus two or three snacks works well for many people with GD.

**Fruit, while nutritious, needs attention.** Whole fruit is preferable to fruit juice, and spreading fruit across meals rather than eating large amounts at once is sensible. Tropical fruits (mango, pineapple, grapes) tend to raise blood glucose more than berries, apples, or pears.

Your diabetes dietitian or specialist midwife will give personalised guidance based on your monitoring results.

## When Medication Is Needed

If blood glucose remains above target despite two weeks of dietary changes, medication is the next step.

**Metformin** is the first-line oral medication and is safe to take during pregnancy. It works by reducing glucose production in the liver and improving insulin sensitivity. It can cause nausea and diarrhoea initially, particularly at higher doses, and these side effects usually improve over time.

**Insulin** is used when metformin alone is insufficient, or when glucose levels are very high at diagnosis. Despite the needle, many people find insulin easier to manage than expected. It is injected subcutaneously (just under the skin) and does not cross the placenta, meaning it has no direct effect on the baby.

Some people need both metformin and insulin together.

## Risks to the Baby — and Why Monitoring Matters

Uncontrolled gestational diabetes carries real risks for the baby, which is why close monitoring is so important.

**Large for gestational age (macrosomia).** When blood glucose is consistently elevated, the baby receives excess glucose through the placenta and grows faster than expected. A baby weighing over 4 kg at birth can lead to complications during delivery.

**Neonatal hypoglycaemia.** After birth, the baby's blood glucose may drop sharply because their pancreas has been producing large amounts of insulin to cope with high maternal glucose levels. Once the glucose supply via the placenta is cut, their own insulin temporarily drives glucose too low. This is why newborns of mothers with gestational diabetes have blood glucose checks in the hours after birth.

**Stillbirth risk.** Poorly controlled gestational diabetes is associated with an increased risk of stillbirth, particularly in the third trimester. This is why the joint team will offer growth scans and closer monitoring from around 28 to 36 weeks.

## Birth Planning and Induction

NICE recommends that women with gestational diabetes who are on medication, or whose blood glucose has been difficult to control, be offered induction of labour between 38 and 40 weeks. This recommendation exists because the risk of stillbirth increases as pregnancy extends beyond full term when gestational diabetes is present.

If blood glucose has been well controlled through diet alone and the baby's growth is normal, there may be more flexibility around timing — but induction is still typically offered no later than 40 weeks.

During labour, your glucose will be monitored regularly. If you are on insulin, your team will manage your doses carefully to prevent hypoglycaemia during the birth.

## After the Birth

In most cases, blood glucose returns to normal within hours of delivery. Your blood glucose is checked before discharge to confirm this.

However, gestational diabetes is a strong predictor of future type 2 diabetes. NICE recommends a fasting plasma glucose test or HbA1c at 6 to 13 weeks postpartum to check that blood glucose has returned to normal range. This appointment is often missed — it is worth noting it in your calendar now.

The long-term risk is significant. Research indicates that around 50% of people who have had gestational diabetes will develop type 2 diabetes within 10 years. That is a serious statistic, but it is also modifiable. Regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, eating a balanced diet with limited refined carbohydrates, and attending annual glucose checks can substantially reduce that risk.

Breastfeeding is also associated with improved glucose metabolism and is beneficial for both mother and baby in this context.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does gestational diabetes mean I did something wrong during pregnancy?**
No. Gestational diabetes is a metabolic condition related to how the placenta affects insulin resistance. It is not caused by eating too much sugar or by any behaviour during pregnancy. Many people who develop it had a healthy diet throughout.

**Will my baby be born with diabetes?**
No. Gestational diabetes does not cause diabetes in the baby at birth. Neonatal hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar after birth) is a short-term concern that is monitored and treated in hospital. The baby's blood glucose typically stabilises within 24 to 48 hours with feeding.

**Can I still have a vaginal birth with gestational diabetes?**
Yes, in most cases. The mode of birth depends on the baby's size, position, your individual circumstances, and how labour progresses — not simply on the diagnosis of gestational diabetes. Macrosomia (a large baby) increases the rate of caesarean section, but many people with GD give birth vaginally.

**Should I monitor blood glucose at every meal, including snacks?**
NICE guidance focuses on fasting and one-hour post-meal readings after main meals (breakfast, lunch, dinner). You do not typically need to check after every snack, but your team may adjust recommendations based on your individual pattern of results.

**What if my blood glucose is normal on the GTT — can I still develop it later?**
Yes, though it is uncommon. If you have significant risk factors, some units will repeat a GTT at 28 weeks even if the 24-week test was normal. If you develop symptoms of high blood glucose (increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue) later in pregnancy, tell your midwife.

**Is gestational diabetes more likely in a second pregnancy if I had it in the first?**
Yes. NICE identifies a previous diagnosis of gestational diabetes as one of the main risk factors for recurrence. The recurrence rate is estimated at around 30 to 84%, depending on the population studied and how much time has passed between pregnancies.

## Key Takeaways

- Gestational diabetes affects approximately 1 in 20 UK pregnancies and is diagnosed using a glucose tolerance test with NICE thresholds of fasting 5.6 mmol/L or 2-hour 7.8 mmol/L.
- Key risk factors include BMI over 30, previous GD, first-degree family history of type 2 diabetes, previous large baby over 4.5 kg, and certain ethnic backgrounds.
- Dietary management — low GI choices, regular meals, pairing carbs with protein — is first-line treatment and resolves glucose levels in roughly a third of cases.
- Metformin is first-line medication; insulin is added or substituted when metformin alone is insufficient. Both are safe in pregnancy.
- NICE recommends induction between 38 and 40 weeks for those on medication or with poorly controlled glucose, due to increased third-trimester stillbirth risk.
- A fasting glucose or HbA1c at 6 to 13 weeks postpartum is essential, and ongoing lifestyle measures can significantly reduce the 50% ten-year risk of type 2 diabetes.
