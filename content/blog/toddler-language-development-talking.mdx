# How to Help Your Toddler Talk: Language Development Activities That Work

Language development is one of the most closely watched milestones of early childhood, and for good reason. The words a child can say and understand in their first three years lay the foundation for literacy, social skills, and academic achievement. But the gap between what parents are told helps and what the research actually supports is significant.

This guide covers how language develops across the toddler years, the specific activities that genuinely accelerate talking, the well-intentioned approaches that do not work, how bilingual development differs, and the referral pathways available when a child's language raises concern.

## Language Is a Two-Way Process from the Start

The first thing to understand about toddler language is that comprehension always exceeds production. A child who says only ten words may understand a hundred. A child who appears to have no language may be following complex instructions with ease.

This matters because the common tendency to measure language development by counting words a child says misses much of what is actually happening. A toddler who is silent but clearly understands what is said to them is in a very different position from a toddler who neither produces nor understands language. Both need attention, but the picture and the path forward are different.

## How Language Develops: The Typical Trajectory

Language does not develop in a smooth, linear curve. It moves in phases with periods of rapid expansion that can seem to appear almost overnight.

**Birth to 12 months.** Babbling begins from around four to six months. By 12 months, most children can say one to five words, respond to their own name, understand simple words like "no" and "bye-bye," and use gestures such as pointing, waving, or raising their arms to be picked up. Gestures are genuine communication and an important early-language milestone.

**12 to 18 months.** Vocabulary grows slowly at first, typically to between 10 and 20 words by 18 months. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) considers 10 words by 18 months a key milestone. Children at this stage understand far more than they say and follow simple two-step instructions.

**18 to 24 months.** This is the period of the vocabulary explosion. Children who had 20 words at 18 months often have 50 to 200 words by their second birthday. Two-word phrases begin to appear — "more milk," "daddy go," "big dog." According to the AAP, 50 or more words and the beginning of two-word combinations by age two is the target milestone.

**24 to 36 months.** Vocabulary expands rapidly. Three-word sentences emerge and then grow in complexity. By 30 months, most children have around 200 words. By 36 months, they can use sentences of three or more words, follow three-step instructions, and be understood by strangers at least 75 percent of the time.

The NHS sets out similar milestones in its guidance on child development, noting that by three years, children should be talking in short sentences that make sense to people outside the immediate family.

## What Actually Drives Language Development

Decades of research have identified the conditions that most powerfully support language acquisition. They have little to do with specific products or programmes. They have everything to do with the quality of everyday interaction.

### Serve-and-Return Conversations

The most powerful driver of language development is what researchers call "serve-and-return" interaction: a child communicates something (a look, a gesture, a sound, a word), and an adult responds in a way that acknowledges and extends that communication.

When a toddler points at a dog and says "duh!" and the parent responds "yes, a dog — a big fluffy dog, isn't he!" the child's communication has been valued and extended. This back-and-forth exchange, repeated thousands of times across the early years, is the mechanism through which language is built. The quality of these interactions matters more than the quantity of words spoken at the child.

### Parentese

Parentese — the naturally higher-pitched, slower, more melodic speech that adults instinctively use with babies and young toddlers — is not babyish or unhelpful. Research shows that infants' brains are specifically tuned to attend to and process this kind of speech. It provides clearer phonological information and slower processing time.

Parentese is distinct from baby talk with made-up words. Clear pronunciation, simplified vocabulary, exaggerated intonation, and a slower pace are all components of effective parentese. Parents who use it are not being silly — they are using the register that infants and toddlers are neurologically primed to learn from.

### Reading Aloud from Birth

There is no minimum age for reading to children. The benefits of shared book reading begin from birth and compound over time. By the time they reach school age, children who have been read to regularly have heard millions more words than those who have not, and they have heard them in a rich variety of grammatical structures, contexts, and narrative forms.

The AAP recommends reading aloud to children from birth. The NHS similarly encourages daily reading as part of early language support.

### Narrating Daily Activities

Talking through what you are doing as you do it — changing a nappy, making lunch, walking to the park — provides a continuous stream of language linked directly to experience. "I'm putting your left shoe on first. Now the right one. There — shoes are on!" This kind of running commentary is not compulsory or exhausting if it becomes a natural habit, and it significantly increases the child's word exposure in meaningful, contextualised ways.

## Activities That Genuinely Accelerate Language

### Shared Book Reading with Questioning

Reading together with interactive dialogue is substantially more effective than reading aloud alone. The technique — sometimes called dialogic reading — involves asking "what" and "where" questions as you read ("What's that animal? Where is the rabbit going?"), expanding on the child's answers, and following their lead in what interests them on each page.

Even a toddler who cannot answer verbally benefits from the modelling of question-and-answer exchange. Over time, they begin to fill the turns themselves.

### Expanding the Child's Utterances

When a toddler says "dog," the most effective language-teaching response is not to say "yes, say dog again" — it is to expand: "Yes, a big brown dog. The dog is running fast." This technique, called expansion, naturally models the next stage of language without drilling or pressure.

### Following the Child's Attention

Language learning is most efficient when the child's attention is on something they are interested in. Commenting on what the child is looking at or pointing to — rather than directing their attention to what you think is important — gives the language a direct connection to the child's current focus. This is why parents who follow the child's lead in conversation are more effective language facilitators than those who direct and test.

## What Does Not Work

### Drilling Words

"Say ball. BALL. Can you say ball?" Research on language acquisition is consistent: drilling words does not accelerate vocabulary development and can increase anxiety around communication. Children are not memorising lists — they are building a complex system through exposure, interaction, and use. Pressure produces the opposite of the relaxed, conversational context in which language develops best.

### Finishing Sentences

When a child is struggling to find a word, the temptation to supply it immediately is understandable. But allowing processing time — a pause of several seconds — gives the child the opportunity to retrieve the word themselves, which strengthens the neural pathway. Jumping in too quickly, though well-intentioned, reduces that opportunity.

### Excessive Background Television

The mechanism by which screen time can delay language development is not simply about what children watch. It is about what they do not get while the television is on: live, responsive adult interaction. Research shows that background television — a TV on in the room, not necessarily being watched — reduces the number of words adults direct to children and reduces the number of serve-and-return conversational exchanges that happen. This is the real mechanism.

The AAP recommends no screen time except video calls for children under 18 months, and limited, high-quality, co-viewed content for children aged 18 to 24 months.

## Bilingual Language Development

Bilingual children — those exposed to two languages regularly — often develop language slightly differently from monolingual peers. They may have a smaller vocabulary in each language at early checkpoints, but their total vocabulary across both languages is typically at or above the monolingual range.

Bilingual children may mix languages within a single sentence, using a word from one language when they cannot recall it in the other. This is called code-switching and is a feature of bilingual proficiency, not a sign of confusion or delay.

The most effective approach to bilingual language support is separation by person or context: one parent consistently speaks one language, the other speaks a different one. Or one language is used at home and another in the outside world. Consistent exposure to both languages, with clear contexts, allows both to develop naturally.

Bilingual children reach all the same developmental milestones as monolingual children. Where differences occur in the timing of milestones, they are typically minor and resolve by school age.

## When to Seek a Speech and Language Therapy Referral

It is important not to adopt a "wait and see" approach if a child's language development raises genuine concern. Early intervention for language delay is significantly more effective than late intervention.

Consider speaking to your GP or health visitor if:

- Your child has no words at all by 12 months
- Your child is not using at least 10 words by 18 months
- Your child has fewer than 50 words and no two-word combinations by 24 months
- Your child is losing language skills they previously had at any age
- Your child cannot be understood by family members at age two or by strangers at age three
- You have any instinct that something is not right with your child's language development

In England, NHS Speech and Language Therapy (SALT) can be accessed via GP referral, health visitor referral, or self-referral in many areas. Early years settings including nurseries can also make referrals. Waiting times vary, but being on a waiting list early is much better than waiting to refer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My 18-month-old only has five words. Should I be worried?**

Ten words by 18 months is the milestone set by the AAP. Five words at 18 months is below this threshold and worth discussing with your health visitor or GP, particularly if comprehension also seems limited. If the child understands well and is communicating through gestures and pointing, the picture is more reassuring, but professional input is still appropriate and useful.

**Is it normal for a toddler to suddenly stop saying a word they used to say?**

Brief plateaus or regression of individual words can happen and are often not significant, particularly around periods of rapid development in other areas. However, any consistent loss of previously established language skills — particularly if combined with other developmental changes — should be discussed with a doctor promptly.

**Does speaking two languages confuse a toddler?**

No. The research on bilingualism is clear and consistent: being raised with two languages does not confuse children and does not cause language delay. Bilingual children develop both languages naturally when they have sufficient, consistent exposure to each.

**My toddler babbles constantly but has few words. Is this normal?**

Yes. Babbling is an important precursor to word development, and a toddler who babbles extensively and communicates with gestures is demonstrating a strong foundation for language, even if words are slow to come. If babbling is present along with clear comprehension and social engagement, the prognosis is generally positive. If words have not appeared by 18 months, a conversation with a health professional is worthwhile.

**Do apps and educational videos help language development?**

Apps and videos have limited effectiveness for children under two and should not substitute for live interaction. For toddlers over two, some high-quality content can introduce vocabulary, but only when an adult watches alongside and discusses what they see. Passive viewing without interaction produces minimal language benefit.

**Should I correct my toddler's grammar or pronunciation?**

Correcting directly tends to be counterproductive and can reduce a child's willingness to attempt words. Instead, model the correct form naturally: if the child says "I goed to the shop," you respond "yes, you went to the shop — what did you buy?" The correct form is provided without correction or drawing attention to the error.

## Key Takeaways

- Comprehension always exceeds production — a child who understands well is not language-delayed even if spoken words are limited.
- Key milestones: 1 to 5 words at 12 months; 10 to 20 words at 18 months; 50 words and two-word phrases at 24 months; three-word sentences at 36 months.
- The most powerful driver of language development is serve-and-return conversation — responsive, back-and-forth interaction that acknowledges and extends the child's communication.
- Parentese — slower, higher-pitched, melodically exaggerated speech — is neurologically appropriate and effective, not babyish.
- Reading aloud from birth is recommended by both the AAP and the NHS, and dialogic reading (with questions and responses) is more effective than reading alone.
- Narrating daily activities provides rich, contextualised vocabulary exposure at no cost.
- Drilling words does not work; expanding utterances and following the child's attention do.
- Background television reduces adult verbal input and conversational exchanges — this is the mechanism behind screen-time effects on language.
- Bilingual children reach the same milestones as monolingual peers, though timing may vary slightly; code-switching is normal.
- Do not wait and see with language concerns — NHS SALT referral can often be made via the GP, health visitor, or self-referral.
