Buying baby products well is not about finding the most expensive option or trusting whatever has the most Instagram reviews. It is about understanding what actually matters in each category, what the safety standards mean, and how to choose products that fit your specific situation — your house, your car, your lifestyle, and your budget.

This guide covers every major product category in the order of importance: safety-critical items first, then practical-use equipment, then the things that make daily life easier.

> **Fact-Checked & Transparent:** This article was written with AI assistance and fact-checked against current CDC, WHO, ACOG, and NHS guidelines. All health information is based on authoritative medical sources. Last verified: June 2026.

## How to Use Baby Product Buying Guides

Before evaluating any product, establish your priority framework:

**1. Safety**: Does this product meet current safety standards? Has it been recalled? Are there known hazards with this type of product?

**2. Fit for purpose**: Does this product work for your specific situation — your car, your home layout, your baby's needs?

**3. Price**: Only after the first two criteria are met does price become relevant.

This is the only rational order. A cheaper car seat that doesn't fit your car is not a saving. A highly rated monitor with a security vulnerability is not a good purchase. Start with safety and fit, then optimise on price.

## Car Seats

Car seats are the most safety-critical purchase you will make for a baby. A car seat that has been in a crash may look intact but its internal structure may be compromised. Never buy a car seat second-hand unless you can verify with absolute certainty that it has never been involved in any accident.

### The Three Stages

**Stage 1 — Infant carrier (birth to approximately 13kg or 75cm)**: Rear-facing only, typically used from birth to around 9–12 months depending on the baby's size. Portable — can be clicked in and out of the car on a compatible base, which avoids waking a sleeping baby.

**Stage 2 — Convertible seat**: Designed to be used rear-facing initially (up to 105cm in i-Size versions) and then forward-facing. More cost-effective long-term as it spans more of childhood.

**Stage 3 — High-backed booster / booster cushion**: From approximately 15kg or 100cm up to 135–150cm (roughly ages 4–12 depending on the child).

### i-Size vs the Older Group System

The i-Size (ECE R129) standard is the current regulation:

- Requires extended rear-facing to at least 105cm height
- Tested at higher speeds than the older system
- Simplifies compatibility checking (ISOFIX is mandatory for i-Size seats)
- Better side-impact protection requirements

The older Group 0+/1/2/3 seats are not banned but i-Size represents best current practice. When buying new, choose i-Size.

### Extended Rear-Facing

Extended rear-facing (ERF) is the practice of keeping children rear-facing well beyond the minimum legal requirement. Child road safety organisations consistently recommend ERF because rear-facing seats distribute crash forces across the whole back, distributing the load over a larger area versus the narrow shoulder belt used in forward-facing seats. Children's necks and spines are proportionally more vulnerable in forward-facing crashes.

In Sweden, where ERF has been standard practice for decades, child road fatality rates are significantly lower than in countries where forward-facing is the norm.

Many families keep children rear-facing until the child's head reaches the top of the seat back — which in a good rear-facing convertible seat can be 4 years of age or beyond.

## Prams, Pushchairs, and Travel Systems

A pram is one of the largest purchases you will make, and there is no single right answer — what works depends on your commute, your car boot size, your terrain, and how much you will use it.

### Newborn Requirements

Newborns must lie flat in prams. A reclining pushchair that does not go to fully flat (180°) is not appropriate from birth. The main options:

- **Traditional pram** with a separate carrycot (lies fully flat, often parent-facing)
- **Travel system**: A pushchair frame with an infant car seat that clips on (convenient, but the car seat position is not suitable for extended use — 2-hour rule applies)
- **Convertible pushchair** with a flat-recline position and (ideally) a parent-facing option

### Parent-Facing

Evidence suggests that parent-facing position in the first 6 months — where the baby faces the pushing parent — supports language development and emotional connection. Look for a pushchair that offers both parent-facing and forward-facing positions; most modern convertible options do.

### What to Test In-Store

- **Fold mechanism**: Can you fold it one-handed? Does it lock shut?
- **Boot fit**: Measure your car boot before you go and take the measurements with you; test the folded pram
- **Handlebar height**: Check it is comfortable for both adults who will push it
- **Weight**: You will lift this up and down kerbs, in and out of boots, up and down stairs

For a second lightweight travel option (for holidays, for grandparents, for when the big pram is inconvenient), a lightweight compact stroller from around 6 months is a sensible secondary purchase.

## Cots, Cribs, and Safe Sleep Equipment

### The Non-Negotiables

Safe sleep guidance from The Lullaby Trust is clear: back to sleep, firm flat surface, no loose bedding. This means:

- **No pillows**: Not appropriate until after the first year, and not necessary in the cot for several years after that
- **No duvets or quilts**: Until age 12 months minimum; baby sleeping bags in the correct tog are the safe alternative
- **No cot bumpers**: Bumpers — padded fabric liners that line the cot sides — pose suffocation and strangulation risks. They are banned in several countries and should not be used at any age
- **No wedges, sleep positioners, or head-shaping cushions in the cot**: These have no evidence of benefit and introduce suffocation risk

### The Mattress

A **new firm mattress** for each baby is the recommendation of The Lullaby Trust. Second-hand mattresses may have softened invisibly, may carry bacteria from a previous occupant, and some research has suggested a link between used mattresses and increased SIDS risk.

The mattress should fit the cot frame with no gap large enough to trap a baby's limbs or head (no more than 4cm gap on any side).

### Height-Adjustable Base

A cot with a height-adjustable base is strongly recommended. The highest position allows you to lower a sleeping baby without bending deeply (a critical consideration in the first few months when abdominal muscles are recovering from birth). As the baby learns to pull up to standing, lower the base to prevent them climbing out.

## Feeding Equipment

### Breast Pumps

For anyone planning to express milk regularly — to return to work, to build a supply, to feed when unable to breastfeed directly — a **double electric pump** is the most efficient option. Single pumps take twice as long. Hospital-grade double pumps can be hired from the NCT or Baby Café if you want to try before buying.

Key considerations:
- Suction settings (adjustability)
- Noise (if expressing at work or when others are sleeping)
- Portability
- Whether it is closed-system (prevents milk backing into the motor — essential for shared/hired pumps)

### Bottles

Try **1–2 bottles before buying a set of 10**. Bottle preference is individual to the baby. If your baby has no preference, do not spend more for claims about reducing wind — the evidence for these shaped or vented designs is weak.

### Steriliser

All bottles, teats, and breast pump parts that contact milk should be sterilised before use in the first year. An **electric steam steriliser** is the most practical for home use — fast (around 6–8 minutes), effective, and easy to use. Cold-water sterilising tablets work well but require planning. The microwave steam bag is a good travel option.

### Highchair

From approximately 6 months, when solid foods begin. Key safety and practical features:

- **Five-point harness**: Both a lap strap and shoulder straps (or a T-bar plus lap strap). A harness that allows a child to slide out or turn around is unsafe
- **Stable base**: Should not tip with normal use or if the baby pushes back
- **Easy to clean**: Padded inserts and multiple crevices dramatically increase clean-up time; simpler designs are more practical
- **Footrest**: A footrest helps the baby maintain a stable seated position and reduces fatigue during eating

Booster seats that strap onto dining chairs are a practical alternative for smaller homes; ensure the chair they attach to is stable and heavy enough.

## Baby Monitors

### Audio vs Video

An audio monitor is sufficient for most families in average-sized homes. Video monitors are genuinely useful in larger homes or for parents who find reassurance in seeing the baby.

The main categories:

- **DECT audio monitors**: Reliable, low interference, good battery life. No internet connection means no cybersecurity vulnerability
- **Wi-Fi video monitors**: Convenient (can be viewed on a smartphone anywhere), but require a secure home network. Change the default password immediately on setup; these devices have been subject to hacking incidents
- **Dedicated screen video monitors (non-Wi-Fi)**: A good middle ground — video with a dedicated parent unit, no internet exposure

### What Monitors Cannot Do

Monitors — including movement monitors that detect breathing — are **not SIDS prevention devices**. No monitor has been shown in research to reduce the rate of SIDS. The evidence-based approach to SIDS prevention is the Lullaby Trust safe sleep guidance, not technology monitoring. If a movement alarm sounds, you will still need to respond in person.

## Health and Safety Equipment

### Thermometer

Accurate temperature measurement is essential when assessing a sick baby. Options:

- **Ear (tympanic) thermometer**: Accurate and fast. Requires correct technique (pulling the ear back gently to straighten the ear canal)
- **Axillary (under-arm) thermometer**: Appropriate for very young babies; slightly less accurate but more comfortable
- **Forehead strip thermometers**: These measure skin temperature, not core temperature. They are not recommended for clinical use

Do not use a forehead strip for medical decision-making. Any fever in a baby under 3 months (above 38°C) requires same-day medical review.

### Nasal Aspirator

Babies cannot blow their noses. Congestion significantly affects feeding — a blocked nose makes it difficult for a young baby to latch or take a bottle effectively. Bulb aspirators (squeeze and release) are less effective than mouth-suction types. The Frida NoseFrida and similar devices use a tube and a parent's mouth — they are more effective and have a filter that prevents the parent from ingesting anything.

### Nail File

Newborn nails grow fast and are paper-sharp. A glass or crystal nail file (like those made by Zara's collection or Crystal Nails) is the safest option — the flexible metal kind files gently without the snipping risk of clippers. Begin from birth; many parents find it easiest to file during a feed or sleep.

### Baby First Aid Kit

Consider assembling or buying a kit that includes:

- Digital thermometer
- Saline nasal drops
- Infant paracetamol (not for under 2 months without GP advice)
- Sterile gauze and bandages
- First aid guidance booklet (St John Ambulance publishes a free baby and child first aid guide)

A baby first aid course — available from the Red Cross, St John Ambulance, and the NCT — is one of the most genuinely valuable things a new parent can do before or shortly after birth.

## The Ongoing Costs Reality

The two largest ongoing costs in the first year are **nappies and formula**.

Nappies cost between £500 and £1,500 in the first year depending on the brand. Independent testing has consistently found that supermarket own-brand nappies perform comparably to premium branded nappies for most babies. The higher price of branded nappies does not reflect superior absorbency in blind tests.

In the UK, all infant formula must meet strict compositional standards set by legislation regardless of brand. The nutritional content of own-brand formula is equivalent to premium brands. The cost difference between brands can be substantial — buying own-brand formula can save hundreds of pounds over the first year.

Subscription discounts are available from most supermarkets and Amazon for both nappies and formula — set up auto-delivery to access typically 15–20% savings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between i-Size and the old Group car seat system?**
i-Size (ECE R129) is the current standard, introduced in 2013 and being progressively rolled out. It requires longer rear-facing travel, is tested at higher speeds, mandates ISOFIX fitting, and has stronger side-impact protection requirements. The older Group 0+/1/2 seats are not illegal but i-Size is the better current standard for new purchases.

**How do I know if a baby monitor is safe from hacking?**
Wi-Fi connected monitors should be on a secure home network (WPA2 or WPA3 encryption), with the default password changed to a strong unique password immediately on setup. Check for firmware update options — a manufacturer that issues updates is more responsive to security vulnerabilities. Some families choose non-Wi-Fi dedicated screen monitors to avoid internet exposure entirely.

**Can my baby sleep in a swing or bouncer?**
No. Swings and bouncers should not be used as sleep spaces. The semi-reclined position can cause the baby's head to slump forward (positional asphyxia) and the soft surface does not meet safe sleep requirements. Transfer a sleeping baby from a swing or bouncer to a firm flat sleep surface as soon as possible.

**When should I buy a highchair?**
From around 6 months when solid foods begin. There is no benefit to buying one before then. Some families wait until they see the signs of readiness for weaning before purchasing, which avoids buying a highchair months too early.

**Is a video baby monitor worth it?**
For most families in average-sized homes, an audio monitor is sufficient and more affordable. Video monitors add genuine value in larger homes, for babies who need more oversight due to health conditions, or for parents who find strong reassurance in being able to see the baby. Non-Wi-Fi dedicated screen video monitors offer a good balance of function and security.

**Are expensive prams actually better?**
Not inherently. Pram quality varies within price points, and a £600 pram that folds easily and fits your car is more useful than a £1,200 pram that doesn't. Test in person, check the fold, verify it fits your car boot, and check the harness. Look for models with good reviews from real users rather than relying on price as a proxy for quality.

## Key Takeaways

- Safety comes first in every product category: i-Size certification for car seats, BS EN 1930 for safety gates, BS EN 716 for cots, and new firm mattress for each baby.
- Car seats must never be bought second-hand without verified crash-free history — structural damage from a crash is invisible and makes the seat unsafe.
- Extended rear-facing is significantly safer than forward-facing; child road safety organisations recommend keeping children rear-facing as long as the seat allows.
- Baby monitors — including movement monitors — are not SIDS prevention devices; the evidence-based prevention approach is the Lullaby Trust safe sleep guidelines.
- Own-brand formula and nappies are nutritionally and functionally equivalent to premium branded products; the cost savings over a year are substantial.
- The five-point harness, wipeable surface, and footrest are the three most important features of a safe and practical highchair.
