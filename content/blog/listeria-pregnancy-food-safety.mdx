# Listeria in Pregnancy: Foods to Avoid and Safe Food Handling

Pregnant women are around 20 times more likely to develop listeriosis than the general population, according to the WHO. The bacterium responsible — *Listeria monocytogenes* — is found widely in soil, water, and animal intestines, and can survive and multiply at refrigerator temperatures that would slow most other pathogens. That combination makes it an unusual and serious risk during pregnancy.

Understanding which foods pose the greatest danger, what safe alternatives exist, and how to handle food properly at home can significantly reduce that risk.

## Why Pregnancy Increases Vulnerability

The immune system naturally suppresses itself during pregnancy to prevent the body from rejecting the foetus. This protective adaptation also reduces the ability to fight off bacterial infections. Listeria can cross the placenta and infect the foetus directly, even when the mother's symptoms are mild or absent. The NHS notes that listeriosis in pregnancy can lead to miscarriage, stillbirth, premature labour, or severe illness in the newborn.

## Foods to Avoid

The following categories carry the highest listeria risk and should be avoided throughout pregnancy.

**Soft and mould-ripened cheeses** — Brie, camembert, chèvre, and blue-veined cheeses like roquefort and gorgonzola have a higher moisture content and surface pH that allows listeria to thrive. Hard cheeses such as cheddar, parmesan, and gruyère are safe, as is soft cheese made from pasteurised milk that is cooked until piping hot, such as halloumi in a baked dish.

**Pâté and meat spreads** — All pâté, including vegetable pâté, should be avoided. Refrigerated canned pâté also carries risk once opened. Alternatives include hummus, cream cheese, and thoroughly cooked meat spread that is eaten immediately while hot.

**Deli meats and cured meats** — Cold cuts, salami, pepperoni, and packaged sliced meats may be contaminated during processing or slicing. Cooking deli meats until steaming hot before eating eliminates the risk if occasional use is unavoidable.

**Smoked fish** — Cold-smoked salmon, trout, and mackerel sold ready to eat are a recognised source of listeria. Tinned fish and fish that has been cooked through are safe.

**Sushi and raw shellfish** — Raw fish sushi, oysters, mussels, and clams should be avoided. Cooked sushi rolls, fully cooked prawn dishes, and well-cooked fish of any kind are safe alternatives.

**Pre-washed and pre-packaged salads** — Bags of prepared salad leaves, including those labelled washed and ready to eat, have been implicated in listeria outbreaks. Washing leaves again at home reduces but does not eliminate the risk. Preparing salad from whole unwashed vegetables washed thoroughly at home is a safer approach.

**Soft-serve ice cream** — Machines used for soft-serve ice cream can harbour listeria if not cleaned properly. The NHS advises pregnant women to avoid soft-serve. Hard ice cream from sealed tubs is safe.

## Food Handling and Kitchen Hygiene

The way food is handled at home matters as much as the food itself.

**Refrigerator temperature** — Keep the fridge at 5°C or below. Listeria can grow slowly at typical fridge temperatures but stops multiplying below 2°C and is killed by thorough cooking. Use a fridge thermometer to confirm the temperature rather than relying on the dial setting.

**Cooking temperatures** — Cook all meat until there is no pink remaining and juices run clear. Poultry should reach an internal temperature of 74°C (165°F). Reheat leftovers and ready meals until they are steaming throughout, not just warm on the surface.

**Hand washing** — Wash hands thoroughly with soap and warm water before preparing food, after handling raw meat, fish, or soil, and after touching pets. The WHO identifies hand hygiene as one of the most effective preventive measures against foodborne illness.

**Cross-contamination** — Keep raw meat and fish on the bottom shelf of the fridge, away from ready-to-eat food. Use separate chopping boards for raw meat and other foods, and wash boards, knives, and surfaces with hot soapy water between uses.

**Leftovers** — Eat leftovers within 24 hours and always reheat fully. Avoid eating food that has been sitting at room temperature for more than two hours.

## Symptoms of Listeriosis and When to Contact a Doctor

Symptoms typically appear between 1 and 30 days after exposure and can include fever, muscle aches, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea. Pregnant women sometimes experience only mild flu-like symptoms, which makes listeriosis easy to overlook.

Contact your GP or midwife promptly if you develop a fever, feel generally unwell after eating high-risk foods, or notice reduced foetal movement. Early treatment with antibiotics — usually amoxicillin or ampicillin — is effective and significantly reduces the risk of complications. The NHS recommends not waiting to see whether symptoms resolve on their own during pregnancy.

## What Happens Without Treatment

Untreated listeriosis during pregnancy can spread through the bloodstream to the placenta and foetus. This can cause spontaneous miscarriage in the first and second trimesters, preterm labour, or a newborn born with neonatal listeriosis, which is a serious condition requiring intensive hospital care. The foetal mortality rate from untreated listeria infection is considerably higher than in the general population, which is why rapid medical assessment is important whenever listeriosis is suspected.
