# Listeria and Pregnancy: Foods to Avoid and Why They Matter

Pregnant women are around 20 times more likely to develop serious listeriosis than the general healthy population, according to the WHO. This elevated risk is not arbitrary — it has a specific biological cause, and understanding it makes the NHS food guidance less arbitrary-seeming and easier to follow consistently.

This article explains what Listeria monocytogenes is, why pregnancy specifically increases susceptibility, exactly which foods carry meaningful risk and why, what safe alternatives exist, and how household food hygiene reduces risk across all categories.

## What Listeria Is

*Listeria monocytogenes* is a bacterium found widely in soil, water, animal intestines, and some plant material. It contaminates food during growing, processing, or handling. Its distinctive property is the ability to survive and multiply at refrigerator temperatures — most pathogens slow significantly below 4°C, but Listeria continues to grow, albeit more slowly, at typical fridge temperatures. This means that refrigerating a contaminated food does not make it safe over time; it may become more contaminated as days pass.

Listeriosis in healthy adults typically causes fever, muscle aches, diarrhoea, and nausea — symptoms that may be attributed to general illness rather than a specific foodborne infection. In healthy people, the infection is usually self-limiting. In pregnant women, older adults, and people with weakened immune systems, listeriosis carries a much higher risk of severe illness and life-threatening complications.

## Why Pregnancy Increases Vulnerability

The immune system undergoes a specific and necessary adaptation during pregnancy: immune activity is reduced at the maternal-foetal interface to prevent the mother's immune system from rejecting the foetus — which contains paternal proteins that would otherwise be identified as foreign. This protective mechanism has the side effect of reducing the immune system's ability to contain bacterial infections throughout the body.

The NHS notes that this immune modulation makes pregnant women significantly more susceptible to listeriosis than non-pregnant adults. More importantly, Listeria can cross the placenta and infect the foetus or the amniotic fluid directly, even when the mother's own symptoms are mild or unrecognised. The foetus has an immature immune system that cannot contain the infection effectively.

The NHS identifies listeriosis during pregnancy as a potential cause of miscarriage, stillbirth, premature labour, or severe illness in the newborn requiring intensive hospital care. This is why the food guidance is conservative — the consequences of infection are disproportionate to how unwell the mother may actually feel.

## Foods to Avoid

The following categories carry the highest risk and are advised against by the NHS throughout pregnancy.

### Soft Mould-Ripened Cheeses

Brie, camembert, chèvre (soft goat's cheese), and similar mould-ripened soft cheeses have a higher moisture content and surface pH that creates an environment in which Listeria survives and multiplies. The mould rind itself is a particular concern. These cheeses are typically eaten cold and uncooked, meaning no heat step kills any contamination present.

**What is safe:** Hard cheeses — cheddar, edam, emmental, gruyère, parmesan — are safe throughout pregnancy. The lower moisture content and acid environment of hard cheese is inhospitable to Listeria. Processed soft cheeses made from pasteurised milk — cream cheese, cottage cheese, ricotta, and fresh mozzarella — are also safe. Any soft cheese heated until piping hot throughout (for example, baked brie) is safe because heat kills Listeria.

### Blue-Veined Cheeses

Roquefort, gorgonzola, Stilton, and Danish blue are higher-risk despite not being mould-ripened in the same way as brie or camembert. The internal structure of blue cheese, with its channels of mould, creates additional moisture and pH conditions associated with Listeria growth. The NHS advises avoiding blue-veined cheeses unless cooked until piping hot.

### Pate and Meat Spreads

All pâté — including vegetable and fish pâté — should be avoided. The processing and storage conditions of pâté make it one of the higher-risk Listeria vehicles. Canned pâté is also included once the can is opened and the contents are refrigerated. Alternatives include hummus, cream cheese spreads, and thoroughly cooked meat that is eaten immediately while hot.

### Cold Cured and Deli Meats

Cold cuts, salami, chorizo, prosciutto, pepperoni, and packaged sliced deli meats may be contaminated during processing or at the slicing machine. Contamination introduced at the deli counter can spread across multiple servings. The NHS recommends avoiding these products unless they are cooked until steaming hot immediately before eating. Cooking through eliminates the risk if occasional use is unavoidable.

### Smoked Fish

Cold-smoked salmon, trout, and mackerel sold ready-to-eat are a recognised source of Listeria. The smoking process does not reach temperatures that reliably kill Listeria, and the product is consumed without further cooking. Tinned fish — tuna, salmon, sardines — is safe because the canning process eliminates pathogens. Freshly cooked fish of any kind is safe.

### Pre-Washed and Pre-Packaged Salad

Bags of prepared salad leaves — including those labelled washed and ready to eat — have been implicated in listeria outbreaks in both the UK and the US. Washing them again at home reduces but does not eliminate the risk because Listeria can adhere to leaf surfaces in ways that are difficult to remove by rinsing. Preparing salad from whole unwashed vegetables washed thoroughly at home is a safer approach.

Coleslaw and other prepared salads sold from delicatessen counters or refrigerated sections carry similar concerns and are on the NHS avoid list.

### Soft-Serve Ice Cream

The machines used to dispense soft-serve ice cream can harbour Listeria if not cleaned and maintained properly. The NHS advises pregnant women to avoid soft-serve ice cream. Commercially produced hard ice cream from sealed factory-produced tubs is safe.

### Raw and Undercooked Meat, Poultry, and Eggs

Raw and undercooked animal products carry risks from multiple pathogens — Listeria, Salmonella, E. coli, Campylobacter — and should be thoroughly cooked throughout. Poultry should reach an internal temperature of 74°C (165°F). All meat should show no pink in the centre and clear juices. Ready-to-eat meat products should be stored appropriately and consumed within recommended timescales.

For eggs specifically, the NHS advises that UK-produced eggs stamped with the British Lion Code of Practice mark are safe to eat runny, as the hens are vaccinated against Salmonella. Non-Lion-stamped eggs, and eggs from outside this scheme, should be fully cooked.

### Sushi and Raw Shellfish

Raw fish sushi and raw shellfish — oysters, mussels, clams — should be avoided. Cooked sushi rolls (vegetable, cooked prawn, or cooked fish) and fully cooked seafood dishes are safe.

## Food Hygiene That Reduces Risk Across All Categories

Even with care about specific high-risk foods, general kitchen hygiene practices significantly reduce overall risk from listeria and other pathogens.

**Refrigerator temperature** — keep the fridge at 5°C or below, and ideally below 3°C. Use a fridge thermometer to verify the actual temperature; dial settings on domestic fridges are often approximate. Listeria growth slows significantly below 2°C.

**Hand washing** — wash hands with soap and warm water before preparing food, after handling raw meat or fish, after handling soil or plants, and after contact with animals. The WHO identifies hand hygiene as one of the most effective single measures against foodborne illness of all kinds.

**Cross-contamination** — store raw meat and fish on the bottom shelf of the fridge, sealed, below ready-to-eat foods. Use separate chopping boards and utensils for raw meat and other foods. Wash boards, knives, and surfaces with hot soapy water between uses.

**Cooking temperatures** — cook all meat and poultry thoroughly until there is no pink remaining. Reheat all leftovers and ready meals until steaming throughout, not just warm on the surface. A food thermometer is the most reliable way to verify meat is cooked through.

**Leftovers** — eat leftovers within 24 hours and always reheat fully before consuming. Avoid food that has been left at room temperature for more than two hours, as bacteria multiply rapidly between 8°C and 60°C.

**Use-by dates** — use-by dates on chilled ready-to-eat foods are food safety dates, not quality dates. Do not eat chilled ready-to-eat foods past their use-by date even if the food looks and smells fine.

## Symptoms of Listeriosis and When to Seek Medical Help

Symptoms typically appear between one and thirty days after exposure. They may include fever, muscle aches, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea. Because the symptoms overlap with general viral illness, listeriosis during pregnancy is frequently not recognised unless there is clinical suspicion.

The NHS recommends contacting your GP or midwife promptly if you develop a fever, feel generally unwell after eating a high-risk food, notice reduced foetal movement, or experience any bleeding. Do not wait to see whether symptoms resolve on their own — in pregnancy, rapid assessment and treatment if indicated is significantly safer than watchful waiting.

Early antibiotic treatment — typically amoxicillin or ampicillin — is effective against listeriosis and substantially reduces the risk of foetal complications. The treatment course is determined by your GP or hospital doctor based on the severity of infection and gestational age.

## Understanding the Risk in Proportion

NHS guidance on food avoidance in pregnancy is occasionally perceived as excessive. It is worth understanding that the absolute risk from any individual food item is low — listeriosis is not common even among pregnant women. The guidance is precautionary because the consequences when infection does occur are disproportionately serious compared to the minor inconvenience of avoiding certain food categories for nine months.

Some foods on the avoid list — particularly soft cheeses and smoked fish — are ones that many people eat regularly without any problem. The relevant factor is not that these foods are dangerous for everyone, but that the specific combination of immune modulation in pregnancy and Listeria's ability to cross the placenta creates a risk-benefit calculation that supports avoidance.

The list is also not as restrictive as it sometimes appears. All hard cheeses, all cooked food, all pasteurised processed soft cheeses, all tinned fish, freshly cooked meat and fish of all kinds, and the vast majority of everyday foods are completely safe. The categories to avoid are specific, and for most of them there are direct safe alternatives.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I eat brie or camembert if it is cooked?**

Yes. The NHS guidance is that soft mould-ripened cheeses are safe when cooked until piping hot throughout — for example, baked brie or camembert used in a cooked dish. Cold brie, camembert, or any uncooked mould-ripened cheese should be avoided.

**Is sushi safe during pregnancy in the UK?**

Cooked sushi — vegetable rolls, cooked prawn, cooked crab, or any roll using cooked ingredients — is safe. Raw fish sushi carries risk from both Listeria and parasites and is on the NHS avoid list. Some UK NHS guidance also notes that reputable UK restaurants following food safety standards reduce but do not eliminate raw fish risk. The precautionary recommendation is to choose cooked options.

**Are Lion Code eggs safe to eat runny during pregnancy?**

Yes. The NHS states that UK-produced eggs carrying the British Lion Code of Practice stamp (a printed lion mark on the shell) are safe to eat runny or partially cooked. The Lion Code requires hens to be vaccinated against Salmonella. Non-Lion-stamped eggs and eggs from outside this scheme should be fully cooked.

**Can I eat smoked salmon on a cooked dish?**

The NHS recommendation is to avoid cold-smoked ready-to-eat salmon during pregnancy. If cold-smoked salmon is heated through as part of a cooked dish — baked pasta, for example — until it is steaming hot throughout, the heat kills Listeria and the resulting dish is safe. Freshly cooked hot-smoked salmon (cooked to steaming throughout rather than cold-smoked) is also safe.

**What if I have already eaten a food I should have avoided?**

If you ate something from the high-risk list once and have no symptoms, the likelihood of having contracted listeriosis is low. Monitor for symptoms over the following two to thirty days — fever, muscle aches, nausea, or any reduction in foetal movement. Contact your GP or midwife if any of these occur. For reassurance or if you are concerned, your midwife or GP can advise whether any monitoring or testing is appropriate based on the specific food and how recently it was eaten.

**Does freezing kill Listeria?**

No. Listeria survives freezing and resumes growth when the food is thawed. Freezing does not make a Listeria-contaminated food safe. Only thorough cooking — reaching a core temperature of at least 75°C — reliably kills Listeria.

## Key Takeaways

- Pregnant women are approximately 20 times more likely to develop serious listeriosis than healthy non-pregnant adults, because immune modulation during pregnancy reduces the ability to contain bacterial infections, and Listeria can cross the placenta directly.
- The NHS foods to avoid are: soft mould-ripened cheeses (brie, camembert, chèvre), blue-veined cheeses, all pâté, cold cured meats, cold-smoked fish, pre-packaged salads, soft-serve ice cream, and raw or undercooked meat, poultry, and eggs.
- Hard cheeses (cheddar, parmesan, gruyère), pasteurised processed soft cheeses (cream cheese, cottage cheese, mozzarella), tinned fish, and freshly cooked food of all kinds are safe — the restriction list is more specific and less broad than it is sometimes perceived.
- Cooking any food from the avoid list until piping hot throughout kills Listeria — baked brie, cooked salami on a pizza, or reheated deli meat are safe when properly heated to 75°C or above.
- Refrigerator temperature, hand washing, preventing cross-contamination, and eating cooked food promptly are the hygiene practices that reduce risk across all food categories.
- Contact your GP or midwife promptly if you develop a fever, feel unwell after eating a high-risk food, or notice reduced foetal movement — early antibiotic treatment is effective and significantly reduces the risk of complications.
