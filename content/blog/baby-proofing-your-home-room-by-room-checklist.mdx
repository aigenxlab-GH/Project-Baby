The moment you bring your baby home, your entire perspective on your living space changes. That charming coffee table corner suddenly looks menacing, the television cabinet becomes a climbing hazard waiting to happen, and every electrical outlet seems to call out for curious little fingers. Baby proofing your home isn't about turning your space into a padded fortress—it's about creating an environment where your little one can safely explore, learn, and grow while giving you peace of mind.

Whether you're currently preparing your [registry checklist](/tools/registry-checklist) or you've noticed your six-month-old starting to scoot across the floor, this comprehensive room-by-room guide will help you identify potential hazards and transform your home into a safe haven for your adventurous explorer.

## Understanding Baby Development and Safety Needs

Before diving into specific rooms, it's helpful to understand why timing matters when it comes to baby proofing. Your baby's developmental stages directly influence which safety measures become priorities.

### The Crawling Stage (6-10 Months)

This is when most parents realise just how many hazards exist at ground level. Babies at this stage put everything in their mouths, pull themselves up on furniture, and have an uncanny ability to find the one thing you forgot to secure. Floor-level hazards become your primary concern during this phase.

### The Walking and Climbing Stage (10-18 Months)

Toddlers are essentially small, determined humans with no fear and limited understanding of consequences. They'll climb bookshelves, reach for items on counters, and test every boundary you set. This stage requires you to think vertically as well as horizontally about safety.

### The Curious Explorer Stage (18 Months-3 Years)

By this point, your little one can open doors, manipulate locks, and figure out how things work. Simple safety measures may no longer be sufficient, and you'll need more sophisticated solutions to stay one step ahead.

## The Living Room: Your Family's Main Gathering Space

The living room presents unique challenges because it's designed for adult comfort and relaxation rather than baby safety. Here's your comprehensive checklist for this space.

### Furniture Safety

Secure all tall or heavy furniture to the wall using anti-tip straps or brackets. This includes bookcases, television units, chest of drawers, and standing shelves. Falls involving furniture tip-overs cause thousands of injuries each year, and many are entirely preventable.

Apply corner guards to coffee tables, side tables, and any furniture with sharp edges. Soft silicone guards work well and are less noticeable than foam alternatives. Consider temporarily removing glass coffee tables or replacing them with upholstered ottomans during the toddler years.

Move floor lamps behind furniture or switch to table lamps placed well out of reach. Standing lamps can be pulled down easily by a curious baby learning to stand.

### Electronics and Cords

Televisions should be mounted on the wall or secured to furniture with anti-tip straps designed for electronics. The base of a heavy television is incredibly tempting for little hands to grab.

Bundle and hide all electrical cords using cord covers or cord concealers. Babies and toddlers will chew on cords, pull on them, or become tangled in them. Consider wireless options for devices where possible.

Cover all accessible electrical outlets with plug covers or switch to tamper-resistant outlets throughout your home. Traditional plug covers work well, but sliding plate covers provide a more permanent solution that doesn't create a choking hazard.

### Small Objects and Decorations

Remove decorative items from low surfaces—this includes candles, photo frames, small sculptures, and anything that could be pulled down, broken, or swallowed. A good rule of thumb: if it fits through a toilet paper tube, it's a choking hazard.

Store remote controls out of reach, as batteries can be dangerous if accessed. Consider a dedicated basket on a high shelf for remotes and small electronics.

## The Kitchen: Where Most Home Accidents Occur

The kitchen is statistically the most dangerous room in your home for young children. It contains heat sources, sharp objects, toxic substances, and countless temptations for curious hands.

### Cabinet and Drawer Safety

Install childproof locks on all lower cabinets and drawers, especially those containing cleaning products, sharp utensils, plastic bags, and heavy items like pots and pans. Consider designating one lower cabinet as a "yes" space filled with safe items your baby can freely explore—plastic containers, wooden spoons, and measuring cups make excellent toys.

Move all cleaning products, detergents, and chemicals to high cabinets or locked storage. This includes dishwasher tablets, which can look like sweets to young children and cause serious injuries.

### Appliance Safety

Use stove knob covers to prevent little hands from turning on burners. Always cook on back burners when possible and turn pot handles inward so they can't be grabbed.

Secure the oven door with an oven lock, as the door and viewing glass become extremely hot during use. Never leave the oven door open, even momentarily.

Install a refrigerator lock if your toddler figures out how to open it—both for safety and to prevent the endless reorganisation of your carefully planned meal prep!

Apply a dishwasher lock to prevent access to sharp cutlery, detergent residue, and breakable dishes. Run the dishwasher when your baby is sleeping or closely supervised.

### Additional Kitchen Precautions

Keep small appliances unplugged when not in use, with cords pushed far back on counters. Toddlers can pull kettles, toasters, and blenders down on themselves by tugging cords.

Never leave hot drinks near counter edges, and consider making it a household rule to only drink hot beverages when seated at the table. Hot drink spills are a leading cause of burns in young children.

Install a baby gate at the kitchen entrance if your layout allows, creating a safe way to keep your little one out while you're cooking with hot oil or boiling water.

## The Bathroom: Hidden Hazards in a Small Space

Bathrooms contain water hazards, medications, sharp objects, and slippery surfaces—all in a relatively confined area. This room requires thorough attention.

### Water Safety

Never leave your baby unattended near water, even for a moment. Drowning can occur in as little as an inch of water and happens silently. If you need to answer the door or phone during bath time, wrap your baby in a towel and take them with you.

Install a toilet lock to prevent drowning hazards and the inevitable fascination with flushing random objects. Keep the bathroom door closed and consider adding a door lever cover to prevent independent entry.

Set your hot water heater to 48°C (120°F) or lower to prevent scalding. Consider adding anti-scald devices to bath taps as an extra precaution.

### Medication and Product Storage

Move all medications, vitamins, and supplements to a locked medicine cabinet or a high shelf in another room. Child-resistant caps are not childproof—determined toddlers can often open them.

Store all personal care products, including shampoos, mouthwash, and cosmetics, out of reach. Many contain ingredients that are toxic if swallowed.

### Physical Safety

Place non-slip mats both inside the bathtub and on the bathroom floor beside it. A spout cover on the bath taps protects little heads from bumps during splash-happy bath times.

Keep razors, scissors, nail clippers, and other sharp objects in locked drawers or high cabinets. Secure glass items like mirrors and perfume bottles away from edges.

## The Nursery: Creating a Safe Sleep Space

While you might expect the nursery to be naturally safe, this room requires careful attention to create an optimal environment for your baby.

### Sleep Safety

Follow safe sleep guidelines by keeping the cot free of blankets, pillows, stuffed animals, and bumper pads until your child is at least 12 months old. Use a firm, flat mattress that fits snugly in the cot with no gaps.

Position the cot away from windows, blind cords, curtain ties, and any wall decorations that could fall. Once your baby can pull up to standing, lower the mattress to its lowest setting to prevent climbing out.

For detailed guidance on developmental milestones that affect sleep safety, check out our [week by week guide](/pregnancy/week-by-week) to understand what changes to expect.

### Furniture and Storage

Anchor the changing table, wardrobe, bookcase, and chest of drawers to the wall. Never leave your baby unattended on a changing table, even for a second.

Choose a nappy bin with a secure lid that locks, keeping contents and any deodorising products safely contained.

Install cord cleats or wind-ups for any window blind cords, or switch to cordless blinds entirely. Blind cords pose a serious strangulation risk.

### Monitoring and Environment

If using a baby monitor, keep the unit and its cord at least 90cm (3 feet) away from the cot. Cords should be completely out of reach.

Use outlet covers on all accessible plugs, and secure any trailing wires from lamps or monitors using cord organisers.

## Stairs, Hallways, and Transitional Spaces

The spaces between rooms often get overlooked in baby proofing efforts, yet they present significant hazards.

### Stairway Safety

Install safety gates at both the top and bottom of all staircases. For the top of stairs, use hardware-mounted gates that screw into the wall—pressure-mounted gates can be pushed out and are not safe for stair tops.

Gates should be at least three-quarters of your child's height to prevent climbing over. Once your child can climb the gate, it's time to teach safe stair navigation while maintaining supervision.

Consider adding stair edge protectors if you have wooden or slippery stairs, providing additional grip for little feet learning to climb.

### Doorways and Exits

Install door lever covers on doors leading to hazardous areas—garages, basements, and outside doors. Door stop guards prevent little fingers from getting caught in closing doors.

Secure external doors with chain locks or high deadbolts that children cannot reach. Sliding glass doors should have locks at both child level (to prevent opening) and adult level (for security).

Mark large glass doors and low windows with stickers to prevent collisions by children (and adults!) who don't see the glass.

## The Garage and Outdoor Areas

If your baby will ever have access to outdoor spaces or transitional areas like garages and utility rooms, these need attention too.

### Garage Safety

Keep the garage door closed and consider disconnecting the automatic opener's emergency release when not in use. Store all chemicals, paint, pesticides, and sharp tools in locked cabinets or high shelving.

Never let your baby near a running car—carbon monoxide is invisible and deadly. Keep car doors locked so curious toddlers can't climb in.

### Garden and Outdoor Areas

Check your garden for poisonous plants and either remove them or fence them off securely. Supervise all outdoor play near water features, and consider draining paddling pools when not in use.

Install locks on garden sheds storing tools or chemicals. Ensure fencing is secure with no gaps large enough for a small body to squeeze through.

Check play equipment regularly for stability, rust, splinters, and sharp edges. Ensure soft landing surfaces beneath climbing frames and swings.

## Creating Your Baby Proofing Action Plan

With so many items to address, it helps to prioritise and create a manageable action plan.

Start with the highest-risk areas—typically the kitchen, bathroom, and staircases. Address these before your baby becomes mobile, ideally during your third trimester. You can use our [due date calculator](/tools/due-date-calculator) to help plan your baby proofing timeline.

Next, tackle the living areas and nursery, then move on to secondary spaces like guest rooms and outdoor areas. Remember that baby proofing is an ongoing process—what works for a crawling baby may need adjusting for a climbing toddler.

Schedule regular safety sweeps of your home, especially after hosting guests who may have left small objects or medications within reach. Keep a list of items you need to address, and chip away at it steadily rather than trying to accomplish everything in a single overwhelming weekend.

The goal of baby proofing isn't to create an environment where nothing can ever go wrong—that's simply not possible. Instead, you're minimising risks and creating a safer space where your little one can explore with appropriate supervision. As your child grows, the safety measures will evolve, and eventually, many of these precautions can be removed as your child learns to navigate their world safely.

Trust your instincts as you baby proof your home. You know your space and your child better than anyone. Get down on your hands and knees occasionally to see your home from your baby's perspective—you'll be amazed at what hazards become visible from just 60 centimetres off the ground. With thoughtful preparation and ongoing vigilance, you'll create a home where your curious explorer can safely learn, play, and thrive.
