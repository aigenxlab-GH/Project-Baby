If you've looked down at your feet recently and wondered where your ankles disappeared to, you're definitely not alone. Swollen feet and ankles during pregnancy—medically known as edema—affect up to 80% of expecting mothers at some point during their journey. While watching your favourite shoes become increasingly uncomfortable can be frustrating, understanding why this happens and what you can do about it makes all the difference in finding relief and peace of mind.

The good news? In most cases, pregnancy swelling is completely normal and simply a sign that your amazing body is working overtime to support your growing baby. Let's explore everything you need to know about those puffy feet, from the science behind the swelling to practical remedies that actually work.

## Why Do Feet and Ankles Swell During Pregnancy?

Your body undergoes remarkable changes during pregnancy, and swelling is one of the most visible signs of all the hard work happening inside. Understanding the causes can help you feel more in control and know what to expect as your pregnancy progresses.

### Increased Blood Volume

During pregnancy, your body produces approximately 50% more blood and bodily fluids than usual. This extra fluid is essential—it helps soften your body to accommodate your growing baby, prepares your pelvic joints and tissues for delivery, and ensures adequate blood flow to your uterus. However, all this extra fluid has to go somewhere, and gravity naturally pulls it toward your lower extremities.

### Hormonal Changes

Pregnancy hormones, particularly progesterone and relaxin, cause your blood vessels to relax and dilate. While this is necessary for healthy circulation to your placenta, it also means fluid can more easily seep from your blood vessels into surrounding tissues. Your kidneys also retain more sodium during pregnancy, which contributes to fluid retention throughout your body.

### Growing Uterus Pressure

As your baby grows, your expanding uterus puts increasing pressure on the large veins in your pelvis, particularly the inferior vena cava on your right side. This pressure can slow the return of blood from your legs to your heart, causing fluid to pool in your feet, ankles, and lower legs. This is why swelling often becomes more pronounced during the third trimester when your baby is at their largest.

### Reduced Physical Activity

Many women naturally become less active as their pregnancy progresses, which is completely understandable when you're carrying extra weight and dealing with fatigue. However, decreased movement means your calf muscles—which act as pumps to help push blood back up toward your heart—aren't working as efficiently, leading to increased fluid accumulation in your lower legs.

## When Does Pregnancy Swelling Typically Occur?

While every pregnancy is unique, there's a general pattern to when most women experience swelling and what factors can make it worse.

### Timeline of Swelling

Most women first notice mild swelling around the end of the second trimester, typically between weeks 22 and 27. The swelling tends to gradually increase throughout the third trimester, often peaking around weeks 35 to 40. Some women experience swelling earlier, particularly if they're carrying multiples, have had previous pregnancies, or started pregnancy with a higher body weight.

### Factors That Increase Swelling

Several factors can make pregnancy swelling more pronounced:

- **Hot weather**: Warm temperatures cause blood vessels to dilate further, increasing fluid leakage into tissues
- **Standing or sitting for long periods**: Staying in one position allows fluid to accumulate in your lower extremities
- **High sodium intake**: Excess salt encourages your body to retain more water
- **Caffeine consumption**: While moderate caffeine is safe during pregnancy, it can contribute to dehydration and paradoxically worsen swelling
- **Late in the day**: Swelling typically worsens as the day goes on due to gravity and accumulated activity

### What's Considered Normal?

Normal pregnancy swelling is usually:
- Gradual in onset
- Symmetrical (affecting both feet and ankles similarly)
- Worse in the evening and better after rest
- Responsive to elevation and other home remedies
- Not accompanied by other concerning symptoms

## Effective Remedies for Swollen Feet and Ankles

While you may not be able to prevent swelling entirely, these tried-and-tested remedies can provide significant relief and help you stay comfortable throughout your pregnancy.

### Elevation Is Your Best Friend

Whenever possible, elevate your feet above the level of your heart. This simple act uses gravity to help fluid drain back toward your central circulation. Try lying on your left side with pillows under your feet, or recline on your sofa with your feet propped on the armrest. Even elevating your feet on a footstool while sitting can make a noticeable difference.

### Stay Hydrated

It might seem counterintuitive, but drinking plenty of water actually helps reduce swelling. When you're well-hydrated, your body is less likely to hold onto excess fluid. Aim for at least 8-10 glasses of water daily, and more if you're active or it's warm outside. You can also boost your fluid intake with water-rich foods like cucumber, watermelon, and citrus fruits.

### Keep Moving

Gentle, regular movement is one of the most effective ways to combat swelling. When you walk, your calf muscles contract and help pump fluid back up toward your heart. Try to take short walks throughout the day, do ankle circles while seated, or try pregnancy-safe swimming, which provides the added benefit of hydrostatic pressure to help reduce swelling.

### Choose Supportive Footwear

Now is not the time for high heels or tight, restrictive shoes. Opt for comfortable, supportive footwear with good arch support and room for your feet to expand throughout the day. Many women find that going up half a shoe size during pregnancy provides the comfort they need. Slip-on styles are practical as bending down becomes more challenging.

### Try Compression Stockings

Maternity compression stockings can be incredibly effective at preventing and reducing swelling. They work by applying graduated pressure to your legs, helping blood and fluid move upward against gravity. Put them on first thing in the morning before swelling has a chance to develop. Your midwife or doctor can recommend the appropriate compression level for your needs.

### Watch Your Salt Intake

While you don't need to eliminate salt completely (your body needs some sodium), reducing highly processed foods and avoiding adding extra salt to meals can help minimise fluid retention. Focus on flavouring foods with herbs, spices, lemon juice, and garlic instead. Be particularly mindful of hidden sodium in foods like canned soups, deli meats, and restaurant meals.

## Lifestyle Adjustments for Managing Swelling

Beyond quick fixes, making some simple lifestyle adjustments can help you manage swelling more effectively throughout your pregnancy.

### Optimise Your Sleep Position

Sleeping on your left side is recommended during pregnancy for multiple reasons, including reducing swelling. This position takes pressure off the inferior vena cava and allows for optimal blood flow. Place a pillow between your knees for comfort and consider elevating your feet slightly with a pillow under your ankles.

### Take Regular Movement Breaks

If your work or lifestyle involves prolonged sitting or standing, make a conscious effort to change positions frequently. Set a timer to remind yourself to move every 30-45 minutes. Simple activities like walking to get water, doing calf raises at your desk, or taking a brief stroll around the office can make a significant difference.

### Cool Down Strategically

Cool water can help constrict blood vessels and reduce swelling. Try soaking your feet in a basin of cool (not ice cold) water for 15-20 minutes, taking a cool shower, or applying cool compresses to your ankles. Swimming in a cool pool combines the benefits of temperature, movement, and water pressure for triple the relief.

### Consider Prenatal Massage

Professional prenatal massage can help stimulate circulation and encourage lymphatic drainage, reducing fluid buildup in your extremities. Ensure you see a therapist specifically trained in prenatal massage who understands the unique needs and safety considerations of pregnancy. If professional massage isn't accessible, gentle self-massage or having your partner massage your feet and legs in upward strokes can also provide relief.

## Warning Signs: When to Contact Your Healthcare Provider

While swelling is usually a normal part of pregnancy, certain types of swelling can indicate potentially serious conditions that require immediate medical attention. Trust your instincts—if something feels wrong, it's always better to check with your healthcare provider.

### Signs of Preeclampsia

Preeclampsia is a pregnancy complication characterised by high blood pressure and protein in the urine, typically developing after 20 weeks of pregnancy. Warning signs include:

- **Sudden, severe swelling** of your face, hands, or feet (especially if it develops rapidly over a day or two)
- **Swelling that doesn't improve** with rest and elevation
- **Severe headache** that doesn't respond to normal pain relief
- **Visual disturbances** such as seeing spots, flashing lights, or blurred vision
- **Pain in your upper abdomen**, particularly on the right side under your ribs
- **Sudden weight gain** of more than 2 pounds in a week

If you experience any of these symptoms, contact your midwife or doctor immediately or go to your nearest maternity unit. Preeclampsia can be serious but is manageable when detected and treated early.

### Signs of Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Pregnancy increases your risk of blood clots. Signs that swelling might indicate a DVT include:

- **Swelling in only one leg** that's significantly worse than the other
- **Pain or tenderness** in your calf or thigh, especially when walking or flexing your foot
- **Warmth or redness** in the affected area
- **Visible veins** that appear more prominent than usual

DVT is a medical emergency—if you suspect a blood clot, seek immediate medical care.

### Other Reasons to Call Your Provider

Contact your healthcare team if you notice:
- Swelling that develops suddenly and severely
- Swelling accompanied by shortness of breath or chest pain
- No improvement in swelling despite trying home remedies
- Swelling that's painful or limiting your mobility significantly

## What to Expect Postpartum

Understanding what happens after delivery can help you prepare for the final phase of this swelling journey.

### The Initial Days

Many women are surprised to find that swelling actually increases in the first few days after delivery. This is completely normal—your body received IV fluids during labour, and hormonal shifts cause temporary fluid retention. You may notice particularly puffy feet, ankles, and even hands and face during this time.

### The Resolution Timeline

For most women, pregnancy-related swelling resolves within one to two weeks postpartum. Your body eliminates excess fluid through increased urination (you may find yourself needing the bathroom very frequently) and sweating, particularly night sweats. Continuing to stay hydrated, elevate your feet when possible, and move gently can help speed this process.

### Permanent Changes

Some women notice their feet remain slightly larger even after all swelling has resolved. This is due to the hormone relaxin, which loosens ligaments throughout your body during pregnancy, including in your feet. The arch of your foot may flatten slightly, resulting in a permanent increase of half a shoe size for some women.

## Embracing Your Changing Body

Swollen feet and ankles during pregnancy can certainly be uncomfortable and sometimes frustrating—especially when your favourite shoes no longer fit or you can barely see your toes over your bump. But remember that this temporary inconvenience is part of the incredible process your body is going through to grow and nurture your baby.

Be gentle with yourself during this time. Rest when you need to, accept help when it's offered, and remember that this phase will pass. Soon, you'll be holding your little one, and those puffy ankles will be a distant memory—albeit one you might look back on with a knowing smile.

In the meantime, keep your feet up, stay hydrated, keep moving when you can, and don't hesitate to reach out to your healthcare provider if anything concerns you. Your comfort and health matter, and there's always support available when you need it. You're doing an amazing job growing that precious baby, swollen feet and all.
