# Newborn Essentials Checklist: What You Actually Need vs What to Skip

The baby industry is enormous, and it is specifically designed to make you feel that more is safer. The reality is that a newborn needs very little — warmth, milk, clean skin, safe sleep, and a way to get around. Everything else exists on a spectrum from genuinely useful to completely pointless for the first few months of life.

This checklist is built on what actually matters, informed by NHS and WHO guidance on infant safety, and filtered through the lens of what new parents consistently report using versus what collects dust in the corner. It is organised by category so you can work through each area without forgetting anything critical.

## Sleep: What Your Newborn Actually Needs

Safe sleep equipment is one of the areas where cutting corners has real consequences, and where buying too much can introduce unnecessary hazards.

### The Sleep Space

Your baby needs a firm, flat sleep surface with a waterproof-covered mattress. This can be a crib, a Moses basket, a carrycot, or a travel cot. Moses baskets are popular for newborns because they are portable and keep babies feeling snug, but they are only suitable until around three to four months, when babies typically outgrow them or begin to roll.

The NHS, following Lullaby Trust guidelines, recommends that the sleep space be kept bare. This means:

- No pillows
- No duvets
- No cot bumpers
- No soft toys or positioners
- No sleep wedges or anti-roll devices

The safe sleep surface should have a firm, flat mattress with a fitted sheet over it. Two to three fitted sheets are practical — you will need to change the sheet at night and having spares means you do not have to do laundry at 3am.

### Bedding

For warmth, you have two options: cellular blankets or sleeping bags (sometimes called grobags or wearable blankets).

Cellular blankets — the thin, loosely woven cotton ones — are suitable from birth. You use one or two layers depending on the room temperature. The holes in the weave allow air to circulate, which reduces overheating. Two to three cellular blankets are enough.

Sleeping bags are an alternative that many parents prefer because they cannot be kicked off or pulled over the face during the night. They must be the correct tog rating for the room temperature and the correct size for the baby's weight. A sleeping bag that is too large is a suffocation risk. Most brands provide clear temperature and weight guidance.

The recommended room temperature for infant sleep is between 16 and 20 degrees Celsius, according to the Lullaby Trust.

## Feeding: Breastfeeding and Bottle Feeding Essentials

What you need for feeding depends on how you plan to feed — though many parents find their plans evolve once the baby arrives, so some overlap is sensible.

### If You Are Breastfeeding

- **Two to three nursing bras**: fitted later in pregnancy or in the early days when your milk comes in
- **Breast pads**: disposable or reusable — milk leaks are constant in the early weeks
- **Nipple cream**: lanolin-based creams such as Lansinoh are widely recommended by midwives for sore or cracked nipples
- **A manual breast pump**: useful for relieving engorgement and building up a supply without committing to a full electric pump from day one

A hospital-grade or high-quality electric pump is not necessary from birth. If you intend to return to work and pump regularly, you can assess your needs after feeding is established rather than spending significantly before the baby arrives.

The WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months of life, with continued breastfeeding alongside solid foods up to two years and beyond. This does not mean formula feeding is wrong — it means breastfeeding support from midwives and health visitors is worth accessing if you want to feed this way.

### If You Are Formula Feeding

- **Six or more bottles**: newborns feed eight to twelve times in 24 hours, so having enough bottles to get through a day without constant washing is genuinely useful
- **A steriliser**: either a microwave steriliser, cold water steriliser, or electric steam steriliser — all are effective
- **First infant formula**: choose any first infant formula (stage 1) — the NHS states they are all nutritionally equivalent and there is no clinical reason to choose one brand over another
- **A bottle brush**: for cleaning before sterilising

Avoid purchasing "hungry baby" or "comfort" formulas without paediatric advice. These are thicker and harder to digest, and are not recommended as a first feed.

## Clothing: How Much Do You Actually Need?

Newborn clothing has a few traps: buying too much in newborn size, buying for looks rather than practicality, and underestimating how fast babies grow.

### The Practical Wardrobe

- **Five to seven sleepsuits (babygros)**: these are the workhorses of newborn clothing — comfortable, warm, and easy to put on. Look for ones with fold-over scratch mitts at the cuffs, which eliminate the need for separate scratch mitts
- **Three to five vests (bodysuits)**: worn under sleepsuits for warmth, or alone in warm weather
- **Two hats**: newborns lose significant heat from the head; a cotton hat is needed indoors in cold conditions and outdoors in all seasons

Do not overbuy in newborn size. Many babies born at average weight outgrow newborn sizing within two to four weeks. Buying primarily in 0-3 months sizing is safer. If you are expecting a larger baby, you may find newborn size is bypassed entirely.

Special occasion outfits for newborns are a very low priority. Babies spend the vast majority of the first weeks in sleepsuits. Tiny formal outfits are often gifted and rarely worn.

Baby shoes have no function before a child is walking. They are decorative only and some evidence suggests tight, stiff shoes can interfere with foot development. They are not on the essentials list.

## Changing: Nappies, Cream, and Cotton Wool

### Nappies

Stock up before the birth, but not excessively in newborn size. A reasonable starting quantity is one pack of newborn and two packs of size 1. Some babies skip newborn nappies entirely.

Newborns typically go through eight to twelve nappy changes per day in the early weeks.

### Wipes and Cotton Wool

The NHS recommends using plain cotton wool and warm water for nappy changes during the first four to six weeks. Fragrance-free, alcohol-free baby wipes can be introduced after this period. Wipes for the first few weeks are fine to have in reserve, but cotton wool is genuinely preferred by most midwives and health visitors for the earliest weeks.

### Barrier Cream

A zinc oxide barrier cream such as Sudocrem or Bepanthen should be applied at every nappy change to protect the skin. Nappy rash is extremely common and largely preventable with consistent barrier cream use and prompt nappy changes.

### Changing Mat

A wipe-clean changing mat is essential — you will use it constantly. A padded changing unit is a luxury item; a mat placed on the floor or a firm surface works just as well and is arguably safer as there is no fall risk.

A nappy bin with odour control is helpful but not essential. A standard bin with a lid and nappy bags achieves the same result.

## Bathing: What You Need and What You Do Not

- **A baby bath tub or bath support seat**: both work; a baby bath tub on a stable surface is easier for back care, but a moulded support that sits inside the main bath takes up less storage
- **Two hooded towels**: soft, warm, and practical; the hood helps keep the head warm after bathing
- **Mild, fragrance-free baby wash**: not needed for the first month — the NHS advises plain water only during this period

You do not need: bath thermometers (your elbow is accurate enough), elaborate bath toy sets (newborns cannot interact with toys), or bath seats that claim to be safety devices (they are not).

## Travel: Car Seat and Getting Around

### Car Seat

A rear-facing infant car seat is a legal requirement from birth in the UK. Your baby cannot leave the hospital without one fitted correctly in your vehicle. This is non-negotiable and cannot be borrowed secondhand safely — see the buying guide below.

Group 0+ infant carrier seats (suitable from birth to approximately 13kg) are the standard starting point. Ensure the seat is compatible with your vehicle and has been fitted correctly — many car seat retailers offer fitting checks.

### Pram, Pushchair, or Carrier

You need a way to transport your baby, but the format depends on your lifestyle.

A pram or travel system that includes a carrycot suitable for newborn flat sleeping is practical for longer outings. A carrier or sling that positions the baby in the M-shape (knees higher than bottom) and supports the head is an effective alternative, particularly for parents who want their hands free. Baby Wearing UK and similar organisations offer sling libraries where you can try different carriers before buying.

You do not need both a pram and a carrier from day one — assess what suits your life and acquire the second if needed.

## What You Do Not Need in the Newborn Stage

The following items are frequently marketed to new parents but have no functional value in the first weeks and months:

- **Baby food maker**: solid foods are not introduced until around six months. A hand blender purchased when needed does the same job.
- **Highchair**: not needed until weaning begins at around six months.
- **Baby walker**: actively discouraged by the AAP and NHS — there is evidence they delay walking and increase injury risk.
- **Wipe warmer**: unnecessary; room-temperature wipes are fine.
- **Baby monitor for the same room**: the guidance from the Lullaby Trust is that babies should sleep in the same room as a parent for the first six months. A monitor for this period is redundant.
- **Elaborate nursery furniture**: nurseries are for older babies. A newborn has no use for a themed room and will sleep wherever it is safe and convenient.

## Second-Hand Buying Guide

Many newborn items can be bought secondhand in excellent condition, saving significant money. The exceptions are critical for safety reasons.

**Always buy new:**

- Car seats: you cannot verify whether a secondhand seat has been in a collision. Even a minor impact can compromise the structural integrity of a seat in a way that is invisible. The NHS and road safety organisations are consistent on this point.
- Cot mattresses: the link between used mattresses and sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS) is not fully understood, but the evidence is sufficient that the NHS recommends a new mattress for each new baby.

**Safe to buy secondhand** (if in good condition, from smoke-free and pet-free homes):

- Prams and pushchairs (check the harness and frame for damage)
- Baby bath tubs
- Bouncers and rockers (check for recalls first)
- Clothing
- Moses baskets (new mattress required)
- Carriers and slings (check stitching and buckles carefully)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many newborn nappies should I stock up on before birth?**

One to two packs of newborn size and two packs of size 1 is a reasonable starting point. Avoid buying large quantities of newborn nappies before birth — if your baby is larger than expected, they may skip that size entirely. You can always buy more once the baby arrives and you know what fits.

**Do I need both a Moses basket and a cot?**

Not immediately. Many families start with a Moses basket for the first two to three months when it fits beside the bed easily, then transition to a cot. A travel cot with a firm insert can also serve as a sleep space from birth and has longer-term value. You do not need both from day one.

**Is a breastfeeding pillow necessary?**

A breastfeeding pillow such as a Boppy or My Brestfriend can be very helpful for positioning, particularly during the early weeks when feeding can last a long time. It is not essential — rolled towels or regular pillows can achieve similar positioning support. It is worth trying before purchasing if a friend or postnatal group has one you can borrow.

**Can I use a baby monitor if my baby is in the same room?**

The Lullaby Trust and NHS guidance recommends having the baby in the same room for the first six months, which largely removes the need for a monitor during this period. If you have a large house and the baby will sometimes be in a different room during sleep, a monitor becomes useful at that point.

**Are baby bouncers and swings essential?**

They are not essential but many parents find them enormously useful for the periods when a baby needs to be set down safely but will not tolerate lying flat. A bouncy chair is relatively inexpensive and compact. A motorised swing is a larger investment — worth considering if a friend or postnatal group can advise on whether it worked for their baby.

**What should I do if my baby outgrows newborn clothes in weeks?**

This is normal. Newborn clothing is a very short-lived size. If you have received many newborn-sized gifts, keep tags on until you know they fit. Many parents find 0-3 month clothing serves them from birth just as well. Focus your clothing budget on 0-3 and 3-6 month sizes rather than loading up on the smallest sizes.

## Key Takeaways

- The genuine newborn essentials fit into six categories: sleep, feeding, clothing, changing, bathing, and travel — and each list is shorter than the baby industry suggests.
- Safe sleep requires a firm, flat, waterproof-covered mattress in a crib or Moses basket — no pillows, duvets, cot bumpers, or positioners.
- A rear-facing infant car seat is a legal requirement from birth; car seats and cot mattresses should always be purchased new for safety reasons.
- Two to three times per week for bathing is sufficient for a newborn; plain water is recommended by the NHS for the first month.
- Skip newborn-sized clothing in large quantities — most babies outgrow it within two to four weeks, and buying primarily in 0-3 months is more practical.
- Items marketed heavily to new parents — baby walkers, wipe warmers, elaborate nursery furniture, and baby food makers — have no function in the newborn stage.
