## What Happens During the Second Trimester?

The second trimester marks a significant transition in your pregnancy. Your baby transforms from a tiny, developing embryo into a fully formed little human with distinct features, working organs, and an increasingly active personality. Meanwhile, your body continues adapting to support this growing life, and you'll likely notice your pregnant silhouette becoming more pronounced with each passing week.

During these 14 weeks, your baby will grow from approximately 3.5 inches (the size of a lemon at week 14) to around 14 inches long (comparable to a head of cauliflower by week 27). Their weight increases dramatically too—from just over an ounce to roughly 2 pounds. This rapid growth means your body is working incredibly hard, even when you're resting.

For a detailed breakdown of each week's developments, check out our [Week by Week Guide](/pregnancy/week-by-week), which offers specific insights into what's happening with your baby and body at every stage.

### Key Milestones to Look Forward To

- **Week 14-15**: Baby's facial muscles develop, allowing them to squint, frown, and grimace
- **Week 16-18**: You might feel those first magical flutters of movement
- **Week 18-22**: Anatomy scan reveals detailed images of your baby
- **Week 20**: You've reached the halfway point of your pregnancy!
- **Week 23-24**: Baby's hearing develops, allowing them to recognise your voice
- **Week 27**: Baby's eyes can open and close, and they may respond to light

## Your Baby's Development: Week-by-Week Highlights

Understanding how your baby develops during the second trimester can help you feel more connected to the little one growing inside you. Let's explore this fascinating journey in more detail.

### Weeks 14-17: Foundation Building

During these early second-trimester weeks, your baby's body systems become increasingly sophisticated. Their kidneys begin producing urine, their liver starts making bile, and their spleen begins producing red blood cells. Fine, downy hair called lanugo covers their body, helping regulate temperature. By week 17, your baby's skeleton is hardening from soft cartilage into bone, and their sweat glands are forming.

Your baby is also becoming more active, though you probably won't feel these movements just yet. They're practising sucking and swallowing, preparing for life outside the womb. Their fingerprints—completely unique to them—are forming during this period.

### Weeks 18-22: The Quickening

This period often brings one of pregnancy's most magical moments: feeling your baby move for the first time. First-time mothers typically notice these sensations between weeks 18 and 22, often describing them as butterflies, gas bubbles, or gentle tapping. Don't worry if you haven't felt anything yet—anterior placentas (positioned at the front of the uterus) can cushion movements and delay detection.

The anatomy scan, usually scheduled between weeks 18 and 22, offers an incredible detailed look at your baby. The sonographer will check your baby's organs, measure their growth, assess the placenta and amniotic fluid, and—if you choose to find out—may be able to determine your baby's sex.

### Weeks 23-27: Rapid Growth and Sensory Development

Your baby's senses come alive during this period. Their hearing develops enough to recognise your voice, your heartbeat, and even music played near your belly. Many parents enjoy talking, singing, or reading to their babies during this time—research suggests babies may remember these sounds after birth.

By week 24, your baby reaches an important milestone called "viability," meaning they would have a chance of survival if born prematurely, though they would need extensive medical support. Their lungs are developing surfactant, a substance that will help them breathe air after birth.

As week 27 approaches, your baby's eyes can open and close, they're developing sleep-wake cycles, and their brain is growing rapidly. They may even have hiccups, which you'll feel as rhythmic movements!

## Physical Changes and Common Symptoms

Your body undergoes remarkable transformations during the second trimester. While many women find this period more comfortable than the first trimester, new symptoms emerge as your pregnancy progresses.

### Welcome Changes

The good news first! Many women experience:

- **Increased energy**: As first-trimester fatigue fades, you may feel more like yourself again
- **Reduced nausea**: Morning sickness typically improves significantly, though some women experience it throughout pregnancy
- **The pregnancy glow**: Increased blood flow and hormonal changes can give your skin a luminous appearance
- **Thicker, shinier hair**: Hormones slow hair shedding, making your locks appear fuller
- **Growing bump**: Your uterus rises above your pelvis, creating that unmistakable pregnant silhouette

### Symptoms to Expect

- **Round ligament pain**: Sharp or jabbing sensations in your lower abdomen as ligaments stretch to support your growing uterus
- **Backache**: Your shifting centre of gravity and loosening ligaments can cause lower back discomfort
- **Nasal congestion**: Increased blood flow causes swelling in mucous membranes, leading to stuffiness
- **Skin changes**: Darkening of the linea nigra (the line running down your belly), and possibly melasma (patches of darker skin on the face)
- **Leg cramps**: Particularly common at night, possibly related to changes in circulation
- **Heartburn and indigestion**: Your growing uterus puts pressure on your stomach
- **Braxton Hicks contractions**: Practice contractions that are typically painless and irregular

If you're experiencing any symptoms that concern you, our [Symptom Checker](/tools/symptom-checker) can help you understand what's normal and when to contact your healthcare provider. Always reach out to your doctor or midwife if you have questions or worries about any symptoms you're experiencing.

## Nutrition and Exercise During the Second Trimester

Fuelling your body properly and staying active benefits both you and your growing baby throughout the second trimester.

### Eating Well for Two

Contrary to popular belief, you don't need to "eat for two" in terms of quantity. During the second trimester, you need approximately 340 extra calories per day—roughly equivalent to a banana with two tablespoons of peanut butter.

Focus on nutrient-dense foods that support your baby's development:

- **Iron-rich foods**: Lean red meat, poultry, beans, spinach, and fortified cereals help prevent anaemia and support increased blood production
- **Calcium**: Dairy products, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, and almonds support your baby's bone development
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: Salmon, sardines, walnuts, and flaxseeds support brain and eye development
- **Fibre**: Whole grains, fruits, vegetables, and legumes help prevent constipation
- **Protein**: Lean meats, eggs, legumes, and dairy support tissue growth

Stay well-hydrated by drinking at least 8-10 glasses of water daily. Adequate hydration helps maintain amniotic fluid levels, supports increased blood volume, and can reduce common pregnancy discomforts like headaches and constipation.

### Staying Active Safely

Exercise during pregnancy offers numerous benefits, including improved mood, better sleep, reduced back pain, and potentially easier labour and recovery. Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week, unless your healthcare provider advises otherwise.

Safe exercise options during the second trimester include:

- Walking and hiking on even terrain
- Swimming and water aerobics
- Prenatal yoga and Pilates
- Stationary cycling
- Low-impact aerobics
- Strength training with appropriate modifications

Avoid contact sports, activities with fall risks (like skiing or horseback riding), hot yoga, and exercises that involve lying flat on your back after week 20. Always listen to your body and stop exercising if you experience dizziness, chest pain, vaginal bleeding, or contractions.

## Important Tests and Appointments

The second trimester includes several important medical appointments and screening opportunities.

### Routine Antenatal Appointments

You'll typically see your midwife or doctor every four weeks during the second trimester. These appointments usually include:

- Blood pressure monitoring
- Urine tests (checking for protein and glucose)
- Measuring your fundal height (the distance from your pubic bone to the top of your uterus)
- Listening to your baby's heartbeat
- Discussing any symptoms or concerns

### Key Screening Tests

**Anatomy scan (18-22 weeks)**: This detailed ultrasound examines your baby's organs, limbs, brain, heart, and spine. It also checks the placenta position, amniotic fluid levels, and can often reveal your baby's sex.

**Glucose screening test (24-28 weeks)**: This test screens for gestational diabetes. You'll drink a sweet glucose solution and have your blood tested one hour later. If results are elevated, you'll undergo a longer diagnostic test.

**Blood tests**: Additional blood work may check for anaemia and measure your antibody levels if you're Rh-negative.

## Preparing for Baby's Arrival

While the second trimester may feel too early for intense preparation, it's actually an ideal time to start thinking ahead. Your energy levels are typically higher now than they will be in the third trimester, making it easier to tackle bigger projects.

### Getting Organised

- **Start your baby registry**: Research products and create a wishlist of essentials. Our [Registry Checklist](/tools/registry-checklist) can help ensure you don't forget anything important.
- **Begin nursery planning**: Choose a colour scheme, order furniture with longer lead times, and start decorating at a comfortable pace.
- **Research childcare options**: If you'll need childcare after parental leave, now is the time to explore waiting lists, visit facilities, and interview potential carers.
- **Review your finances**: Assess your budget, understand your parental leave entitlements, and start building your baby fund.
- **Consider prenatal classes**: Book antenatal classes, breastfeeding workshops, and infant CPR courses while spaces are available.

### Pregnancy-Safe Travel

If you're planning a "babymoon" or any travel, the second trimester is generally the most comfortable time. Most airlines allow pregnant women to fly until 36 weeks for domestic flights (less for international), but always check specific policies and carry a note from your healthcare provider.

Stay hydrated, move around regularly during long journeys, wear compression socks to prevent blood clots, and know the location of medical facilities at your destination.

## Emotional Wellbeing and Relationship Changes

The second trimester brings its own emotional landscape. While you may feel more settled than during the anxiety-prone first trimester, other feelings often emerge.

### Navigating Your Emotions

Many expectant parents experience:

- **Excitement and anticipation** as the pregnancy feels more "real"
- **Anxiety about birth, parenting, or health concerns**
- **Body image adjustments** as your shape changes
- **Nesting instincts** and urges to prepare and organise
- **Increased connection** with your baby, especially after feeling movement

These feelings are all normal. If you're experiencing persistent sadness, overwhelming anxiety, or thoughts of self-harm, please reach out to your healthcare provider immediately. Antenatal depression and anxiety are common and treatable.

### Strengthening Your Partnership

Pregnancy affects relationships, too. Keep communication open with your partner about fears, expectations, and hopes for your growing family. Attend appointments together when possible, discuss parenting values, and make time for your relationship beyond pregnancy talk.

Intimacy often improves during the second trimester as nausea fades and energy returns. For most women with uncomplicated pregnancies, sexual activity remains safe throughout pregnancy. However, always discuss any concerns with your healthcare provider.

## Your Second Trimester Journey Awaits

The second trimester is truly a special time in your pregnancy journey. As your baby grows from a tiny, developing being into a moving, hearing, responsive little person, you'll experience countless moments of wonder and connection. Embrace this "golden period"—the increased energy, the exciting milestones, and the joy of watching your bump grow.

Remember, every pregnancy is unique. Your experience may differ from what you read or hear from others, and that's completely normal. Trust your instincts, lean on your support system, and don't hesitate to reach out to your healthcare provider with any questions or concerns.

You're doing an amazing job growing a tiny human. Take care of yourself, celebrate each milestone, and know that you're exactly the right parent for your baby. The adventure is only just beginning!
