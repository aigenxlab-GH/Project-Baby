# Baby Registry Needs vs Wants: How to Build a Registry You'll Actually Use

The baby industry generates billions in revenue every year. A significant portion of that revenue comes from new parents and gift-givers who genuinely cannot distinguish between what a newborn requires and what a brand has convinced them is essential. Building a registry without a framework leaves you vulnerable to both over-spending and under-preparing.

This guide applies a clear filter: what does a baby actually need in the first few months, what is genuinely useful but optional, and what is a clever product that no one needs at all.

## The Marketing Problem

Baby product marketing is specifically designed to conflate safety and love with spending. If you see language like "every parent needs" or "give your baby the best start," pause. Those phrases are not medical guidance. They are persuasion.

The NHS, the AAP, and the WHO all have published guidance on what babies genuinely require for health, safety, and development. None of those lists include wipe warmers, elaborate nursery sets, or baby food makers for a child who will not eat solids for six months.

A registry that starts from clinical guidance and then layers in personal preferences is far easier to defend to well-meaning relatives and far more likely to result in items that get used.

## The Four Categories of Genuine Need

A useful way to approach a registry is to start with the four areas where a baby has non-negotiable requirements in the early months: safe sleep, feeding, transportation, and nappy care. Everything else comes after.

### Safe Sleep

A baby needs a safe sleep surface from day one. The NHS, AAP, and The Lullaby Trust all specify a firm, flat, waterproof-covered mattress in a cot, crib, or Moses basket. The mattress must be new — second-hand mattresses carry a higher SIDS risk, according to The Lullaby Trust research.

What you need: a cot, crib, or Moses basket that meets current safety standards, and a new mattress that fits it snugly with no gaps at the sides.

What you do not need: cot bumpers (unsafe — associated with suffocation risk), a decorative duvet (not used in the cot until 12 months at earliest), a decorative pillow (not for use in the sleep space), a co-sleeper pod or nest (these are not recommended for overnight unsupervised sleep by the AAP or NHS).

### Feeding

The feeding category depends on how you plan to feed. But some items cross both paths.

For breastfeeding parents: at least one nursing bra (ideally two), breast pads, lanolin nipple cream, and a hospital-grade breast pump or a new personal pump. Do not register for a second-hand pump — the internal components that come into contact with milk are not fully cleanable between users, and hospital-grade rental services exist specifically for parents who need premium pump access.

For formula feeding: four to six bottles in a smaller size (some babies reject certain teat shapes — do not stock up on one brand before birth), a bottle brush, a steriliser. All UK formula, whether branded or own-label, must meet the same EU-derived regulatory nutritional standards. Own-brand formula is nutritionally identical to Aptamil or SMA.

For both: around five to eight muslin squares. These are genuinely one of the most versatile items in the first year — for burping, for a barrier during nappy changes, as a light sun cover, as a comfort object. Register for more than you think you need.

### Transportation

An infant car seat is a legal requirement from birth in the UK. You cannot leave the hospital after a car birth without one. It must be correctly installed, not expired (most car seats have a 10-year expiry from manufacture), and — this is important — bought new or with 100% verified crash-free history.

Structural damage from a crash is often invisible. A car seat that has been in even a minor impact may have compromised its ability to protect a child in a subsequent collision. The NHS and road safety organisations consistently advise buying new unless you have direct knowledge of the seat's history.

You also need a way to transport your baby without a car. A pram or pushchair, or a carrier or sling, covers this. These are items where second-hand purchase is generally safe if the frame is undamaged and any harness works correctly.

### Nappy Changing

You need nappies — far more than you expect. A newborn goes through eight to twelve nappies per day in the early weeks. Register for a range of sizes, not just newborn; some babies skip newborn entirely. Wipes are non-negotiable.

A changing mat — portable, wipe-clean, with a curved edge — is all you need for a changing surface. A full changing table is a convenience, not a necessity. A portable changing mat can go on any flat surface, can be used in any room, and costs a fraction of a changing unit.

A nappy cream for preventing and treating nappy rash is worth having from the start.

## The Middle Tier: Genuinely Useful but Optional

These items are not required but are commonly found helpful by a significant proportion of parents.

A bouncer or swing can soothe a fussy baby and give you a safe space to put the baby while both your hands are occupied. Simple bouncers start at under £30. Expensive swings with multiple speeds, music, and Bluetooth connectivity are not more effective at soothing than a basic model. Register for what fits your space and budget.

A baby monitor is only necessary if the baby sleeps in a separate room. Many families in small flats or homes spend the first months with the baby in the same room, as recommended by the NHS and AAP for the first six months. In that context, a monitor adds nothing. If the house is larger and the nursery is distant, an audio monitor is sufficient — a video monitor adds reassurance but is not a safety device.

A nursing pillow supports breastfeeding positioning and can help with reflux positioning too. It is helpful but not strictly necessary — a rolled blanket or firm cushion serves the same function. If breastfeeding is planned, it is a reasonable registry item.

A carrier or sling is particularly useful for parents who do a lot of walking, live in areas with limited pram access, or have a baby who settles better when held. There are many styles and carriers. It is worth attending a local sling library consultation — most UK areas have one — before registering, to find what works for your body shape and preferences.

## What to Skip

There are several product categories that appear on registry lists because companies market them aggressively to pregnant parents, not because babies need them.

Wipe warmers are a luxury with significant practical downsides — wipes dry out, units require regular cleaning to prevent mould, and you cannot use them anywhere other than where the unit is plugged in. Warming a wipe in your hands for 10 seconds is free and equally effective.

Bottle warmers sound sensible but are rarely used long-term. A jug of warm water heats a bottle in two to three minutes. Many parents switch to this within weeks of buying an appliance.

Baby food makers will not be needed for approximately six months, when solids are introduced. A blender or even a fork handles the food preparation a baby actually needs. This is not a first-year purchase.

Baby shoes for pre-walking babies serve no developmental purpose and can actually interfere with natural foot development, according to paediatric guidance. Socks or soft bootees for warmth are fine. Hard-soled shoes before a child is walking independently are not recommended.

Coordinated nursery bedding sets — the bumper, the decorative duvet, the matching pillowcase — are sold as aesthetics, not baby requirements. The bumper is unsafe and should not be in the cot at all. The duvet is not safe for use until 12 months. The decorative element is for your eye, not for your baby.

## What Parents Wish They Had Registered For

The gap between pre-birth expectations and postpartum reality is significant. In surveys and parenting forums, the items new parents most commonly wish they had on their registry include things that support the parent, not just the baby.

Postpartum supplies for the recovering parent are genuinely useful and rarely thought of. High-waisted soft underwear that sits above a caesarean scar. Maternity pads for the first weeks of lochia. Nipple cream in sufficient quantity. Comfortable nursing bras that fit correctly. These are items that directly affect how the first weeks feel — and they are rarely on anyone's registry because they require thinking about the parent's recovery, not just the baby.

Food delivery or meal kit subscriptions reduce one of the biggest practical pressures on new families: making dinner when you are exhausted and have both hands occupied. A registry gift of a meal delivery subscription or a voucher for a local restaurant that delivers is far more useful to most new parents than another set of baby bath toys.

Professional house cleaning for the first month is another gift that is used rather than stored. An hour of professional cleaning once a week removes a source of anxiety in the fourth trimester without the new parent having to ask a relative to do it.

Freezer meal prep — either done by a friend group as a gift, or registered for via a meal prep service — is one of the most appreciated postpartum supports. Having twelve ready meals in the freezer going into the last month of pregnancy changes the early weeks materially.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many babygros and vests do I actually need?**

For the newborn period, aim for five to seven babygros and four to five vests in newborn size, and the same again in 0-3 months. You will wash them frequently. Having too many means you are buying sizes the baby will not grow into. Many parents buy in 0-3 months only, as some babies skip newborn sizing entirely — particularly if born at or above average birth weight.

**Do I need a bath tub for a baby?**

Not strictly. A clean sink with warm water works for the first weeks. A dedicated baby bath tub is convenient but not necessary. If registered for, choose a model with a foam insert for newborns or a sloping back that keeps the baby's head elevated safely.

**Should I register for both a pram and a carrier?**

Many parents find both useful for different contexts. A pram is easier for longer walks and shopping. A carrier is better for narrow spaces, stairs, and settling a baby who wants to be close. If budget is a concern, a carrier costs significantly less than a pram and does more in terms of contact. A pram can wait until the baby is a few weeks old and you have a better sense of your daily movements.

**How do I choose between a Moses basket and a crib?**

A Moses basket is portable, light, and can move from room to room. Its usable life is typically three to four months, until the baby can pull themselves up. A crib lasts longer — often to six months — and some convert to a cot. If you plan to have the baby nap in the living room during the day, a Moses basket is practical. If space is limited, a crib or bedside crib that attaches to the parent's bed is more economical in the long run.

**Can I register at more than one store?**

Yes, and it is worth doing so to give gift-givers flexibility. Many parents maintain a registry at a larger retailer for big items and a secondary list on Amazon or a universal registry site for everything else. Just ensure there is no duplication of big-ticket items across registries, as receiving two of the same large product creates its own logistical problems.

**When should I build the registry?**

Most parents find that starting at around 20 weeks gives enough time to research without feeling rushed, but late enough to have passed the higher-anxiety first trimester. Completing it by 28 to 30 weeks means it is available if a baby shower is organised, and gives time to make adjustments.

## Key Takeaways

- The baby industry benefits from parents buying more than they need. Start registry-building from the four genuine need categories: safe sleep, feeding, transportation, and nappy care.
- The car seat and cot mattress must be bought new. These are safety items where the risks of second-hand purchase are documented.
- Many of the most-regretted baby purchases — wipe warmers, bottle warmers, coordinated nursery sets, baby food makers — will not be used long enough to justify their cost.
- Postpartum recovery supplies for the parent — pads, comfortable underwear, nipple cream, nursing bras — are under-registered for and genuinely needed.
- Practical support items like meal delivery, freezer meals, and professional cleaning are among the most appreciated new-parent gifts and are worth adding to a registry.
- When in doubt about a product category, ask a parent who has a baby 6 to 12 months old. Their perspective on what got used versus what gathered dust is more useful than any manufacturer's claim.
