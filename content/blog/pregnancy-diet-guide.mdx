Eating well during pregnancy is one of the most powerful things you can do for your baby's development — and for your own energy, mood, and recovery. But with contradictory advice everywhere, it can feel overwhelming.

This guide cuts through the noise. Here's what actually matters, based on current guidance from leading health organisations.

## The Most Critical Nutrients in Pregnancy

### Folate/Folic Acid: The Most Important Nutrient

Folate (vitamin B9) is critical in the very earliest weeks of pregnancy — often before you even know you're pregnant — because it prevents neural tube defects like spina bifida.

**What to do:**
- Take a prenatal vitamin with at least 400mcg of folic acid daily — ideally starting before conception
- Eat folate-rich foods daily: dark leafy greens, lentils, black beans, asparagus, fortified cereals
- If you have a personal or family history of neural tube defects, your doctor may recommend a higher dose (5mg)

### Iron: For You and Baby

Your blood volume increases by 50% during pregnancy. Iron supports this increase and prevents anemia, which causes fatigue and can affect your baby's development.

**Best sources:**
- Red meat, chicken, fish
- Lentils, chickpeas, kidney beans
- Spinach and dark leafy greens
- Fortified cereals

**Absorption tip:** Eat iron-rich foods with vitamin C (orange juice, bell peppers, strawberries) to significantly increase absorption. Avoid tea, coffee, and calcium-rich foods within an hour of iron-rich meals.

### Calcium: Building Baby's Bones

Your baby needs calcium for bone and tooth development. If you don't consume enough, your body draws calcium from your own bones.

**Daily target:** 1,000mg (1,300mg if you're under 18)

**Best sources:**
- Dairy: milk, yoghurt, cheese
- Fortified plant milks
- Sardines and salmon with bones
- Tofu, broccoli, kale

### DHA: Brain and Eye Development

Omega-3 fatty acids — particularly DHA — are critical for your baby's brain and eye development, especially in the third trimester.

**Best sources:**
- Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel — low mercury varieties)
- DHA-enriched eggs
- Algae-based DHA supplements (great for vegetarians)

Most prenatal vitamins don't contain enough DHA — check yours and consider a separate supplement.

### Vitamin D

Many pregnant women are deficient in vitamin D, which is essential for bone health, immune function, and your baby's development.

**Sources:**
- Sunlight (10–15 minutes daily)
- Fortified dairy and plant milks
- Egg yolks, fatty fish
- Most prenatal vitamins (check the dose — 600 IU is the minimum, 1,000–2,000 IU may be recommended)

## What to Eat Every Day

Think of your daily diet in terms of food groups rather than strict meals:

| Food Group | Daily Target | Best Choices |
|------------|-------------|-------------|
| Vegetables | 5+ servings | Dark leafy greens, sweet potato, broccoli, bell peppers |
| Fruit | 3–4 servings | Berries, citrus, mango, avocado |
| Whole grains | 6–8 servings | Oats, brown rice, whole wheat bread, quinoa |
| Protein | 3 servings | Lean meat, fish, eggs, beans, lentils, tofu |
| Dairy/calcium | 3 servings | Milk, yoghurt, cheese, fortified alternatives |
| Healthy fats | Daily | Avocado, olive oil, nuts, seeds, fatty fish |

## Protein and Omega-3 in the Third Trimester

The nutritional demands of pregnancy increase most significantly in the third trimester, when your baby is growing rapidly and laying down fat stores, developing lung maturity, and finalising brain structure.

### Protein Needs

Protein requirements increase substantially in the second half of pregnancy. The Reference Nutrient Intake (RNI) for protein in the UK rises by approximately 6g per day above baseline during pregnancy, but many nutrition researchers and midwives note that women who are active or carrying a large baby may benefit from more. Practical targets vary, but aiming for a source of protein at every meal and most snacks covers most needs.

The richest protein sources in pregnancy are: lean red meat, poultry, fish and seafood, eggs, dairy, tofu, legumes, and nuts. These also overlap significantly with other critical nutrients — eggs provide DHA and choline; legumes provide iron and folate; fish provides both protein and omega-3.

### Omega-3 DHA: Why the Third Trimester Matters Most

DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) is an omega-3 fatty acid that accumulates most rapidly in the fetal brain during the third trimester. The final 12 weeks of pregnancy see a dramatic increase in brain growth and the laying down of the neural architecture that underpins cognitive function, visual acuity, and language development. DHA is the primary structural fat of the brain and retina.

NHS guidance recommends consuming oily fish as a source of DHA during pregnancy, specifically:
- Up to 2 portions of oily fish per week (salmon, mackerel, sardines, trout, herring)
- The 2-portion limit exists due to pollutant accumulation in oily fish, not because oily fish is harmful — it remains a recommended food

For women who do not eat oily fish — whether through preference, allergy, or a vegetarian or vegan diet — algae-based DHA supplements are the recommended alternative. Algae is the original source of DHA in the marine food chain (fish accumulate it by eating algae or other fish that eat algae), and algae-derived DHA supplements are widely available, evidence-backed, and suitable in pregnancy.

Check whether your prenatal vitamin contains DHA. Many do not, or contain quantities lower than those associated with benefit in research. A combined EPA and DHA daily supplement of 200–300mg DHA is a common evidence-based recommendation, but discuss the appropriate dose with your midwife or GP if you are unsure.

## Foods to Limit — Not Necessarily Avoid

A significant amount of anxiety around pregnancy nutrition stems from lists of foods to avoid presented without nuance. Several foods that are often treated as forbidden are actually safe within defined limits — and eliminating them entirely may remove important nutrients.

### Caffeine: The 200mg Limit

The NHS and WHO both set the safe upper limit for caffeine in pregnancy at 200mg per day. Above this level, caffeine intake is associated with increased risk of lower birth weight and pregnancy loss. Below it, the evidence does not show harm.

200mg of caffeine is approximately:
- 1 mug of filter coffee (around 140mg per mug)
- 2 cups of tea (around 75mg per cup)
- 5 cans of cola (around 40mg per 330ml can)
- 2 shots of espresso (around 100mg per shot, but this varies significantly)

This means a daily cup of tea or a single coffee is within safe limits. You do not need to give up caffeine entirely — you need to track and cap your total intake from all sources including chocolate, cola, and energy drinks. Green tea also contains caffeine.

### Tuna: Weekly Limits, Not an Absolute Ban

Tuna contains mercury, which accumulates in large, long-lived fish and can harm fetal neurological development in high doses. However, tuna is also a highly nutritious food containing protein, B vitamins, and omega-3 fatty acids — it should not be eliminated, simply limited.

NHS guidance specifies:
- No more than 2 tuna steaks per week (fresh or frozen)
- No more than 4 medium-sized cans of tuna per week

Light canned tuna (skipjack) contains less mercury than albacore (white) tuna. Keeping within these limits means you can eat tuna regularly throughout pregnancy without concern.

### Oily Fish: Up to 2 Portions Per Week

As noted above, oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel, trout, herring) is both recommended and limited to 2 portions per week in pregnancy. The reason for the limit is pollutant accumulation — polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and dioxins — not mercury in this case. The DHA, protein, vitamin D, and iodine in oily fish make it one of the most valuable foods you can eat in pregnancy. Two portions per week provides benefit while keeping pollutant exposure within accepted safe levels.

The goal with both tuna and oily fish is informed consumption within recommended limits — not avoidance. A nutrient-rich food is removed only when the risks of eating it outweigh the benefits; for fish eaten within guidance, the reverse is true.

## Foods to Avoid During Pregnancy

### High-Mercury Fish
Mercury accumulates in fish tissue and can harm your baby's developing nervous system. Avoid:
- Swordfish, shark, king mackerel, tilefish, bigeye tuna
- Limit albacore (canned white) tuna to 1 serving per week

Safe fish (2–3 servings per week): salmon, sardines, tilapia, catfish, light canned tuna, shrimp

### Raw or Undercooked Meat and Eggs
Risk of salmonella, listeria, and toxoplasma. Avoid:
- Raw or rare meat, poultry, or seafood
- Sushi (raw fish varieties)
- Runny eggs or foods containing raw eggs (homemade mayo, raw cookie dough)
- Deli meats unless heated until steaming (listeria risk)

### Unpasteurised Foods
- Unpasteurised cheeses (some brie, camembert, feta, blue cheese)
- Raw or unpasteurised milk and juices
- Soft-serve ice cream (listeria risk from contaminated machines)

### Alcohol
No safe amount of alcohol has been identified during pregnancy. The advice is straightforward: avoid it completely.

### Liver and Vitamin A Supplements
Liver is very high in vitamin A — in large amounts, this can harm your baby's development. Avoid:
- Liver and liver products (pâté, liverwurst)
- High-dose vitamin A supplements (retinol form)
- Multivitamins not specifically designed for pregnancy

## Managing Common Food Challenges

### Food Aversions
Up to 85% of women experience food aversions in pregnancy. If healthy foods are triggering nausea, focus on what you can tolerate. A few weeks of suboptimal eating won't harm your baby — severe, prolonged restriction is the concern.

See our detailed guide on [morning sickness remedies](/blog/morning-sickness-remedies) for strategies to manage nausea while maintaining nutrition.

### Vegetarian and Vegan Pregnancies

Vegetarian and vegan pregnancies are entirely healthy with proper planning. Focus on:
- B12 supplementation (essential if vegan)
- Iron from plant sources + vitamin C for absorption
- DHA from algae-based supplements
- Calcium from fortified foods, tofu, green vegetables
- Protein combining across the day (beans, grains, nuts, seeds)

Work with your midwife or a dietitian to ensure your prenatal covers all needs.

### Gestational Diabetes

If you're diagnosed with gestational diabetes, you'll receive specific dietary guidance from your healthcare team. General principles include:
- Spreading carbohydrates evenly across 3 meals and 2–3 snacks
- Choosing complex carbohydrates over refined ones
- Pairing carbohydrates with protein and fat to slow blood sugar rise
- Monitoring blood glucose as directed

## Meal Ideas by Trimester

### First Trimester: Focus on What Stays Down
Nausea may rule your diet — that's okay. Aim for:
- Plain crackers, toast, rice
- Cold foods (less smell)
- Small, frequent meals
- Prenatal vitamin with food

### Second Trimester: Build Nutritional Reserves
Most women feel better. This is the time to focus on quality:
- Iron-rich meals 5–6 times per week
- Calcium at every meal
- DHA 2–3 times per week
- Plenty of folate

### Third Trimester: Fuel for Labour and Recovery
- Continue all the above
- Increase protein intake — aim for a protein source at every meal
- Prioritise DHA from oily fish or algae supplement for fetal brain development
- Focus on iron for blood loss during delivery
- Vitamin C daily for tissue repair and iron absorption
- Hydration becomes even more critical

## The Simple Bottom Line

A healthy pregnancy diet doesn't require perfection — it requires consistency. Fill most of your meals with vegetables, lean proteins, whole grains, and healthy fats. Take a quality prenatal vitamin daily. Avoid the high-risk foods listed above, and consume caffeine, tuna, and oily fish within the specified limits rather than cutting them out entirely.

Everything else? Mostly noise. Eat what you enjoy, stay hydrated, and be kind to yourself on the days when crackers and ginger ale are all that's going down.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I really need to give up coffee entirely during pregnancy?**
No. The NHS and WHO both allow up to 200mg of caffeine per day during pregnancy. This equates to approximately one mug of filter coffee, two cups of tea, or a combination of smaller amounts from multiple sources. The key is tracking your total daily caffeine from all sources — tea, coffee, cola, chocolate, and energy drinks — and staying below 200mg. Exceeding this limit regularly is associated with lower birth weight and pregnancy loss; staying within it is considered safe.

**How much oily fish can I safely eat per week?**
NHS guidance recommends no more than 2 portions of oily fish per week during pregnancy. Oily fish includes salmon, sardines, mackerel, trout, and herring. This limit exists because oily fish can contain environmental pollutants that accumulate in fatty tissue. Within 2 portions, oily fish is an excellent source of DHA, protein, vitamin D, and iodine — it should not be avoided, just limited.

**I don't like fish. How do I get enough DHA?**
Algae-based DHA supplements are the recommended alternative for those who don't eat oily fish. Algae is the original source of DHA in the marine food chain — fish accumulate DHA precisely by eating algae or other fish that have eaten algae. Algae-based supplements provide directly bioavailable DHA without the fishy taste or the pollutant concerns associated with oily fish. Discuss supplementation with your midwife or GP for appropriate dosing.

**Is it safe to eat canned tuna every day?**
No. NHS guidance recommends no more than 2 tuna steaks or 4 medium cans of tuna per week due to mercury content. Eating tuna daily throughout pregnancy would exceed this limit and could expose your baby to mercury levels associated with neurological effects. Within the weekly limit, tuna is a nutritious and safe protein source.

**My prenatal vitamin says it contains omega-3 — is that enough?**
Check the label carefully. Many prenatal vitamins contain small amounts of omega-3 — often 50–100mg — which is below the 200–300mg of DHA per day associated with fetal brain benefit in research. If your prenatal provides less than 200mg of DHA, consider adding a separate algae-based DHA supplement. Fish oil supplements are also an option, but check they are molecularly distilled (to remove mercury and pollutants) and appropriate for pregnancy use.

**I'm in my first trimester and can barely eat. Will my baby be affected?**
In most cases, no. The embryo and early fetus draw on your nutrient stores during the first trimester, and a few weeks of limited eating due to nausea does not typically harm development. The most critical nutrient in the first trimester is folate — if you can do nothing else, take a prenatal vitamin with folic acid daily. Focus on whatever foods you can manage, stay hydrated, and know that the second trimester improvement in appetite gives you a window to rebuild nutritional quality.

## Key Takeaways

- The NHS and WHO both allow up to 200mg of caffeine daily — that is approximately one mug of filter coffee or two cups of tea, not a complete ban
- Oily fish provides essential DHA for fetal brain development and should be eaten up to 2 portions per week, not avoided; tuna is safe within 2 steaks or 4 cans per week
- DHA demand increases significantly in the third trimester; women who don't eat oily fish should use algae-based DHA supplements
- Protein needs increase throughout pregnancy and should be prioritised in the third trimester with a source at every meal
- The foods to genuinely avoid are high-mercury fish (swordfish, shark), raw or undercooked meat and eggs, unpasteurised products, alcohol, and liver — not simply "restrict"
- A quality prenatal vitamin with folic acid is non-negotiable from pre-conception through delivery; check whether it includes adequate DHA or whether a separate supplement is needed
