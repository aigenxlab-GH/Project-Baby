Packing your hospital bag is one of those third-trimester milestones that feels equal parts exciting and overwhelming. Pack too little and you'll be sending someone out for supplies mid-labour. Pack too much and you'll be dragging two suitcases through the maternity ward.

This guide covers exactly what experienced mothers (and their birth partners) actually use — stripped of everything that sounds nice in theory but sits untouched in the bag.

## When to Pack

**Pack by week 36.** If you're carrying multiples, or have any pregnancy complications, pack by week 32–34. Labour has a way of arriving unexpectedly, and the last thing you want during early contractions is to be frantically searching for your insurance card.

Pro tip: keep a list on your phone of last-minute items to grab (phone charger, snacks, birth plan) so you don't need to pack them weeks in advance.

## For Labour: What You'll Actually Use

### Comfort Items
- **Your own pillow** — hospital pillows are notoriously flat; bring one with a colourful case so it doesn't get mixed up
- **Warm, non-slip socks** — hospital floors are cold and you'll be on your feet
- **Lip balm** — you'll use this more than almost anything else; labour causes dry lips
- **Hair ties** — multiple; they disappear
- **Cooling face mist or small fan** — labour is hot work
- **Massage tools or tennis balls** — for back pain relief (ask your partner to do this)

### Practical Items
- **Birth plan copies** (at least 3 printed copies) — see our guide on [how to write a birth plan](/blog/how-to-write-a-birth-plan)
- **Insurance cards and ID**
- **Phone and charger** — a long cable is useful
- **Camera or memory card** — or just use your phone
- **Headphones** — your own music/podcast during early labour
- **Snacks** — more on this below

### Snacks: Don't Underestimate This
Labour can last many hours. Even if you can't eat during active labour, early labour is long and you'll be hungry. Your birth partner will definitely be hungry. Pack:
- Cereal bars, trail mix, and crackers
- Isotonic sports drinks (for energy and hydration during labour)
- Lollipops — surprisingly helpful during active labour for giving your mouth something to do
- Individual snack packs that are easy to eat one-handed

## For After Birth: Mum's Hospital Stay

### Clothing
- **2–3 nightgowns or pyjamas** — front-opening if breastfeeding; button-front is ideal
- **Robe** — for walking the corridors
- **Nursing bras** (2–3) — soft cup, wire-free; bring these even if you're undecided on feeding, as your milk will come in regardless
- **Comfortable underwear** (5+ pairs of disposable or very old pairs) — you'll be wearing maternity pads for days
- **Going-home outfit** — something loose; you'll still look 5–6 months pregnant immediately after birth

### Toiletries
- Shampoo, conditioner, and body wash (travel size)
- Face wash and moisturiser
- Dry shampoo (a post-birth hero product)
- Toothbrush and toothpaste
- Deodorant
- Gentle makeup if desired — for photos

### Post-Birth Essentials
- **Maternity pads** (the thickest you can find — bring at least 20) — the hospital provides some but not enough
- **Witch hazel pads** (Tucks) or perineal spray — for soreness after a vaginal birth
- **Nipple cream** (lanolin-based) — start applying before you even need it; your nipples will thank you from day one
- **Nipple pads** (breast pads) — your milk may come in on day 2–4 and leaking is very common even before that
- **Stool softener** — ask your provider about this; the first post-birth bowel movement is notoriously anxiety-inducing

## What to Pack for a Caesarean

If you know in advance you're having a planned caesarean, or if you have any reason to think an emergency caesarean might be possible, pack specifically for it. The standard list above applies, but these additions make a real difference to your recovery.

### Clothing for After a C-Section
- **Loose, high-waisted trousers or skirts** — anything that sits above the scar line. Low-waisted jeans or trousers that press directly on your incision will be genuinely painful for several weeks. Maternity trousers with a soft waistband often work well post-surgery.
- **Knickers that sit above the scar** — standard post-surgery advice from NHS maternity units. High-leg cotton knickers rather than bikini-cut styles reduce irritation significantly.
- **No tight waistbands at all** for the first 2–4 weeks — skirts and dresses are often the most comfortable option in the early weeks.

### For the Journey Home
- **A firm pillow to hold against your abdomen** — holding a pillow over the incision site when you cough, sneeze, laugh, or go over a bump in the car is standard advice given by NHS midwives. It provides counter-pressure and dramatically reduces the sharp pulling sensation. Pack this in the bag, not the boot of the car.

### Digestive Comfort After a C-Section
- **Peppermint oil capsules** — trapped wind after abdominal surgery is one of the most uncomfortable and undertalked aspects of C-section recovery. Gas moves upwards and can cause significant shoulder and chest pain in the first 24–48 hours. Peppermint oil capsules or peppermint tea help to dispel this. Many hospitals do not routinely offer these, so bring your own.
- **Mild laxative** — ask your midwife or GP before discharge about an appropriate gentle laxative. After abdominal surgery, bowel function slows considerably. The combination of anaesthetic, pain medication (particularly codeine), reduced mobility, and anxiety about straining makes constipation almost universal. Your care team may prescribe something, but it is worth knowing to ask.

### Nursing Bra Specifics for C-Section Recovery
Standard nursing bra guidance applies — wire-free, soft cup — but with one additional criterion: you need a bra you can open **entirely one-handed**. After a C-section, getting your other arm into a position to manipulate clips or fasteners may be uncomfortable or impossible in the early days. Front-clasp nursing bras or simple crossover styles are the most practical. Practise opening yours before you go into hospital.

## For Baby

### Clothing
- **2–3 newborn (NB) babygrows/sleepsuits** — with built-in scratch mitts if possible
- **2–3 0-3 month babygrows** — newborn size sometimes only fits for days
- **Going-home outfit** — something that goes over the head easily or has a wide neck
- **Hat** (2) — newborns lose heat rapidly through their heads; the hospital may provide one but bring your own too
- **Muslin cloths** (4–6) — these are multipurpose: swaddle, burp cloth, sun shade

### Feeding Supplies
- **Breast pump** (if using) — particularly useful for initial milk establishment
- **Ready-made formula** (if formula feeding) — 4–6 individual ready-to-feed bottles; far easier than making up feeds in hospital
- **2–4 bottles** with slow-flow teats
- **Sterilising tablets** — for on-the-go sterilising in the sink

### Nappy Changing
- **Nappies** (1 small pack of newborn size) — the hospital provides some but packs run out
- **Nappy cream** — Bepanthen or similar
- **Unscented baby wipes** or cotton wool pads with water

### Going Home
- **Car seat** — properly fitted and installed before birth; without this, you cannot legally leave the hospital with your baby
- **Baby blanket** — for warmth on the way home

## For Your Birth Partner

Don't forget the person who'll be with you for potentially 24+ hours:

- Change of clothes
- Toothbrush and basic toiletries
- Phone and charger
- Snacks and drinks
- Cash for the vending machine and car park
- Something to do during slow periods (book, tablet, downloaded Netflix)
- Comfortable shoes — they'll be on their feet too

## What to Leave Home

- Expensive jewellery or valuables
- Heavy books (you won't read them)
- Too many fancy clothes for baby (they'll be in a nappy and babygrow)
- Elaborate birth plan portfolios — one page, three copies is plenty
- Your entire skincare routine

## Packing Your Bag: Practical Tips

1. **Use separate bags** — one for labour, one for post-birth, one for baby. Clearly labelled bags save enormous frustration when your birth partner is searching for nappies at 3am.

2. **Roll clothes** rather than folding — saves space significantly.

3. **Keep it in your car from week 36** — so you're never caught unprepared.

4. **Tell your birth partner where everything is** — they need to be able to find things quickly.

## The Most Important Thing

All of this is preparation — but the hospital you're going to has delivered thousands of babies. They have everything you truly need. The bag exists for your comfort, not your survival.

Pack it, tick it off your list, and let yourself focus on the incredible thing that's about to happen.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a separate bag for a planned C-section?**
Not necessarily a separate bag, but you do need specific items added to the standard list. The most important additions are: high-waisted, loose trousers or skirts that don't press on the scar; a firm pillow for the car journey home; peppermint oil capsules for trapped wind; a gentle laxative; and a nursing bra you can operate one-handed. Prepare these before your admission date.

**How many maternity pads do I actually need in hospital?**
Bring at least 20 for a 1–2 night stay. Postpartum bleeding (lochia) is heaviest in the first 24–72 hours and you may go through several pads per hour initially. The hospital provides some, but not enough. NHS guidance recommends having plenty available — it is one of the most commonly underestimated items.

**Can I eat and drink during labour?**
NHS guidance allows eating and drinking during low-risk labour. During early labour especially, staying hydrated and having light carbohydrates (toast, crackers, fruit) maintains your energy. Many hospitals restrict eating once an epidural is in place or if a caesarean becomes likely, so follow your care team's specific advice on the day.

**What if I go into labour before my bag is packed?**
Send your birth partner to grab the phone charger, ID and insurance card, and your birth plan. Everything else can be brought later or borrowed from the hospital. The essentials are: something to wear, ID, and the car seat for getting home.

**Do I need to pack a going-home outfit for myself?**
Yes, and plan it in advance. Many women underestimate how they'll look and feel in the days immediately after birth. Your body will still look approximately 5–6 months pregnant. Pack something loose, comfortable, and easy to manage — a loose dress or maternity trousers with a soft top works well. For C-section recovery specifically, make sure nothing sits at your incision line.

**Should I bring my own snacks even if the hospital has a cafeteria?**
Yes. Labour is unpredictable in timing, and the cafeteria may not be open when you need it. Your birth partner will be with you continuously and also needs sustenance. Pack enough for both of you for at least 24 hours — this is one of the most universally praised additions to any hospital bag list.

## Key Takeaways

- Pack your hospital bag by week 36; by week 32–34 if carrying multiples or if complications are present
- For a planned or potential caesarean, add high-waisted loose trousers, a pillow for the car journey home, peppermint oil capsules for trapped wind, a mild laxative, and a one-handed nursing bra
- Nipple cream and nipple pads belong in the bag from the start — apply nipple cream before feeding begins, not after soreness sets in
- Maternity pads are almost always underpacked; bring at least 20 for a short stay
- Use separate labelled bags for labour, post-birth, and baby — it saves significant time when your birth partner is searching at 3am
- The car seat must be fitted and installed before labour begins; no hospital will allow you to leave without one for your baby
