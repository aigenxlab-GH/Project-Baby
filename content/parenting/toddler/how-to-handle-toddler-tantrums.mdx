## Why Toddlers Have Tantrums

Tantrums are not a sign of bad parenting or a badly-behaved child. They are a completely normal part of toddler development.

Between ages 1 and 3, toddlers are experiencing enormous emotional development. They have strong feelings — frustration, desire, anger — but the part of the brain that regulates those feelings (the prefrontal cortex) won't be fully developed until their mid-twenties.

In short: your toddler's emotions are very big, but their coping tools are very small.

Research from the University of Minnesota found that toddlers have tantrums an average of 1–3 times per day — so if your child is having daily meltdowns, they are completely within the normal range.

## Tantrums by Age: What to Expect

### 12–18 Months
Early tantrums are mostly frustration-based. Your baby-toddler wants to do things independently but can't yet — open a container, reach a toy, make themselves understood. The meltdown is almost always about capability, not defiance. Distraction and redirection work extremely well at this age.

### 18 Months – 2 Years
This is when the "terrible twos" begin — often before the second birthday. Tantrums become more intense as children develop stronger preferences and autonomy-seeking behaviour. "No" becomes their favourite word. They know what they want but still lack the language to negotiate. Power struggles are common.

### 2–3 Years
Peak tantrum territory. Your toddler now has enough language to be frustrated when it fails them, and enough self-awareness to feel thwarted. Tantrums can be explosive and prolonged. This is also when public tantrums feel most mortifying. The good news: this phase is also when emotional coaching has the most impact.

### 3–4 Years
Tantrums should start decreasing as language skills improve dramatically. Your child can now often tell you what they want. Tantrums that persist or intensify past age 4 warrant a conversation with your paediatrician.

## Common Tantrum Triggers

Understanding what sets off tantrums helps you anticipate and prevent many of them:

- **Hunger** — low blood sugar amplifies every frustration; never underestimate this
- **Tiredness** — an overtired toddler has even less emotional regulation
- **Transitions** — stopping play to come inside, leaving a playground, ending screen time
- **Wanting independence** — feeling unable to do something themselves
- **Not being understood** — language limitations create enormous frustration
- **Overstimulation** — busy environments, crowds, screens, too many activities
- **Boredom** — under-stimulated toddlers are more explosive
- **Big feelings they can't name** — excitement, disappointment, jealousy

## How to Respond in the Moment

### 1. Stay Calm Yourself
Your nervous system regulates your child's nervous system. Deep breaths, a soft voice, and a calm posture communicate safety even when your toddler is in full meltdown. If you escalate, they escalate. This is the hardest part — and the most important.

If you feel yourself getting angry, try: lower your voice rather than raising it. A quiet voice forces them to quieten to hear you.

### 2. Don't Give In to the Demand
If the tantrum started because you said no to sweets before dinner, giving in teaches your child that tantrums work. Stay consistent with your boundary. This is called "intermittent reinforcement" — and it's the fastest way to increase tantrum frequency.

### 3. Keep Them Safe
If they're throwing themselves on the floor or flailing, move them to a safe space or clear the area around them. Don't try to physically restrain them unless there is a genuine safety risk. Let the emotion move through them.

### 4. Name the Feeling
Even mid-tantrum, narrating the emotion helps: *"You're really angry that we have to leave the park. That feels so hard."* This is called emotional labelling, and research from UCLA shows it actually reduces the intensity of strong emotions in the brain. You're not agreeing with the behaviour — you're acknowledging the feeling underneath it.

### 5. Offer Comfort After
Once the storm has passed — don't lecture. A hug, a calm word, and moving on is enough. The tantrum itself was the consequence; there is no need for further punishment. Reconnection after a meltdown builds emotional security.

## What NOT to Do

Avoid these common responses that inadvertently worsen tantrums:

- **Matching their volume** — yelling at a yelling toddler never ends well
- **Lengthy explanations during the tantrum** — a toddler in full meltdown cannot process reasoning; save the talking for after
- **Shaming** — "You're embarrassing yourself" or "Big boys don't cry" teaches them to suppress feelings, not regulate them
- **Giving in to stop the noise** — especially hard in public, but it teaches that tantrums are effective
- **Time-outs for children under 2** — developmentally too young to connect the consequence to the behaviour
- **Threatening punishments you won't follow through on** — empty threats teach your child your words don't mean anything

## Strategies to Reduce Tantrum Frequency

**1. Consistent daily routine**
Predictability reduces anxiety. Consistent mealtimes, nap times, and bedtime help toddlers feel secure and regulated. Even small predictabilities (same order of getting dressed, same song at bath time) matter.

**2. Give advance warnings**
"We're leaving the park in 5 minutes" gives toddlers time to mentally prepare for transitions. A visual timer they can watch works brilliantly.

**3. Offer limited choices**
"Do you want to put on your shoes or your coat first?" gives toddlers a sense of control within your boundaries. Two choices only — more than that overwhelms them.

**4. Build language skills**
Read books about emotions. Use feeling words throughout the day. "You look frustrated." "I can see you're disappointed." The more vocabulary a toddler has for their inner world, the less they need to use their body.

**5. Protect sleep and meals**
A well-rested, well-fed toddler is a calmer toddler. Never skip naps or push mealtimes when you can help it. Schedule challenging activities (errands, appointments) for your toddler's best time of day.

**6. Say yes more often**
Many toddler battles start because they hear "no" dozens of times a day. Save your firm "no" for genuine safety issues and important limits. Redirect, distract, or find ways to say yes more often: "Yes, you can have a biscuit — after lunch."

**7. Special one-on-one time**
Even 15 minutes of fully focused, child-led play per day significantly reduces attention-seeking behaviour (which includes tantrums). Let them lead the play completely — no teaching, no correcting, just following their lead.

## Handling Public Tantrums

Public meltdowns feel catastrophic but are experienced by every parent. A few strategies:

- **Acknowledge the feeling, not the audience**: Focus on your child, not the people watching.
- **Leave if you can**: Removing the audience and the stimulation often shortens the tantrum significantly.
- **Have an exit plan**: Know where the quiet corner or exit is before you need it.
- **Don't bribe to stop it**: Every successful bribe increases the likelihood of a repeat.

Most bystanders are other parents who have been exactly where you are. The ones who look disapproving haven't had toddlers, or they've forgotten.

## What to Say: Scripts That Actually Help

- *"I can see you're really upset. I'm right here."*
- *"You wanted [X] and I said no. That made you so angry."*
- *"It's okay to feel angry. It's not okay to hit."*
- *"When you're ready, I'm here for a hug."*
- *"That was really hard. You worked through big feelings."* (after the tantrum)

## When to Seek Help

Most tantrums are completely normal. Consider speaking to your paediatrician if:

- Tantrums are lasting more than 25 minutes regularly
- Your child is injuring themselves or others consistently during tantrums
- Tantrums are increasing in frequency and intensity after age 4
- Your child seems unable to be comforted at all, even after the meltdown
- You notice tantrums accompanied by breath-holding, turning blue, or loss of consciousness (febrile convulsion — seek care immediately)
- You are struggling to cope and feel overwhelmed

You are not alone. Toddler behaviour is genuinely hard, and asking for support is a sign of great parenting, not failure.
